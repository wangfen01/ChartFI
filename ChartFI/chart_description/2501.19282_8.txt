This heatmap illustrates the pairwise unique code coverage across all programs, where each cell represents the number of code branches covered by the fuzzer of the column but not by the fuzzer of the row. The most striking pattern is that G2FUZZ(GPT-4) and G2FUZZ(GPT-3.5) are able to identify more unique code coverage than the remaining four AFL++ fuzzers. This is evident in the large values in the bottom-left quadrant, indicating that the G2FUZZ configurations find tens of thousands of unique branches missed by the AFL++ variants; for example, G2FUZZ(GPT-3.5) covers 44,784 branches that AFL++(mopt) does not. In stark contrast, the AFL++ configurations find a much smaller number of unique branches missed by G2FUZZ, as shown by the low values in the top-right quadrant, such as the 2,054 branches covered by AFL++(mopt) but not by G2FUZZ(GPT-3.5). While dominant overall, the two G2FUZZ variants are also quite similar to each other, with mutual coverage gaps of 9,400 and 12,841. Within the AFL++ family, differences are more incremental, particularly between the fast, rare, and mopt modes, which exhibit smaller mutual gaps. AFL++(cmplog) finds some distinct coverage compared to other AFL++ modes, but is still substantially outperformed by the G2FUZZ configurations. The significant asymmetry in coverage gaps, often by an order of magnitude, highlights a clear performance hierarchy where the G2FUZZ configurations substantially expand upon the code coverage achieved by the AFL++ variants.

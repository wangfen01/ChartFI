The chart illustrates the distribution of junipers by size across different altitudes, comparing results from photo-interpreted (PI) data samples, the model’s deployment output trained and configured with PI data, field-work (FW) data samples, and the model’s deployment output trained on PI data but configured with FW data. Across all four panels, shrub density is shown as a distribution over altitude for six size classes from XS to XXL, where N refers to the number of detected individuals and the dashed line to the median value. A consistent size–altitude gradient is evident across all datasets, where smaller shrubs (XS, S, and M) are more likely to grow at higher altitudes, while larger shrubs (XXL, XL, and L) tend to grow at lower altitudes. In the PI data, the distributions are relatively tight and strongly peaked, with XS and S clustering around the upper mid-elevations near roughly 2.6 to 2.7 km, while XL and XXL peak lower around roughly 2.3 to 2.4 km. The FW data preserves this same ordering but exhibits more pronounced multimodality and a broader spread, particularly for the larger classes, suggesting either stronger site heterogeneity in the field sample or that field plots capture mixed shrub communities across elevation bands more often than photo interpretation. According to the model outputs, junipers are concentrated between 2 km and 2.6 km in altitude, and while these outputs reproduce the main gradient and relative ordering of size classes observed in the PI and FW data, they smooth and broaden the distributions. This smoothing reduces sharp peaks and emphasizes a long right tail toward higher elevations, suggesting an altitudinal uplift in the optimal performance of the species. A notable shift is that the model tends to pull class medians toward a common mid-elevation band, compressing the separation among the mid and large classes and slightly underrepresenting the distinct high-elevation concentration seen for XS in the PI data. The strongest anomaly relative to empirical patterns is the model’s heavier low-elevation mass for larger classes and its overall smoothing, which likely reflects the model learning a generalized elevation response that dampens the local bimodality evident in the field-work distributions.

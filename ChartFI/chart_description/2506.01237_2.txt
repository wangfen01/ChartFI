The distribution of KoGEM is illustrated across five main categories and 16 subcategories in total, classified according to the Korean grammar taxonomy, where the number in parentheses next to each subcategory name represents the number of questions it contains. Syntax emerges as the most heavily weighted domain, driven primarily by Sentence Structure (290) and supported by Syntactic Features (148), indicating that structural sentence-level phenomena are the primary emphasis of the framework. Semantics forms the next major cluster with a balanced focus between word knowledge and meaning relations, as seen in the nearly identical volumes of Vocabulary (167) and Lexical Semantics (165), while Pragmatics (53) remains a smaller component of this domain. Morphology exhibits a mid-to-high volume but is unevenly distributed, with Word Formation (152) significantly outstripping Part-of-Speech (87) and Morpheme (29), suggesting a stronger coverage of derivational and compounding processes than of basic morphemic units or category labeling. Phonology shows a pronounced skew toward dynamic sound patterns, as Phonological Alternation (187) far exceeds the Phonological System (26), signaling that variation and rule-governed changes are prioritized over inventory-level description. Norms is the smallest main category overall and is fragmented across several low-count subareas; while Orthography (114) stands out as the main contributor, other subcategories including Standard Pronunciation (36), Standard Language (27), Romanization (18), Loanword Orthography (18), and Cross-Category (7) remain minor. This data distribution reveals a sharp concentration in a few key subtopics—especially Sentence Structure and Phonological Alternation—contrasted with a long tail of smaller normative categories, suggesting the KoGEM framework is weighted toward core grammatical structure and phonological variation rather than prescriptive standardization concerns.

The charts present a comprehensive comparison of edge-detection models in terms of running efficiency (inference speed on an NVIDIA RTX 2080 Ti), memory storage (the number of parameters), and detection performance (ODS F-measure). A distinct efficiency–accuracy tradeoff is visible, with the PiDiNet family delivering the fastest inference while maintaining competitive accuracy; specifically, the fastest version, PiDiNet-tiny-L, achieves comparable prediction performance with more than 200 FPS on GPU (215 FPS), further demonstrating the effectiveness of the underlying methods. While PiDiNet-small-L follows closely at 212 FPS, classic baselines run notably slower, with BDCN variants dropping to 47–91 FPS and RCF and HED hovering between 67 and 78 FPS. Regarding model size and accuracy, PiDiNet models demonstrate high parameter efficiency, achieving ODS scores roughly between 0.787 and 0.807 with parameter counts in the tens to hundreds of thousands, contrasting sharply with the multi-million parameter requirements of other architectures. Notably, with the inclusion of PDC, the standard PiDiNet model further boosts performance to an ODS of 0.807, being the same level as RCF, while still achieving nearly 100 FPS (recorded at 92 FPS) on the GPU; it should be noted that this version is slightly slower than the lighter variants as the RPDC is converted to 5 x 5 vanilla convolutions. While the absolute peak accuracy is reached by BDCN5 at 0.820 ODS, this requires 16.3M parameters, and the largest model, COB (28.8M parameters), only reaches 0.793 ODS, indicating diminishing returns from scale alone compared to the highly efficient PiDiNet architecture.

This sunburst chart illustrates the hierarchical structure of the perception and reasoning capabilities of MLLMs across three levels. The benchmark is organized around two primary (L-1) abilities, Perception and Reasoning, which are further refined into 11 L-2 and 16 L-3 capability dimensions, each annotated with its sample count. Overall, Perception tasks (approximately 28,600 samples) are more prevalent than Reasoning tasks (approximately 17,200 samples). Within Perception, the distribution is dominated by the `Object Properties` dimension, which includes two of the largest individual tasks in the entire dataset: `Object Classification` [9172] and `Object Color` [8930]. Another significant area is `Object Spatial Relationship` [4819]. A distinct pattern emerges in both `Scene Classification` and `Counting`, where tasks focused on localized areas (`Regional Land Use Classification` [1854] and `Regional Counting` [972]) significantly outnumber those requiring a global summary (`Overall Land Use Classification` [904] and `Overall Counting` [370]), indicating an emphasis on fine-grained, region-bounded analysis. The Reasoning half is overwhelmingly skewed towards `Visual Grounding`, with `Condition-based Visual Grounding` [6305] constituting the largest component of this ability. Classic `Planning` tasks like `Route Planning` [1130], `Complex Reasoning` such as `Environmental Condition Reasoning` [1125], and `Anomaly Reasoning` like `Anomaly Detection and Interpretation` [1131] are all comparatively scarce, with some tasks like `Regional Counting with Change Detection` [270] being the smallest in the entire benchmark. This distribution reveals a clear focus on foundational perception and localized, condition-aware visual grounding, while more abstract, multi-step deliberation or anomaly-focused inference represents a smaller portion of the evaluated capabilities.

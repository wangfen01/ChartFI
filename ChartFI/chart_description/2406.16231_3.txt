These three triangular heatmaps illustrate the taskwise accuracy of different CL approaches—ER, DER++, and DARE—while learning new tasks T1 through T6, where rows indicate performance after a later checkpoint and columns track the retention of specific earlier tasks. Across all methods, the dominant pattern is a strong diagonal of high initial performance that generally decays as the temporal distance grows, indicating progressive drift in representations as training advances. However, distinct differences in stability are evident; DARE retains the accuracy of old tasks better compared to ER and DER++, exhibiting the strongest resistance to forgetting. For instance, DARE achieves higher performance in task 1 compared to DER++, beginning with a 64.9% accuracy that outperforms the 53.9% of DER++ and maintaining 35.0% after T6, compared to 24.6% for DER++ and only 14.3% for ER. While ER exhibits the sharpest long-range decay with early-to-late comparisons falling quickly into the low teens, DARE retains more performance on seen domains compared to ER and DER++, showing elevated mid-range similarities and fewer extreme collapses. A notable deviation from the general trend of forgetting occurs around the T4 boundary; the data reveal that learning task 4 helps improve performance on task 1 across all CL algorithms, visible as a distinct rebound in the T1 column (e.g., DARE rises from 44.0% after T3 to 51.7% after T4). This positive backward transfer contrasts with the phase change around T3, where cross-checkpoint similarity drops markedly, reinforcing the interpretation that while task distribution shifts can accelerate forgetting, DARE better mitigates these effects while capitalizing on constructive task interference.

The three boxplot panels display the distribution of the metrics aggregated across five datasets ranging from MNIST to ImageNet, comparing mimicry, Sinvad, and DeepJanus on boundary distance, boundary coverage, and Laplacian variance. Considering boundary distance, mimicry consistently generates boundary inputs characterized by lower boundary distances for all datasets, showing wider dispersion and less consistent ability to push samples far from boundaries. Related to the baselines, DeepJanus’s inputs exhibit higher distance on the simpler and mid-complexity datasets, while Sinvad becomes comparatively stronger on the more complex natural-image datasets like CIFAR10 and ImageNet. Regarding label coverage, DeepJanus outperforms GenAI-based methods in label coverage for MNIST; however, this trend reverses for SVHN, where its coverage drops markedly. With more complex datasets, mimicry outperforms Sinvad in terms of label coverage, as Sinvad declines sharply on CIFAR10 and is lowest on ImageNet, indicating that the methods achieving larger boundary distances on complex datasets are not necessarily exploring boundary regions broadly. Regarding Laplacian variance, the data highlights that DeepJanus performs better when a model is available, consistently showing the highest variance across its applicable datasets, which implies it produces higher-frequency, more textured outputs. Conversely, as data complexity increases, Sinvad’s performance drops significantly, remaining the lowest and most stable, whereas mimicry shows moderate variance that rises strongly on ImageNet. Ultimately, the most notable shift occurs on CIFAR10 and ImageNet, where Sinvad’s boundary distance increases while its boundary coverage collapses, pointing to a mode of generating fewer but more extreme boundary-reaching cases, while mimicry’s comparatively higher coverage on harder datasets suggests broader exploration rather than maximizing extremity.

The chart illustrates a comparison of daily precipitation rates from the IPSL model, an observed series (obsp), and various bias correction methods over the course of a year. Across the timeframe, all series share a clear seasonal structure characterized by relatively lower precipitation early in the year, a pronounced rise into a midyear wet season, and a gradual decline toward the year's end. The most notable divergence occurs between roughly days 120 and 220, where precipitation levels and variability increase sharply; in this window, the raw model outputs, especially LS and IPSL, show frequent high-amplitude spikes that exceed the observed peaks, indicating a tendency to overstate extremes and day-to-day volatility. Additionally, the VS series exhibits pronounced oscillations and occasional negative excursions, which stand out as physically implausible artifacts. While bias-corrected methods generally reduce variance relative to the raw models, the data clearly highlights the superior alignment achieved by Deconfounding BC. This method distinguishes itself by tracking the observed seasonal rise and fall more closely than other adjustments like QDM or Temporal_BC, effectively mitigating the exaggerated variability seen in the unadjusted series and demonstrating a robust performance in aligning with observed trends throughout the peak precipitation months.

This visualization presents the distributions and ranks of four loss functions—SIMSE, L1, JTFS, and DTW—across four distinct scenarios (BP-Noise, Add-SineSaw, Noise-AM, and SineSaw-AM) based on three performance measures: MSS, P-Loss, and Likert ratings, where higher values indicate better performance. Color-coded violin plots visualize the best performers for each program, with green representing the top rank, followed by grey, dark grey, and black for lower ranks. In the BP-Noise scenario, the manual hearing test and MSS selected SIMSE as the best performer and L1 as the second-best performer, while P-Loss gave the reverse order by ranking L1 highest; despite this discrepancy in objective metrics, SIMSE maintains the strongest subjective profile with Likert ratings clustered near the top end. For the Add-SineSaw scenario, JTFS was the best performing loss function in all evaluation methods, showing MSS concentrated at the highest values and Likert ratings shifted significantly upward compared to SIMSE and L1, while all evaluation methods produced identical results in their ranking of the four models. A similar consensus is observed in the Noise-AM and SineSaw-AM scenarios, where all models selected DTW as the best performer; in these amplitude-modulation conditions, DTW dominates decisively with the highest MSS distributions and subjective ratings that sit far above the rest. In the final SineSaw-AM scenario, all methods of analysis gave identical rankings to all programs, confirming DTW as the most robust choice when temporal alignment is critical. Across all panels, Likert ratings generally track MSS more consistently than P-Loss, and the most notable shift is the change in the top-ranked method by scenario—from SIMSE in BP-Noise to JTFS in Add-SineSaw to DTW in both AM conditions—highlighting that performance is highly regime-dependent rather than driven by a single universally superior method.

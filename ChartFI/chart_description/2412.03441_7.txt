The six panels display density histograms comparing the activation distributions of clean Malware (blue) and Poisoned Malware (red) across different training strategies, with the x-axis representing the underlying activation scores. Across baselines such as FT, LP, Fe-Tuning, and FST, the targeted sample combined with the trigger deviates significantly compared to the original malware samples, often forming a clear secondary mode on the negative side. This indicates that the activations of poisoned samples remain shifted toward “benign” even after fine-tuning, creating a mixed population where a substantial portion of the poisoned set is displaced from the clean manifold. While FT-Init results in a distribution where the two groups overlap more tightly around the central peak, the poisoned curve still retains heavier tails and a leftward extension, demonstrating that other baselines based on reinitialization and shifting model parameters fail in deviating the activation distribution of backdoor neurons on triggered malware samples toward that of non-triggered malware samples. Conversely, the most notable shift occurs in the PBP method (Ours), where the two curves nearly collapse onto the same narrow, symmetric peak centered near zero. PBP is the only fine-tuning method that can help fix the activation distribution of the model and help it activate indiscriminately given the malware and a corresponding triggered malware sample, effectively neutralizing the poisoning-induced drift and variance inflation observed in other strategies.

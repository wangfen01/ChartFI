The perplexity distribution of the Llama 3.1-based model for German, Khmer, and Manchu illustrates a sharp downward shift in perplexity and a significant compression of variability once Language-Adaptive Pre-Training (LAPT) is applied. For German, the condition without LAPT shows a very wide spread with extreme outliers reaching well into the hundreds of thousands, whereas with LAPT the entire scale collapses to the low hundreds, indicating both a large reduction in typical perplexity and far fewer catastrophic high-perplexity cases. Khmer shows an even more dramatic stabilization, where the distribution spans up to approximately 80,000 without LAPT, but with LAPT most observations cluster near the bottom of the axis with only a small number of moderate outliers, suggesting LAPT largely eliminates the heavy tail and makes performance much more consistent. Manchu improves substantially but remains the most challenging; without LAPT it exhibits the largest dispersion and the most extreme outliers, while with LAPT the center of the distribution moves to the mid-hundreds and tightens, yet notable high outliers persist and the upper range remains materially higher than German and Khmer. Across all three languages, there are some outliers regardless of whether LAPT is performed or not, suggesting out-of-distribution (OOD) issues occur and implying that there might be out-of-distribution samples with limited training data. This residual difficulty, particularly evident in Manchu, indicates that while LAPT reduces performance gaps, it does not fully resolve the underlying domain mismatch or the impact of sparse data on model stability.

The radar charts compare the controllability error, defined as the average normalized L1 error, of three systems—MCTune, IFT, and ChatGPT—across a set of lexical and readability features, split by model family (AlphaGPT-4 and WizardLM) and increasing difficulty levels from σ = 0.1 to 0.3. Across nearly all panels, ChatGPT traces the outermost shape, indicating significantly higher error rates, while MCTune consistently outperforms the other baselines across all settings by maintaining the lowest error values. Surprisingly, a state-of-the-art model like ChatGPT underperforms in controllability compared to the instruction-finetuned LLaMA2-7B (IFT), which generally occupies the middle ground. The clearest separations between the methods appear on content and structure measures, such as counts of nouns, verbs, and unique word-related metrics, where ChatGPT’s disadvantage is most pronounced. However, for noun_var, verb_var, and adj_var, the differences between each baseline’s errors are not significant, as the lines remain comparatively close across methods and noise levels. As σ increases, the relative ordering remains largely stable, though gaps widen slightly for AlphaGPT-4; conversely, WizardLM shows tighter clustering among methods, suggesting less sensitivity to the tuning approach, although at σ = 0.3 there is a noticeable lift in error for readability-related behaviors, with FKRE and average reading time rising as the models struggle with greater difficulty.

The three heatmaps display IPv4, IPv6, and TCP reassembly similarities across tested implementations, where each cell reports the similarity between pairs of protocol implementations. The color intensity represents the percentage of test cases that behave the same after reassembly, with purple cells encoding perfect reassembly similarities. Overall, the data reveals 15 IPv4, 14 IPv6, and 14 TCP distinct behaviors for 18 to 23 protocol implementations. Across all panels, a clear family clustering pattern dominates: mainstream OS kernels and closely related versions tend to show very high similarity to each other, while lightweight embedded stacks and IDS/monitoring tools form distinct groups with noticeably lower similarity to general-purpose OS implementations. In IPv4, the strongest similarities concentrate among the BSD and Linux-derived implementations and across nearby version transitions, while several embedded stacks diverge from the main OS cluster to produce a patchwork of midrange similarities. However, as the purple and dark squares reveal, IPv6 and TCP tend to be reassembled more similarly across implementations than IPv4. In IPv6, similarity becomes more uniformly high among the operating systems, with fewer pronounced cold regions inside the OS block, suggesting more standardized behavior across major kernels. In TCP, OS implementations still cluster with high mutual similarity, but IDS/monitoring tools and specialized implementations show broader low-similarity bands against the OS cluster, indicating that TCP stream reassembly introduces more opportunities for policy differences. Across all protocols, tools like Snort, Suricata, and Zeek sit apart from the OS cluster as distinct outliers, showing moderate-to-low similarity against most operating systems and reinforcing that their reassembly logic is intentionally different from end-host stacks.

This figure evaluates the performance of three methods—IID (grey), covariate shift adjusted via doubly robust estimator (green), and entropy balancing (purple)—across the Pipeline (P) and ManyLabs 1 (M) datasets. The left panels quantify the under-coverage of 95% prediction intervals averaged over all pairs of sites within each hypothesis, relative to the nominal level marked by the red dashed line. In the Pipeline coverage panel (P, a), the IID approach is generally the strongest and most stable; the doubly robust estimator shows pronounced failures at hypothesis indices 2 and 6, indicating severe miscalibration. The pattern shifts in the ManyLabs 1 data (M, a), where entropy balancing achieves consistently high coverage, often outperforming IID. The right panels display the value of estimates, where segments connect estimates for the same pairs of sites to visualize shifts from the source analysis to reweighted adjustments. A critical analysis of these panels reveals that estimates based on existing approaches (via doubly robust estimator and entropy balancing) do not bring the source estimates closer to the target estimate (red dashed line). For instance, in the Pipeline estimate-shift panel (P, b), entropy balancing introduces wide dispersion and extreme values rather than convergence toward the target. Similarly, in ManyLabs 1 (M, b), while covariate shift methods induce shifts, they do not systematically correct the bias, suggesting that these reweighting techniques may introduce instability or fail to improve accuracy relative to the unadjusted source estimates.

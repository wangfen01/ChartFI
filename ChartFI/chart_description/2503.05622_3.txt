The charts compare model performance across three panels by plotting Test BPR on the x-axis against Test Log Likelihood on the y-axis, illustrating the tradeoff between ranking quality and probabilistic fit. In all three tasks, the proposed decision-aware ML (DAML) delivers better top-K decisions as measured by BPR than ML estimation. In the Massachusetts fatal overdoses (2020–2021) panel, performance clusters tightly around BPR values near 0.59–0.62; while direct optimization of BPR can improve BPR, gains on test data over ML are minimal (less than +0.01). In contrast, surrogate loss methods (SPO+, PG) yield much worse likelihood despite similar BPR, indicating that these solutions provide lower quality decisions than the DAML and direct BPR approach. The Cook County fatal overdoses (2021–2022) data reveal a wider divergence, where direct optimization of BPR improves BPR by +0.04 over ML, achieving the highest ranking score but at the cost of a much worse likelihood. Here, DAML and NLL-only retain the highest log likelihoods, highlighting that direct BPR optimization sacrifices fit for ranking. Finally, in the ANWR TX cranes (2009–2010) dataset, the DAML objective surprisingly offers better BPR than the BPR-only objective, yet its likelihood fit degrades sharply compared to NLL-only, which achieves the best log likelihood. This panel explicitly demonstrates that optimizing NLL or BPR alone will yield poor results in the other metric, as evidenced by the separation between the high-likelihood NLL-only model and the high-BPR DAML variants.

The three panels illustrate the accuracy improvement after applying LWS under different client numbers, local epochs, and partial participation ratios, consistently revealing that the proposed method brings performance improvement across all tested federated learning settings. When tuning the number of clients across values of 10, 30, and 50, most methods experience slightly higher gains as the count increases from 10 to 30, with FedDisco+LWS, L-DAWA+LWS and FedAvg+LWS peaking at the 30-client mark, while FedDyn+LWS drops at 30 before rebounding at 50. At 50 clients, these improvements generally flatten or dip relative to the 30-client setting, suggesting diminishing returns for LWS as the federation expands, though FedAvg and FedDisco remain comparatively strong. The effects of local epoch variations—tested at 1, 5, and 10 epochs—show a clear divergence where all methods cluster around modest improvements at 1 epoch, but FedProx+LWS and FedDisco+LWS jump sharply to the highest gains at 10 epochs, indicating that LWS becomes especially valuable as clients train longer locally and drift risk increases. In contrast, FedDyn+LWS and L-DAWA+LWS show more moderate gains at 10 epochs, implying their baselines may already mitigate drift more effectively. The partial participation ratio, tuned at 0.1, 0.3, and 0.5, produces the largest swings in performance; at a low ratio of 0.1, gains are high for most methods, but at 0.3, FedDyn+LWS spikes dramatically to the top of the chart while L-DAWA+LWS collapses to a minimal gain. As the participation ratio reaches 0.5, improvements generally fall across all methods, which aligns with the observation that LWS provides the most significant benefits when participation is sparse and the system is more heterogeneous or unstable.

This chart illustrates the relationship between cost per problem in dollars on a logarithmic x-axis and the F1 Score percentage on the y-axis across different methods and models. The visualization reveals a clear cost-performance frontier where higher expenditure generally correlates with increased accuracy, though the choice of inference strategy significantly influences a model's position on this curve. Greedy decoding serves as the low-cost baseline, exhibiting wide performance dispersion from the very low F1 of Llama 3.1 8B Instruct to the mid-50s for GPT-4o mini and Deepseek-R1-Qwen-7B. While majority voting and multi-model debate typically shift performance upward at a moderate cost increase, the most significant gains are concentrated among stronger variants, whereas weaker models like Llama 3.1 8B remain comparatively low even after aggregation. Notably, the Temporal Consistency method most consistently pushes performance to the top of each model’s cluster; specifically, this method enables distilled 7B/8B models to achieve 71.3% for Deepseek-R1-Qwen-7B and 67.2% for Deepseek-R1-Llama-8B, surpassing all existing 70B/72B models and GPT-4o. While Temporal Consistency allows these smaller models to reach peak F1 values within a mid-range cost bracket, GPT-4o achieves the highest overall F1 score but at a sharply higher cost than any other data point, signaling that marginal accuracy gains at the top end require a disproportionate price premium. Conversely, GPT-4o mini remains a highly cost-efficient value option, approaching a 60% F1 score without entering the high-cost regime. Ultimately, the data suggests that while advanced inference methods can meaningfully improve accuracy for capable models, the cost of top-tier performance accelerates rapidly, and weaker models do not reliably benefit enough to justify the investment in more complex strategies.

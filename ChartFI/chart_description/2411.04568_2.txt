The figure compares confusion matrices on the FACED-9, SEED, and SEED-V classification tasks without the DyA module and with the DyA module, illustrating how the inclusion of DyA shifts probability mass toward the diagonal and reduces cross-class leakage. For the FACED-9 dataset, the DyA module led to the greatest improvements in recognizing the positive emotion categories of amusement, inspiration, and joy, with accuracy increases of 15%, 17%, and 6%, respectively; specifically, Inspiration rose from 0.37 to 0.54 and Amusement from 0.50 to 0.65. The classification accuracies of negative emotions, such as anger, disgust, and fear were also enhanced by 9%, 7%, and 10%, respectively, alongside solid lifts for Tenderness from 0.63 to 0.69. Despite these gains, errors concentrate in persistent confusions, notably between Inspiration and Anger, indicating DyA helps overall separability but does not fully resolve specific semantic overlaps. On the SEED dataset, performance is already strong without DyA, yet the DyA module led to improvements in recognizing positive and neutral emotions, with accuracy increases of 3% and 1%, respectively, pushing Positive recognition to 0.98. In the SEED-V dataset, the DyA module enhanced the model’s ability to recognize nuanced negative emotions, with notable accuracy gains of 6% for both disgust and sadness and 5% for neutral. While Happy slightly dips from 0.76 to 0.74, there was a reduction in the misclassification rates of these negative emotions as positive (i.e., happy), contributing to a more refined emotional recognition overall.

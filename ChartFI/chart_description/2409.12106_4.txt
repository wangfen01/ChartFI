These three heatmaps visualize the correlations between values when using different measurement tools—Self-report, ValueBench, and GPV—to evaluate how well each captures the theoretical circumplex structure where values that are adjacent exhibit positive correlations and those that are theoretically distant show negative correlations. The Self-report and ValueBench panels are characterized by a predominance of positive correlations, suggesting a lack of differentiation between conflicting values; for instance, Self-report displays a theoretically contradictory strong positive alignment between Security and Hedonism (0.86) and between Security and Self-Direction (0.75). ValueBench preserves this clustering but appears more smoothed, maintaining strong positive coupling among Security, Conformity, and Tradition while failing to capture significant trade-offs. In sharp contrast, the heatmap for GPV reveals the superior construct validity of this instrument, as it is the only measurement to clearly demonstrate the expected oppositions between competing value domains. Specifically, GPV shows distinct negative associations between Power and Universalism (-0.64), between Tradition and Universalism (-0.64), and between Conformity and Self-Direction (-0.64), relationships that are obscured or absent in the other datasets. Furthermore, GPV corrects the counterintuitive patterns found in the Self-report data, such as reversing the relationship between Security and Self-Direction to a negative correlation (-0.39), which accurately reflects the theoretical tension between conservation and openness to change. Ultimately, while Self-report and ValueBench present broad, highly intercorrelated value neighborhoods, GPV successfully reorganizes these relationships to reflect the distinct positive and negative covariances predicted by value theory.

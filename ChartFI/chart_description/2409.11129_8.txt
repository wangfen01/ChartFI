The figure compares relative speedups across eight graph datasets for a range of partitioning and ordering methods, shown separately for CPU and GPU runs under DGL and PyG. On DGL CPU, speedups vary substantially by dataset and method, with the most pronounced uplift appearing on the products graph where several methods peak noticeably higher than elsewhere. However, on the remaining graphs, some strategies—mainly slashburn, minla, hubcluster, hubsort, degsort, rcm, and dfs—lead to slowdowns (speedups smaller than 1x) for CPU-based training. A notable exception is observed for the graph dimacs9-USA, where there is not much difference in terms of speedup for the different graph reordering strategies when comparing training on the CPU versus training on the GPU. In contrast, on the DGL GPU, the strategies that struggled on the CPU perform much better and lead nearly in all cases to speedups, resulting in more uniform behavior where most methods deliver similar, moderate gains. Similar to DGL, the PyG results demonstrate that graph reordering is more effective for GPU-based training than for CPU-based training. While the PyG CPU panel shows speedups hovering in a relatively narrow interval with some methods causing slowdowns, for PyG GPU-based training, all graph reordering strategies lead to speedups. This performance shift is exemplified by the dfs method, which leads to a speedup of 1.67x, 1.38x, 1.47x, 1.61x, 1.14x, 1.16x, 1.02x, and 1.44x on the graphs dimacs9-USA, livejournal, orkut, products, reddit, soc-pokec, web-BerkStan, and wikipedia, respectively. The consistent improvement from CPU to GPU across both frameworks suggests that as execution becomes more throughput-oriented, the performance differentiation among methods diminishes, and strategies that are detrimental on the CPU become beneficial on the GPU.

These radar charts illustrate a multi-dimensional benchmark analysis based on domain characteristic measures across three levels—genre, style, and topic shifts—using eight variables: document and summary length, density, compression, coverage, abstractiveness, and document and summary diversity. In the genre shift analysis, distinct profiles emerge regarding how extractive versus abstractive the sources are; specifically, CNN/DM exhibits the highest density and coverage, indicating summaries that reuse many source spans and touch a large portion of the input. Conversely, PubMed produces the longest summaries and achieves the highest abstractiveness, implying a shift toward paraphrasing and longer outputs, while Reddit demonstrates the highest compression ratio. SAMSum demonstrates the greatest diversity in summaries, highlighting significant variance in lexical choice compared to other genres. Under style shifts, editorial preferences drive significant divergence even when document lengths are comparable. The Washington Post (WaPo) demonstrates the highest document length, compression, and abstractiveness, pointing to a style that heavily condenses long source texts into abstractive summaries. In contrast, NYDaily ranks highest in coverage and density, consistent with summaries that closely track original phrasing, while Fox showcases the greatest summary diversity. Distinct summarization behaviors are evident elsewhere, with the Crime category demonstrating the highest density and the Law category showing the highest coverage, reflecting a tendency to retain specific source details. Meanwhile, Soccer exhibits the highest abstractiveness, and both the Media and Soccer categories display higher summary diversity, suggesting that these topics involve broader subtopic coverage or less standardized writing conventions than more rigid categories.

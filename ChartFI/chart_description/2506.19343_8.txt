The similarity distribution of heterophilic node pairs across the Roman, Squirrel, and Actor datasets reveals a consistent progression from the low or diffuse similarity of the Raw Feature baseline toward increasingly concentrated embeddings in learned methods. In the Roman dataset, the Raw Feature density sits near low similarity, while GREET and DGMAE shift the mass rightward into mid-to-high similarity; however, GraphMAE enforces similar representations between nodes and their neighbors through feature reconstruction, resulting in higher similarity between pairs of nodes in a heterophilic graph and forming a tight peak near the upper end. In the Squirrel dataset, the Raw Feature again concentrates near very low similarity, while GREET spreads broadly across the mid-range with a long tail; compared with GraphMAE, it can be observed that the distribution of pairwise similarity learned by DGMAE shifts to the left, clustering in the upper-mid region. The Actor dataset shows the most striking contrast, where the Raw Feature collapses into a very sharp spike around zero similarity, implying near-orthogonal representations, while GREET and DGMAE move the distribution into positive similarity. Across all three panels, DGMAE is more clearly distinguishable between these heterophilic node pairs compared with GREET, indicating that it can effectively suppress the similarity of node pairs to prevent the features of different classes of nodes from converging. Ultimately, while GraphMAE consistently produces the highest and most concentrated cosine similarities near 1.0, DGMAE maintains a distinct separation from this near-perfect similarity, signaling a method-driven approach to balancing representation consistency and class discriminability in heterophilic environments.

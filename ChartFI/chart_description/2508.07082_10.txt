The chart presents the average annual contributions of the various factors that affect the dynamics of housing credit from 2004 to 2019, with the overall movement represented by the solid blue line. Housing credit exhibited volatility before the global financial crisis, dipping in 2005, rebounding in 2006, and rising into 2008, before a clear structural shift emerged. From 2009 onward, the post-GFC dummy becomes a large, persistent positive contributor; the post-GFC dummy reflects post-2008 factors, such as unconventional monetary policies, that sustained the demand for housing credit even when other components turned restrictive. Alongside this structural shift, macroeconomic variables and bank characteristics contributed to the housing credit increase after 2009, while excess tier 1 capital increased housing credit by providing intermittent support throughout the observed period. In stark contrast, while housing macroprudential policy contributions were small before 2010, housing MaP measures made a substantial downward contribution to the development of housing credit after 2011. This negative pressure deepened notably around 2015 to 2017, aligning with a mid-decade slowdown in overall credit growth and culminating in the weakest overall growth of the post-2010 period during the 2016 to 2017 downturn, where the intensified macroprudential drag was not fully offset by the still-positive post-GFC effect. Regarding monetary influences, domestic monetary policy contributions remained generally modest and mixed across the timeline. Conversely, foreign monetary policy had some impact on local housing credit in the years before the GFC, showing episodic negative influence around the crisis period, and did not contribute to credit dynamics in the years after the GFC. By 2019, housing credit recovered sharply to a period high, a rebound that coincides with stronger positive residuals and supportive non-policy components, indicating that factors outside the modeled policy channels played a larger role in the late-cycle upswing even as housing macroprudential policy remained a persistent headwind.

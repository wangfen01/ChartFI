This heatmap presents a comparative analysis of one-shot segmentation results across 16 anatomical targets in terms of DSC (%) performance, evaluating six methods: MG, UniMiSS, SUP, HySparK, VoCo, and Hi-End-MAE. The targets, abbreviated as Lun (Lung), Liv (Liver), Kid (Kidney), Vein (Veins), Aor (Aorta), Int (Intestine), Spl (Spleen), IVC (Inferior Vena Cava), Sto (Stomach), Duo (Duodenum), Pan (Pancreas), Pro/ute (Prostate/Uterus), ADR (Adrenal Gland), Rec (Rectum), Gall (Gallbladder), and Eso (Esophagus), reveal a consistent gradient of difficulty where performance generally declines from the relatively easier leftmost organs to the more challenging mid-to-right structures. Hi-End-MAE achieves optimal results for most organs and drives the highest overall average at 62.42, demonstrating that it encourages the encoder to learn strong and rich localized anatomical representations. Compared to the recent SOTA method VoCo, which forms a clear second tier with an average of 54.85, Hi-End-MAE shows significant improvements across organs of different scales, including enhancements of 28.91% for the duodenum, 10.48% for the pancreas, 10.26% for the lung, and 11.40% for the inferior vena cava. While mid-pack methods like HySparK (38.88) and SUP (34.22) perform well on early organs but lose substantial ground on smaller or lower-contrast structures like the adrenal gland and rectum, MG and UniMiSS exhibit the steepest degradation. These baseline methods show conspicuous anomalies, including multiple zero or near-zero entries for the duodenum, rectum, and prostate/uterus, signaling failure cases or severe instability on specific targets. Ultimately, the data highlights that Hi-End-MAE is uniquely robust, maintaining high performance across vascular structures and complex regions where other models fail, thereby setting a new benchmark for anatomical segmentation.

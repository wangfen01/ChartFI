This heatmap illustrates the comparative performance of multiple language models across a diverse set of task categories, where each cell corresponds to the model’s accuracy on that specific task. The rightmost column of the figure indicates the models’ average accuracies, demonstrating that all models outperform their random guesses, which sit at a baseline average of 27.3%. Despite this general competency, even the top-performing model, GPT-4, only reaches an accuracy of 60.5%, while the remaining models hover around 40.0%, with Mixtral-8×7B leading the second tier at 46.7% and others like Llama2-Chat-70B and GPT-3.5-Turbo clustering near 41–42%. In terms of specific category performance, all models achieve over 70.0% accuracy on the single node (SN) and single edge (SE) tasks, with GPT-4 even reaching 100.0% on the single node, indicating strong foundational capabilities across the board. However, beyond these initial categories, the most consistent pattern is a steep drop into a long middle band of tasks where scores frequently fall into the 20–50% range, suggesting these areas are systematically harder or less aligned with general instruction tuning. A notable anomaly within this difficult stretch is the CT column, where several models spike sharply—such as Llama2-Chat-70B reaching 73.3%—relative to their surrounding categories, suggesting that CT may be more learnable or benchmark-friendly. Conversely, the MRS category represents a universal weak spot where nearly all models converge around 19–29%, dangerously close to the Random Guess baseline, indicating a category that remains largely unsolved. Ultimately, while GPT-4 leads most categories, its dominance is defined by fewer collapses on these difficult mid-benchmark categories, whereas other models show high volatility and frequent regressions toward baseline on the hardest tasks.

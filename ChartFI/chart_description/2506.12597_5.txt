The expert-to-expert overlap matrices compare the overlap ratios of upcycled LLMs posttrained without an orthogonal penalty and with an orthogonal penalty, where darker cells indicate higher overlap and the diagonal is fixed at 1.00 for self-overlap. Without the orthogonal penalty, experts exhibit higher overlap ratios that are consistently moderate, with many pairs clustering around the mid-0.20s to low-0.30s, indicating substantial redundancy and shared behavior across experts. In contrast, with the orthogonal penalty, off-diagonal overlaps drop sharply across the board, concentrating mostly around 0.07 to 0.17, which indicates that experts generally have low overlaps and share a small amount of parameters. This shift demonstrates that the orthogonality term effectively pushes experts to diversify, reducing pairwise similarity and encouraging more distinct expert roles. While specialization is broadly distributed, domain-similar experts exhibit marginally higher overlaps; for instance, maths- and code-domain experts {2, 6, 7} and general- and safety-domain experts {3, 4} maintain slightly elevated values compared to the global average. Overall, the dominant trend is a global compression of cross-expert overlap from moderate to low levels, effectively mitigating redundancy while allowing for residual shared features among experts with related functional domains.

The chart displays the monthly mean absolute error (MAE) in gigawatts for electricity forecasting models in France and Germany from January 2019 through early 2023, highlighting how forecast accuracy evolved through the COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent energy market disruptions. In France, MAE initially fluctuates between 3 and 4 GW, but a significant divergence occurs in early 2020; the high overall errors in the GAM compared to the States model originate from the springtime in 2020, where the baseline GAM spikes above 6 GW while the States model remains significantly lower. Although averaged errors in France are reduced when COVID-19 information is integrated, the States model maintains the lowest average errors during this volatile period. A similar pattern is evident in Germany, where baseline errors jump near 4 GW in Spring 2020, but the States model effectively reduces modeling errors for experiments during this time, resulting in the lowest average MAE and RMSE. Following the pandemic onset, errors in both countries trend downward to stabilize at their lowest levels around mid-2021, before a second regime shift in early 2022 drives MAE back up toward 4 to 5 GW in France and 4 GW in Germany. Unlike the 2020 shock where the States model clearly outperformed the baseline, the early 2022 surge affects all model variants similarly, indicating a broad structural change in the energy market that persists into early 2023 despite a modest easing of errors in late 2022.

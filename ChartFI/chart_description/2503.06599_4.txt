The charts present the net spillover index across eight different markets, illustrating the total effect (black) decomposed into the 1–4 horizon (red) and smaller, smoother contributions from the 4–12 (green) and 12+ (blue) horizons. The net spillovers of all variables show a larger jump at the end of 2012, initiating a period of extreme short-run volatility that is followed by a marked stabilization across the sample. Subsequently, CPU plays the role of a net recipient, exhibiting a sharp early positive spike that quickly flips into a deep negative trough by 2014–2015, then gradually mean-reverts toward a mild, persistent negative level through 2023. Conversely, while energy markets are net contributors, their specific trajectories differ; Fossil Energy starts with large positive swings that fade into near-zero contributions by 2016–2019, then shows a clear regime shift around 2020 where the total turns persistently positive and remains elevated into 2023, whereas Clean Energy experiences a striking early anomaly, plunging to very large negatives before snapping back to hover slightly above zero as a net contributor thereafter. In the food-related series, Meat trends structurally downward, becoming steadily negative from roughly 2015 onward and staying depressed through 2023, with longer-horizon components contributing to the persistence of the decline. Cereals display a classic rebound pattern where a severe early negative shock is followed by a sustained positive phase from about 2015 to 2019, then a gradual fade back toward near-zero by 2022–2023. Oils transition from early negatives to a prolonged upswing, turning clearly positive after 2017 and peaking around 2022 before easing but remaining above zero. Notably, dairy and sugar markets shift in their spillover dynamics over the sample period; Dairy shows a dramatic one-off crash followed by a long period near zero before drifting modestly negative after about 2020, and Sugar is characterized by an early surge reaching elevated positive levels around 2014, followed by a decline in 2015 before a secondary rebound in 2016, after which the net spillover gradually decays toward near-zero by 2018–2019, before a noticeable step down around 2020 into slightly negative territory, indicating a shift from supportive to mildly adverse conditions.

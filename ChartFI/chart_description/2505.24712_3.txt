The heatmap displays the proportion of bias types annotated within each academic area, with values normalized by row to highlight the relative focus within each area, where each row represents a school area and each column corresponds to a type of bias identified by the teacher during the activity. The number of annotations per academic area (rows) and per bias type (columns) is indicated by N, and the values indicate the proportion of annotations per category within each area. Gender and other biases emerge as the most consistently prominent categories overall, while disability and sexual orientation are generally the least cited across the board. Gender bias is particularly elevated in quantitatively oriented fields, peaking in Physics & Chemistry at 25.3% and Mathematics at 24.8%, whereas it is comparatively lower in areas like Technology and Sociology. The other category shows the strongest cross-discipline volatility, reaching its highest levels in Sociology at 36.4% and Psychology at 32.4%, while also remaining notably high in Biology & Natural Sciences and Mathematics, suggesting a broader mix of uncaptured bias experiences or differences in how respondents interpret the provided categories in those fields. Age and socioeconomic status form a second tier of frequently reported bias, with socioeconomic status particularly pronounced in Law at 18.3%, Philosophy & Ethics at 17.6%, and Physical Education & Sports at 17.2%, while age bias is relatively higher in Law at 18.8% and Health at 16.5%. Nationality bias is moderate but more visible in Programming & Computer Science, Psychology, and Sociology, all exceeding 16%, while ethnicity bias is generally modest yet comparatively higher in Communication and Language & Literature. Profession and physical appearance biases are usually mid-to-low, though physical appearance bias rises significantly in Physical Education & Sports at 13.5% and Technology at 13.4%, and profession bias peaks in Physics & Chemistry at 14.5%. Ultimately, the most significant shifts are the sharp spikes in other biases within the social sciences and the heightened gender bias in math-intensive fields, indicating that the dominant bias narratives differ meaningfully by discipline rather than following a uniform pattern.

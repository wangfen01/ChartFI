This chart evaluates Large Language Models (LLMs) acting as Independent System 1 and System 2 in Overcooked, mapping the inter-quartile mean Score of 20 games on the vertical axis against Score Efficiency—defined as the average score gained per macro action—on the horizontal axis, with the size of each model’s circle representing latency (the time taken from the request to the output of a macro action). The distribution reveals a distinct tradeoff between speed and performance: as independent System 1, models with fewer than 20B parameters excel in latency but often have near-zero score efficiency, indicating fast responses but ineffective actions. Conversely, when reasoning models use long Chain-of-Thought (CoT) as System 2, despite their stronger reasoning capabilities, their performance presents even lower score efficiency and overall scores compared to many smaller models functioning as System 1. This underperformance is largely driven by temporal constraints; since missed orders lead to score deductions, some high-score-efficiency models with high latency still score below zero. Consequently, the models that can balance capability in generating scoring actions with low latency perform better, such as GPT-4o and Qwen2.5-14b, which separate from the dense pack to achieve positive scores. Ultimately, however, all agents perform worse than the FSM agent, which stands as the clear frontier point in the upper-right corner with a score efficiency of 5.59 and negligible latency, highlighting a significant gap between current LLM capabilities and optimized rule-based execution.

The visualization illustrates the mean prediction average treatment effect (ATE) and predictive error (1-AUC) across 5-fold cross-validation iterations for various methods applied to the Law School Admissions and Adult Census Income datasets. In the Law School Admissions data, most approaches cluster near an ATE of zero but suffer from relatively high predictive error between 0.38 and 0.50, while the Unfair baseline achieves the lowest error near 0.33 at the cost of the largest ATE of approximately 0.13. In contrast, FairPFN achieves a prediction ATE below 0.01 on both datasets and maintains accuracy comparable to Unfair, effectively matching the performance of baselines that have access to inferred causal information while only utilizing observational data. This balance is particularly evident in the Adult Census Income dataset, where FairPFN and counterfactual averaging sit close to an ATE of zero with comparatively low error around 0.17 to 0.20, standing out against methods like EGR and CLAIRE which occupy a middle band with errors between 0.25 and 0.30 and higher ATE values. Furthermore, FairPFN exhibits lower variability in prediction ATE across folds compared to EGR, indicating stable causal effect removal. While a Pareto-like frontier generally suggests that pushing ATE toward zero increases predictive error—as seen in the Law School dataset with the Unaware method's jump to 0.35 error or the near-random performance of Constant and Random baselines—FairPFN serves as a notable exception by maintaining competitive error rates while sharply reducing the estimated causal effect to near-zero levels across both domains.

The chart illustrates the shifting composition of model families between 2020-E and 2023-E, comparing the top performance quartile against the bottom quartile to highlight how architectural preferences have evolved. The most significant structural shift is the decisive move away from older encoder and recurrent architectures toward large generative transformer families, a trend that is far more pronounced in the high-performing Q4+ group than in Q1−. GPT shows the clearest rise, with its share in Q4+ increasing from 21.06% to 33.7%, while Q1− grows much more modestly from 5.07% to 5.7%, indicating that higher-performing groups are increasingly dominated by GPT-style models. Other modern families such as T5 and LLaMA also expand primarily in Q4+, with T5 rising from 7.16% to 12.4% and LLaMA jumping from 1.71% to 8.2%, while smaller but generally positive additions like CLIP, BLOOM, OPT, and PaLM suggest a broad diversification around the large language model ecosystem. In stark contrast, the BERT component in Q1− remains unaffected, where it still firmly takes up more than 40% of N and even saw a ~3% increase from 40.37% to 43.29%. Furthermore, Q1− sees a much more modest change overall, or even increased interest for models pre-2020 like BART and COMET, while legacy architectures like word2vec, GloVe, and CNNs trend downward across both groups. This widening gap suggests that top-tier performance is increasingly driven by a wholesale transition to newer generative families, whereas the bottom quartile remains anchored to older architectures or adopts newer ones at a significantly slower pace.

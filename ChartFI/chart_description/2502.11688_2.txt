This visualization illustrates the distribution of four languages—Bangime, Basque, Kusunda, and Mapudungun—across various language-family categories based on three distinct feature sets: Lexibank, Grambank, and a Combined model, with results limited to the first three families to which an isolate is affiliated and the remaining proportions grouped under the label Rest. In the Lexibank model, Bangime is classified consistently as Dogon at 95%, while the Grambank model shifts significantly to affiliate it as Mande at 66% or Atlantic-Congo at 29%, leading the combined model to primarily leave Bangime unclassified at 86%. Basque remains mostly unclassified in both the Lexibank (46%) and Grambank (47%) models, although the latter also tends to propose an affiliation with Sino-Tibetan at 39%; consequently, the combined model proposes an affiliation with Indo-European at 23% or Sino-Tibetan at 18% while maintaining a 32% unclassified affiliation. Kusunda displays a split profile in Lexibank, affiliated with Nuclear Trans-New Guinea at 51%, Austroasiatic at 19%, or left unclassified at 17%, but the Grambank model mostly affiliates it with the Sino-Tibetan family at 60%, resulting in a combined model that mostly proposes no affiliation with other language families at 79%. Mapudungun is split between several families in the Lexibank model, including Timor-Alor-Pantar at 29%, Austronesian at 18%, and Unclassified at 11%, whereas the Grambank model mostly suggests a Salishan affiliation at 68% or no affiliation at 17%, which ultimately causes the combined model to find no clear affiliation pattern at 88%. Across these languages, the sharp reassignments driven by Grambank toward Sino-Tibetan for Basque and Kusunda and toward Salishan for Mapudungun contrast with the combined view’s broad reversion to unclassified status, indicating that lexical and grammatical evidence often pull in different directions and their combination tends to dampen confident family attribution.

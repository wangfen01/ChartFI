The chart displays the distribution of variable importance from RID for three important variables on the COMPAS dataset, with and without correcting for predictive equivalence. Across the three histograms, the PE-corrected RID generally tracks the original RID, suggesting the correction adjusts magnitudes modestly rather than fundamentally reshaping feature reliance, though distinct distributional shifts are evident. For the age variable, importance is primarily concentrated in the low-to-mid range of roughly 0.03 to 0.05, but a notable deviation occurs at the lower bound; specifically, when adjusting for predictive equivalence (shown in orange), more probability mass is given to zero importance for age. This trend of dampening weak contributions is even more pronounced for the number of juvenile crimes, where importance is overwhelmingly concentrated near zero with only a thin tail extending to about 0.05. Consistent with the pattern observed in age, more probability mass is given to zero importance for the number of juvenile crimes under the PE correction, reinforcing that this variable is largely unused by the model except in a small subset of cases. In contrast, the priors_count variable exhibits the highest and most stable reliance, forming a tight unimodal distribution centered roughly between 0.13 and 0.14. Unlike the other variables where the correction shifts density toward zero, for the number of priors, high importance values receive more probability mass, implying a consolidation of importance around the peak rather than a reduction. Ultimately, the data reveals a strong separation in typical importance levels, where priors_count dominates, age is moderate, and juvenile_crimes is mostly negligible.

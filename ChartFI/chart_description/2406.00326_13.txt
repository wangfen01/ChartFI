This visualization illustrates hourly electricity price forecasts and their components for selected models and periods, comparing two distinct weeks from October 21–28, 2019, and April 1–8, 2024, across varying forecast horizons. The price exhibits a strong daily cycle with daytime peaks and overnight troughs, where the sum of the components at each time point equals the forecast, revealing a fundamental tug-of-war between load as the dominant positive driver and renewable energy sources (RES) as the primary negative driver. While fuel and CO₂ components provide steadier upward pressure, the 2024 period is markedly higher and more volatile than 2019, with peak price levels roughly doubling and the amplitude of both positive load contributions and negative RES swings increasing significantly. In the short term we are able to capture negative price shocks due to strong RES infeed, particularly evident in the h=1 models where the forecast tracks the sharp downward spikes seen during the 2019 weekend and the frequent midweek troughs in 2024. However, as the horizon extends to h=360, the forecast becomes smoother because in the longer term we predict average seasonal patterns of the power price, leading the model to underreact to sharp intraday reversals and sudden dips. Consequently, while the models effectively decompose price formation into structural drivers like gas, coal, and seasonal intercepts, their accuracy is highest during regular diurnal cycles and deteriorates during rapid regime shifts where the true series deviates from the predicted average seasonal patterns.

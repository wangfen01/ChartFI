The chart illustrates the relative changes of normalized median (||w_in||_2 * b_in), a proxy for monosemanticity, across relative layer depth for four models after DPO training. Consistent with the observation that DPO training indeed improves monosemanticity in earlier model layers, the data shows that gains are generally modest and positive in the initial stages across the different GPT-2 models and GPT-neo. Specifically, GPT-neo (2.7B) and GPT2-xl (1.5B) frequently trend above the zero baseline in the first half, whereas GPT2-medium (355M) is more volatile, briefly dipping negative around mid-depth before recovering. As depth increases into the mid-to-late range, variability grows and model behavior diverges; GPT2-xl exhibits the largest positive spikes in the later layers but also sharp reversals, while GPT2-large (774M) displays a pronounced late-layer collapse with a deep negative trough around the final fifth of depth. Similarly, GPT-neo trends downward and finishes negative at the very end. This dominant pattern of depth-dependent instability and the eventual decline in monosemanticity observed in later layers can be attributed to the increased complexity and polysemantic nature of information nearer to the prediction layer, which is necessary for handling diverse tasks, suggesting that while DPO effectively optimizes early layers, the final layers resist this simplification to maintain functional performance.

This heatmap illustrates the performance comparison of video LMMs across 14 languages on ViMUL-Bench, where each box represents a model’s average accuracy across all question types for a specific language, with darker shades indicating higher accuracy. The closed-source model, GPT-4o, generally outperforms its open-source counterparts, maintaining the most stable results by clustering in the low-to-mid 50s and peaking in English at 63.2%. However, even this top-performing model faces challenges with several low-resource languages, as GPT-4o’s performance drops significantly from 63.2% on English to 49.6% on Urdu. Among open-source models, the multilingual VidLMM, ViMUL, achieves a better tradeoff with respect to high-and low-resource languages with an overall accuracy of 51.1%, followed by LLaVA-OneVision with 49.1%. While English serves as a high-resource outlier where LLaVA-OneVision reaches 65.1% and ViMUL reaches 61.8%, methods struggle on low-resource languages such as Sinhala, Urdu, and Tamil. Specifically, LLaVA-OneVision drops from 65.1% on English to 24% on Sinhala, indicating that the model struggles with underrepresented languages, whereas ViMUL outperforms LLaVA-OneVision by approximately 9.2% on Urdu and 7.5% on Sinhala, showing its efficacy on these low-resource languages. Smaller models, including VILA v1.5-7B, VideoChat2, LLaVA-NeXT, and VideoChatGPT, sit substantially lower overall and exhibit more pronounced volatility, with VILA sinking to 17.2% in Sinhala and 17.6% in Tamil. Ultimately, while all models show a strong language-resource effect, ViMUL provides a better tradeoff between high and low-resource languages, achieving an overall gain of 2% over LLaVA-OneVision and demonstrating greater robustness in environments where other open-source models collapse.

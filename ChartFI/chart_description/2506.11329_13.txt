This chart illustrates the performance and LLC hit rates of real-world workloads under various LLC orchestration schemes, comparing a Default baseline and an Isolate baseline against four A4 variants across two distinct execution environments. In the HPW-heavy scenario, which consists of 7 HPWs and 4 LPWs with 3 antagonists detected, the A4 variants significantly lift performance above the baselines, achieving speedups of 52% for HPWs and 22% for all workloads. High-priority workloads (HPWs) are indicated in bold, while asterisks denote workloads experiencing pseudo LLC bypassing. Notable gains are concentrated in memory-sensitive benchmarks like x264, where A4 rises well above 2.4x relative performance, alongside meaningful improvements in Fastclick, Redis-C, and the Avg (HP) bar, which reaches the mid-1.5x range. Conversely, workloads such as lbm* and bwaves* remain near 1.0 across all methods, indicating limited headroom or low sensitivity to the policy. The LLC hit-rate trace generally trends upward when A4 delivers its largest gains, suggesting that speedups are closely tied to improved cache residency or reduced interference, while near-flat, low hit-rate segments align with benchmarks that barely respond. In the LPW-heavy scenario, comprising 4 HPWs and 8 LPWs with 4 antagonists detected, the benefits are more broadly distributed, resulting in speedups of 47% for HPWs and 33% for all workloads. While x264 again stands out with A4 pushing to approximately 2.0–2.4x, visible improvements also appear in mcf and blender, consistent with workloads sensitive to cache and memory pressure. Some benchmarks show little separation between Default, Isolate, and A4 despite changes in LLC hit rate, implying bottlenecks outside the last-level cache or already-saturated performance. Ultimately, the data indicate that A4’s advantage is driven by reducing cache contention in antagonistic mixes, yielding larger relative gains when interference is high and cache behavior can be materially improved.

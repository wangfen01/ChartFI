This qualitative visualization of q(.) scoring distribution for test samples from different categories illustrates how the percentage of samples varies over the normalized range across eight distinct objects. In these panels, the dashed lines indicate the mean percentage values of sketches falling into a specific scoring range among four instantiations, while the shaded area highlights individual differences between the methods. The data reveals that samples corresponding to smaller q values are often aesthetically less pleasing, hard to recognise or simply incomplete and unreliable sketch data, which corresponds to the generally low percentages observed near zero. Instead, the dominant behavior is a late-stage accumulation of probability, where curves rise gradually and peak strongly between roughly 0.7 and 0.9 before dropping as q approaches 1.0. While all four GACL instantiations show a similar trend of score distribution change, the q(.) learned by Ours-Sca/Ours-Mul/OursAdd/Ours-Cos is noticeably different as evidenced by the shading areas, particularly where the spread widens in the high-q region. This divergence is visible in categories like Birthday cake, Umbrella, Alarm clock, and Computer, which display pronounced high-q surges, whereas Rabbit and Passport follow a similar pattern but with a smoother ramp. Pig stands out for having more structure in the middle, implying multiple regimes, while Angel appears as an anomaly with a distinct localized spike and significant variance in the shaded regions, contrasting with the consistent late-rise pattern seen elsewhere.

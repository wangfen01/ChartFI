This correlation heatmap illustrates the correlation of performance, measured by Absolute Trajectory Error (ATE), across 16 different static RGB imaging perturbations, revealing how similarly a model's performance degrades under different types of visual distortion. The matrix is dominated by high correlation values, with most pairs clustering between 0.8 and 1.0, indicating that many perturbations produce highly redundant impacts on performance. Strong intercorrelations are particularly evident within specific families of distortion; for instance, blur effects like Defocus, Gaussian, and Glass Blur are tightly clustered. Similarly, perturbations like Gaussian Noise, Shot Noise, Impulse Noise, and Spatter have high correlations with others, forming a distinct noise-and-speckle group where performance impacts are nearly identical, with correlations often at or above 0.99. Digital artifacts such as JPEG compression and Pixelation also show very high correlations with several blur and noise types, suggesting their impact on performance is largely interchangeable with these other common degradations. In contrast, certain atmospheric and lighting adjustments exhibit more unique performance characteristics. Brightness stands out with markedly lower correlations across the board, showing the weakest relationship with Snow (a correlation of 0.43). Other weather-related perturbations like Snow, Frost, and Fog also demonstrate weaker correlations with the main blur/noise cluster, indicating they introduce distinct challenges. This pattern suggests that while many perturbations are interchangeable for robustness testing, the distinct behavior of effects like Brightness and Snow highlights their importance for creating a diverse test set; however, while these correlations reveal broad performance patterns, they may not capture every specific effect.

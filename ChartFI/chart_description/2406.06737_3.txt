These two heatmaps display the Attack Success Rates (ASRs) for prompt extraction attacks, with panel (a) showing the corresponding ASR for each singular attack and panel (b) demonstrating the ASR for each compound attack across various models. In the singular setting, the data reveals that there are attack categories that are effective across all models, such as Prefix Injection (PI) and Distractor Instruction (DI), which consistently yield high vulnerability scores. Notably, all models have at least one attack category that has over 80% attack success rate, with GPT-4-1106 having one prefix injection prompt that has a 99% success rate. While the larger open-source models were initially characterized as resilient, the visual evidence contradicts this, indicating that the overall most insecure model is Llama-2 70B, which maintains high ASRs across the majority of singular pairings compared to the steeper declines seen in models like GPT-3.5. Moving to the compound setting, success rates for compound attacks varied, with certain combinations proving more potent than others; the first four combinations remain consistently strong across models, but there is a pronounced drop to near-zero ASRs for many models across the middle and right-side pairings, suggesting that stacking specific attack components often results in failed attacks for most architectures. A significant anomaly in this trend is GPT-4-1106, which does not follow the general pattern of improved defense (lower ASR) on the right side of the compound matrix; instead, it creates a distinct island of high values—such as 0.81 and 0.79 on combinations where others score near zero—indicating that it remains uniquely susceptible to these complex, stacked attack vectors while other models successfully resist them.

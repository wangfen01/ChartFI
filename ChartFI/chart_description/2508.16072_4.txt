The heatmap displays model performance where each cell shows average accuracy under one of four prompting modes (A–D), evaluated using both strict (exact role) and relaxed (role group) criteria. Stronger models appear toward the top, and the data reveals a clear uplift when moving from strict to relaxed prompting for most models. While strict role identification remains challenging for most models, their performance under relaxed scoring demonstrates emerging potential. Mode A achieves the highest scores under both strict and relaxed conditions, with DeepSeek-R1 leading the strict A condition at 0.55. Under strict prompting, performance is generally moderate in modes A through C, and shifting the prompt perspective from first-person (Mode B) to third-person (Mode C) yields similar results. However, accuracy drops sharply in mode D across nearly all models, indicating that strict D is consistently the most challenging setting and a universal failure point. The biggest relaxed-mode gains concentrate among the top tier, where GPT-4o rises from 0.51 and 0.44 in strict A and C to 0.65 and 0.63 in relaxed A_r and C_r, while Qwen2.5-72B similarly reaches 0.65 in A_r and holds near 0.59 in C_r. Relaxed prompting not only increases average scores but generally reduces the penalty of moving between modes for the strongest models. Despite this overall trend, notable anomalies exist. For instance, o3-mini is comparatively competitive in strict settings but underperforms in relaxed A_r and B_r relative to peers, suggesting it benefits less from relaxed prompting. Additionally, while shifting to third-person C_r maintains high scores for GPT-4o and Qwen2.5-72B, DeepSeek-R1 experiences a sharp anomaly, plummeting to 0.30 in C_r despite a strong 0.59 in A_r. Overall, the separation between leading models like GPT-4o, Qwen2.5-72B, and QwQ and the rest of the pack becomes most visible in relaxed modes A_r through C_r, highlighting relaxed prompting as a major driver of higher scores.

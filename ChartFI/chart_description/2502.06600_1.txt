These three heatmaps illustrate the Pearson correlation scores between different languages for the original multilingual CLIPScore model, represented by squared cells, and a finetuned version, represented by circular cells. The visualization compares language-to-language similarity patterns across MLaion (ML) and MLaion-F (MLF), where the left heatmap, which includes the entire VICR test set, displays a lower-triangular view of ML squared cells against MLF circular cells, while the center and right heatmaps isolate the original and finetuned models respectively. In the left heatmap, consistently high correlation values are observed across all languages, though MLF exhibits a more uniform cross-language similarity with most pairs clustering near the top of the scale compared to the wider spread and clearer pockets of weaker alignment in ML. Within MLF, similarities among Western European languages such as EN, PT, ES, FR, DE, NL, and IT are tightly grouped and remain very high, indicating a broadly coherent multilingual structure with only modest soft spots involving KO and ZH. Conversely, the ML structure is more heterogeneous; while European languages correlate strongly, KO and especially ZH show noticeably lower similarity with many non-Asian languages, creating a visible band of cooler values. The most pronounced anomaly is ZH’s comparatively weak alignment with several European languages in ML, which stands out against the otherwise warm European block. These results indicate that CLIPScore correlations are influenced by the quality of the translated captions, as the key shift from ML to MLF is a compression upward in similarity values and a reduction of cross-script or cross-family gaps, suggesting that the finetuned MLF yields more consistent multilingual alignment and mitigates the weaker links prominent in the original ML model.

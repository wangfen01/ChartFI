These four heatmaps illustrate the frequency of frame co-occurrence between text and image frames across the selected topics of crime, immigration, politics, and war, revealing that alignment between visual and textual narratives is highly topic-dependent and often concentrated in specific thematic clusters. For articles about crime, there is a tight and intuitive coupling where the use of criminal framing in images corresponds with texts that highlight the security, quality of life, and legal frames, suggesting that visual narratives of crime are anchored in threat and protection cues. Immigration exhibits a broader spread of moderate associations, yet it remains heavily concentrated around the security and legality image frames, indicating that coverage is visually and verbally pulled toward enforcement and rule-based interpretations rather than humanitarian or quality-of-life angles. Politics is characterized by a distinct polarization where most combinations are faint, but sharp spikes occur where political or public-opinion text aligns strongly with political imagery, implying episodic moments where visuals become explicitly partisan or campaign-oriented. War displays the most concentrated alignment of all topics, as images consistently highlight the security & defense framing even when the article text is highlighting the policy or legality frames, reinforcing a narrowly defined visual narrative that prioritizes conflict and security over other contextual elements. Across all four topics, a notable anomaly is the consistently weak presence of economic, cultural, and health linkages, suggesting these frames are either underused visually or lack matching textual framing, creating a significant gap between what is said and what is shown outside the dominant security-legal-political nexus.

The correlation matrix illustrates the relationships among nine standardized curiosity metrics for all messages, revealing how user-level and category-level attributes interact across dimensions of novelty, uncertainty, conflict, and social influence. A primary observation is that userConflict and userUncertainty are strongly correlated, with a Pearson’s correlation ρ of 0.93, indicating these user signals move almost in lockstep and capture a very similar underlying construct. On the category side, catNovelty aligns strongly with catUncertainty at 0.69, while catUncertainty aligns with catConflict at 0.72, suggesting a coherent pattern where more novel categories also tend to be more uncertain and conflict-prone. A particularly notable anomaly is that catConflict and catComplexity have a perfect inverse correlation (ρ = -1), a relationship that may reflect a derived variable or a specific scoring rule where increased category complexity is characterized by markedly lower conflict. Similarly, catComplex is strongly negatively related to catUncertainty at -0.73 and moderately so to catNovelty at -0.45. While userNovelty relates moderately to userUncertainty at 0.59 and userConflict at 0.57, cross-level links between user and category measures remain relatively weak, suggesting user dynamics are more internally consistent than they are driven by category context. Furthermore, direct and indirect social influence metrics, represented by maxDirInf and maxIndInf, capture distinct aspects of social-related curiosity and both metrics have weak correlations with the other metrics, generally ranging between -0.22 and 0.23. These findings ultimately highlight seven complementary metrics—userNovelty, catNovelty, userUncertainty, catUncertainty, catComplex, maxIndInf, and maxDirInf—that represent relatively distinct dimensions of the data.

The two heatmaps display the Pearson correlation coefficient of each pair of bias types, with results on the CrowS-Pairs and StereoSet datasets shown respectively on the left and right. In the CrowS-Pairs dataset, relationships are heterogeneous, characterized by distinct attribute groupings where strong positive correlations (i.e., r > 0.65) exist among pairs such as race color vs. gender and race color vs. religion. Notably, race color and gender biases have the highest correlation (i.e., 0.73) compared to other bias pairs, while disability and socioeconomic status tend to move in the opposite direction relative to gender. Conversely, sharp contrasts are evident as race color obtains the lowest correlation (i.e., -0.81) with sexual orientation; similarly, sexual orientation vs. nationality and socioeconomic status vs. religion obtain strong negative correlations. Age remains comparatively independent, showing only a moderate positive link with appearance (0.55) and otherwise weak connections. The StereoSet dataset, by contrast, reveals a simpler, more polarized structure with very strong positive coupling among gender, profession, and religion, indicating these attributes frequently co-occur. Race acts as the clear outlier in this set, exhibiting consistent negative correlations with the other three variables, most strongly against gender (-0.6). Ultimately, while CrowS-Pairs exhibits multiple competing associations and sharper negative contrasts across several attributes, StereoSet concentrates the signal into a single highly correlated block with race systematically moving in the opposite direction.

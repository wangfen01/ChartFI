The visualization presents the prediction distributions of positive, negative, and adversarial edges in the Movielens-1M and Yelp2018 datasets, illustrating how effectively different approaches preserve the separation between true links and non-links while suppressing adversarial signals. Four methods with three backbone models are compared, including the IF-based method GIF, the partition-based method GraphEraser, the retraining-based exact unlearning, and the proposed UnlearnRec on the GCN, SGL, and SimGCL backbones. In the retraining panels, positive edges remain concentrated at high scores while negative and adversarial edges sit clearly lower, creating a clean margin that serves as the target behavior. However, the baseline approaches often fail to replicate this distinct separation; in many backbones, GIF still exhibits significantly higher predicted scores for adversarial edges compared to negative edges, a trend that is particularly evident for SGL in both datasets where the adversarial distribution shifts dangerously close to the positive region. While the mean predicted scores for adversarial edges given by GraphEraser are closer to the mean of negative instances, the method still tends to leave noticeable overlap or introduce multimodality, suggesting residual memorization or instability. In contrast, the proposed UnlearnRec distributions most closely resemble retraining, yielding a sharper separation where positive edges stay tightly peaked on the right and adversarial edges are concentrated near low scores with minimal encroachment into the positive region. This superior performance is consistent across datasets, including Yelp2018 with SimGCL, where UnlearnRec effectively pushes adversarial edges away from positives without collapsing predictive confidence, thereby matching the retraining baseline by maximizing class separation and minimizing distributional distortion.

The density plots illustrate the perplexities (PPLs) of LLM-generated and human-written news, comparing distributions for real and fake articles across different authorship sources. In human-written news, real news exhibits lower perplexity than fake news; however, the two distributions heavily overlap, with the fake news curve only slightly shifted toward higher PPL, suggesting that while human fakes may be marginally less fluent, the separation is weak. Different from human-written news, LLM-generated fake news has lower perplexity than the real, an inversion of the pattern observed in human writing. This is demonstrated in both the gpt-4o-mini and Llama-3.1 generated news panels, where the "real" news distributions are broad with a pronounced right tail, while the fake variants cluster more tightly at the low end of the PPL scale. Furthermore, for both LLMs, the perplexity is lowered as the faking level increases from L1 to L3, meaning generated news at L3 consistently has lower perplexity, forming the sharpest and most concentrated peaks. This trend is amplified in the Llama-3.1 panel, where all fake levels concentrate strongly around a PPL near 1. The most notable shift across the authorship sources is the increasing separability between real and fake content, driven not by fakes becoming less fluent, but by them becoming unusually low-PPL and low-variance, a potential anomaly suggesting that text appearing "too fluent" may be a stronger signal of fakery in LLM-generated content.

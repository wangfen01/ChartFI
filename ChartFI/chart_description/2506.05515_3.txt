This visualization of predictions on the SOLAR dataset compares Tactis2 (purple), TimeGrad (red), TempFlow (orange), and TimeMCL (blue) across three dimensions (D = 1, 3, and 7), with each model predicting sixteen hypotheses within a light yellow prediction window. Across all series, the observations display a clear daily periodicity characterized by a sharp peak followed by a return toward the baseline, where dashed lines represent the predicted mean of the conditional distribution—computed as a weighted sum of hypotheses by scores for TimeMCL—and score intensities are displayed as shaded blue lines according to the scale on the right. While all methods learn the timing of the late peak, which rises sharply before dropping back toward baseline, Tactis2, TimeGrad, and TempFlow struggle to produce predictions that deviate significantly from the mean, as most of their predictions are sampled from the same mode of the distribution. Specifically, Tactis2 produces wide, spiky hypothesis spreads that occasionally overshoot the mean, TimeGrad aligns well with peak timing but shows moderate dispersion, and TempFlow often underestimates the sharpness of the maximum while maintaining a slightly elevated tail. In contrast, TimeMCL’s predictions are noticeably smoother and more diverse, demonstrating the ability to generate predictions far from the mean with a non-negligible probability. This indicates that TimeMCL successfully captures different modes and estimates the probability of each, including rare events, as evidenced by occasional extreme trajectories that far exceed observed maxima. The annealed version of TimeMCL remains comparatively conservative in its mean, while the relaxed version shifts the mean upward and broadens the upper tail, signaling that relaxing constraints increases the probability mass on extreme outcomes during rapid demand spikes.

The collection of boxplots tracks Benign Accuracy (Benign AC) and Attack Success Rate (Attack SR) across three federated learning algorithms (FedAvg, FedDC, and MetaFed) and various defense mechanisms, evaluated against a concentration parameter alpha (α), where lower values indicate more diverse local data. A dominant pattern emerges across most configurations: as alpha decreases, the average Attack SR increases, revealing a greater vulnerability in environments with more heterogeneous data. For the FedAvg and FedDC algorithms under poisoning-style defenses (DPois, MRepl, DBA, CollaPois), Benign AC generally remains stable in the mid-to-high 0.7 range, though with substantial run-to-run variability, especially at α=0.01. Concurrently, Attack SR is highest and most volatile at this low alpha value, compressing downward as alpha increases to 1 and 100. CollaPois exemplifies this trade-off; while maintaining consistent Benign AC, at alpha=0.01, COLLAPOIS achieves 83.33% Attack SR, dropping to 80.00% at alpha=1 and 79.89% at alpha=100. In contrast, defenses like DP, NormBound, and Krum show stable Benign AC, while RLR presents a significant anomaly: at α=0.01, its Benign AC collapses to near-zero, but it rapidly recovers at higher alpha values, indicating extreme sensitivity to data diversity. The MetaFed algorithm displays a more pronounced degradation in Benign AC as alpha increases, particularly for poisoning defenses. Furthermore, a slight difference is observed with MetaFed, as the Attack SR for CollaPois shows a minor increase with higher alpha values, deviating from the overarching trend. Overall, α=1 frequently serves as a practical inflection point, effectively reducing the Attack SR without a substantial loss in Benign AC, whereas the highest data diversity at α=0.01 exposes the most significant model instabilities and attack vulnerabilities.

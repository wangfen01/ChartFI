The chart compares multiple fusion models on SSIM (VIF) along the x-axis and latency in milliseconds on the y-axis, with bubble size representing FLOPs and an inset pie indicating each model’s share of parameter count. Overall, SSIM improves as you move right, but latency and compute cost vary widely, creating clear efficiency trade-offs. The proposed method, labeled as Ours, sits at the far right with the highest SSIM around 1.5 while maintaining a relatively small bubble, indicating strong quality with modest compute. In terms of inference speed, the average latency of the proposed method is 70.13 ms, which is higher than that of some lightweight methods, but still demonstrates a significant speed advantage over computationally intensive methods such as SwinFusion (1157.83 ms) and MambaDFuse (227.97 ms), realizing a time saving of 93.9% and 69.2%, respectively. For computational complexity, the FLOPs of the present method are 82.81 G which are 67.5% and 64.4% lower than those of SwinFusion (254.92 G) and MambaDFuse (232.60 G), respectively. Several models cluster around SSIM 1.4 to 1.45 with low-to-moderate latency, but differ in efficiency: HitFusion and DRDEC achieve similar SSIM with very low latency and small bubbles, while EMMA and MambaDFuse push latency higher with larger compute footprints for only marginal SSIM differences. CDDFuse stands out as a major anomaly: it has high SSIM but extremely large compute, shown by the largest bubble, and very high latency around 600 ms, suggesting poor practicality despite strong quality. SwinFusion is the most latency-heavy outlier at a slightly lower SSIM than the best models, indicating a steep speed penalty without a commensurate quality gain. At the low-SSIM end, ITFuse shows the poorest quality near 1.0 but also very low latency, reflecting a classic quality-speed compromise. The parameter pie reinforces that model size is unevenly distributed. In terms of the number of model parameters, the proposed method only requires 0.78 M parameters to achieve an excellent fusion effect, which is a significant compression of the number of parameters compared with the existing methods such as HitFusion (9.81 M) and EMMA (1.52 M), and the reduction rate reaches 92.0% and 48.7%, respectively. Although slightly higher compared to ITFuse (0.08 M), this slight increase in the number of parameters is an acceptable trade-off considering the excellent performance of the present method in all performance metrics. Ultimately, the main plot shows that larger parameter allocation and higher FLOPs do not reliably translate into better SSIM; instead, the most notable shift is that the best quality-per-latency frontier is achieved by the proposed method, while CDDFuse and SwinFusion represent efficiency breakpoints where latency and compute rise sharply for limited or no quality advantage.

This visualization compares how CLIP, Qwen2-VL 7B, Molmo-O 7B, and humans assign spatial attention across a 3x3 grid, where dashed lines delimit the ground truth sections and colors indicate the response for each cell. Human judgments reveal a highly structured pattern where the vertical component is separated into three equal bands, granting almost perfect performance on the top, center, and bottom designations, while for the horizontal component, humans tend to shrink the area for the center to only the central row of positions, leaving more space to the left and right. Within the human map, colors fade to white to represent a decrease in agreement votes for the majority class, showing higher agreement near the corners and the center and lower agreement near borders and around the center. Regarding the absolute position of the object, models show higher performance in the corners and the center, with Qwen2-VL and Molmo assigning these regions to the correct positions and obtaining almost perfect accuracy on the top right, bottom right, and bottom left sections of the scene. CLIP deviates most from these patterns, showing close to random performance by assigning top center to the central section of the scene and top left to most of the other cells, resulting in a systematic positional bias rather than content-driven localization. While Qwen2-VL 7B shifts away from this top-left dominance toward a more center-weighted allocation with a large central block, it exhibits sharper, blocky transitions and a heavier right-side bottom bias than humans. Molmo-O 7B aligns most closely with the human distribution, preserving a top-left preference in the upper band while maintaining substantial center and center-right structure in the middle and a more human-like split across the bottom, effectively reducing the blanket top-left bias seen in CLIP in favor of the smoother, more graded spatial preferences characteristic of human perception.

For the dataset, the model quality varied both between models and ROI handling approaches, with median c-indices ranging from 0.456 for the combination largestROIdiversityIndex + SVMvanbelle1 to a peak of 0.634 for allROIMax + randomForest. The top and right annotations of the heatmap highlight the best median c-indices for the models and ROI handling methods, respectively, revealing that the random survival forest consistently achieved a higher c-index for each ROI handling method compared to other model families. To rigorously evaluate these performance shifts, the schemes were compared with the reference model, largestROI + CoxStepAIC, which had a median c-index of 0.554. In the significance panel, colors represent differences between median c-indices, and stars show Cohen’s d effect size—where asterisks denote small to large effects. Analysis of these differences shows that 7 schemes performed better with a large effect size, 20 performed better with a medium effect size, and 37 performed better with a small effect size, while only 2 performed worse with a large effect size; for the rest, the effect size was negligible. Beyond central tendencies, the schemes also differed in the distribution of c-indices across the 1000 cross-validation iterations. The minimum c-indices were between 0.090 for the scheme weightedMeanROI + SVMvanbelle1 and 0.364 for allROIMax + randomForest, while the maximum c-indices ranged from 0.733 for largestROI + SVMvanbelle1 to 0.941 for randomROIdiversityIndex + SVM regression. Regarding stability, schemes such as MetaHistogram + randomForest, allROIMean + Weibull, weightedMean + randomForest, and allROIMax + Weibull were most robust to overfitting, as all of them had a c-index less than 0.5 in fewer than 60 out of 1000 iterations.

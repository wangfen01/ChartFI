These heatmaps illustrate the effects of input-output noise on the identification of natural frequencies, damping ratios, and mode shapes for an 8-element 3D beam using the iLF method at a minimum order of 32. The data reveal a distinct contrast between the stability of lower modes and the degradation observed in higher orders. The first nine modes are coherent with the analytically derived values for all noise levels, demonstrating robust identification, with the notable exception of mode 5 at the 1.5% noise level, where a distinct drop in mode shape agreement is observed. As expected, the maximum discrepancies are found for the higher modes, where parameter stability deteriorates significantly. While natural frequency estimates remain largely stable with deviations clustered near zero for most entries, stability breaks for specific higher modes; notably, modes 11 and 16 exhibit frequency differences rising into the multi-percent range, coinciding with missing values at specific noise settings that suggest estimation failure or mode tracking loss. The damping ratio proves far more sensitive than frequency, transitioning from modest fluctuations in low modes to large swings and sign changes in mid-to-high modes. Modes 7, 11, 15, and especially 16 show substantial damping volatility, with mode 16 displaying extreme positive spikes reaching nearly 50% difference at higher noise levels, indicating that damping is the first parameter to degrade. Mode shape agreement via MAC remains near-perfect for the majority of modes, yet clear pockets of deterioration align with these parameter anomalies: alongside the singular dip in mode 5, mode 10 shows a progressive drop in correlation, mode 14 collapses at the highest noise, and mode 16 remains consistently low. The dominant pattern indicates robust identification for lower modes contrasted with abrupt, mode-specific breakdowns in higher modes, characterized first by erratic damping, followed by frequency outliers and reduced shape correlation.

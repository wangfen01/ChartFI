This heatmap illustrates the minimum size of P needed to reject the null hypothesis across various model architectures and feature sets, with values in the cells representing the specific sample counts required for verification. The vertical axis categorizes different models ranging from LDM256 to U-ViT256-T2I-Deep, while the horizontal axis displays feature combinations used for fitting, separated by blue vertical lines that denote the complexity of the feature set. These sections progress from individual features and existing MIAs on the left, through all possible combinations of two and three features, to the inclusion of all available features on the far right. A color gradient on a logarithmic scale indicates magnitude, where red represents high sample requirements and green denotes lower requirements. The data reveal that applying new features in CDI leads to a remarkable reduction of the number of samples needed to reject the null hypothesis with p < 0.01, a trend particularly evident in the initially most challenging cases of models trained on higher-resolution large datasets. For instance, for the U-ViT512 model, the number of samples required from a data owner for confident verification decreases substantially from 20,000 when relying on existing MIAs to 2,000 when utilizing the full feature set.

The figure compares two confusion matrices for a seven-class emotion classifier, illustrating the shift in predictive patterns from a Baseline model to an Ordinal variant. A structural analysis reveals that the baseline confusion matrix makes more severe misclassifications that are far from the main diagonal, indicating errors between semantically distant emotions. In contrast, in the ordinal confusion matrix, the misclassifications tend to move away from the top-right and bottom-left corners, where the misclassification error is at its maximum, and gather around the diagonal. This shift toward diagonal clustering correlates with performance improvements in specific categories; relative to the Baseline, the Ordinal model improves correct recognition for sadness (183 to 191), disgust (145 to 171), fear (161 to 165), and joy (200 to 201). However, the tendency for errors to concentrate locally results in noticeable degradation for intermediate classes, with correct predictions for anger dropping from 156 to 136 and guilt from 144 to 117, while shame remains essentially flat (136 to 132). The most prominent manifestation of this pattern is a sharp increase in anger being misclassified as the adjacent class of disgust (rising from 22 to 53), suggesting the Ordinal approach pulls anger toward disgust more often than before. Similarly, ambiguity persists between the neighboring classes of guilt and shame; guilt predicted as shame increases slightly (29 to 34), while shame predicted as guilt decreases (47 to 32). Overall, the Ordinal model trades improved performance on distinct classes for increased confusion among semantically adjacent negative emotions, effectively minimizing the severity of errors by constraining them to the immediate vicinity of the true label.

This visualization presents a min–max normalized RMSE comparison between CirT and various data-driven and numerical methods across pressure levels for geopotential height z, temperature t, and wind components u and v, with Weeks 3–4 and Weeks 5–6 lead times shown side by side. A lighter color indicates better results, and across nearly all variables, CirT consistently outperforms all models by achieving lower errors across all pressure levels. Errors are systematically larger in the upper troposphere and lower stratosphere, with the darkest bands concentrated around roughly 100–300 hPa, while near-surface levels and the deepest layers trend lighter, indicating comparatively better skill. For geopotential height and temperature aloft, Weeks 5–6 generally show a broad degradation relative to Weeks 3–4, most evident as a strengthening and widening of the mid-to-upper-level error band. When the lead time increases from Weeks 3-4 to 5-6, the performance of baselines significantly reduces, especially in temperature; in contrast, CirT maintains relatively low errors and demonstrates superior stability. The 2 m temperature summary bars reinforce this pattern, as skill is materially better at Weeks 3–4 and drops at Weeks 5–6, with the spread between models becoming more pronounced at the longer lead while CirT remains the top performer. Wind components exhibit the clearest vertical structure, where both u and v show a pronounced error maximum centered near 200–300 hPa that persists and often intensifies at longer leads, consistent with reduced predictability in jet-level flow. Near-surface wind summaries for u10 and v10 are comparatively uniform and lighter, suggesting that while longer leads still degrade performance, the largest forecast challenges are concentrated aloft rather than at 10 m. Overall, the dominant pattern is a lead-time-driven loss of skill that is amplified in the 100–300 hPa layer, yet CirT consistently maintains the lowest error profile across both temporal scales and all atmospheric depths.

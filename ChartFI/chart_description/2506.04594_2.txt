The chart illustrates the transmission modes of Single Link Operation (SLO), Multi-Link Operation with Non-Simultaneous Transmit/Receive (MLO-NSTR), and Multi-Link Operation with Simultaneous Transmit/Receive (MLO-STR) for four packets over time. In SLO, the transmitter can use only one link to transmit data at any given time, resulting in all packets being serialized on Link 1 where each transmission is preceded by noticeable busy and back-off intervals, creating a sequential flow that pushes later packets further right in time. Under MLO-NSTR, multiple links connected to a Multi-Link Device (MLD) can either synchronize and transmit simultaneously or operate with only a single active link, which initially improves parallelism as the MLD defers Link 2’s back-off counter to allow the simultaneous transmission of Packets 1 and 2 on both links. However, the chart reveals significant coordination overhead in NSTR mode; when transmitting Packet 3, the exclusive channel occupancy of Link 1 inhibits the carrier-sensing capability of Link 2, thereby suspending the back-off process on Link 2 and creating a long cross-link interference segment that delays the completion of later packets. In contrast, MLO-STR allows packets to be transmitted independently on different links, enabling Packets 1–4 to be transmitted asynchronously and concurrently on Links 1 and 2 with fewer and shorter idle-control segments. By eliminating the cross-link interference blocks and deferral periods seen in NSTR, MLO-STR significantly reduces the overall transmission time and ensures that later packets complete with a smoother cadence and reduced end-to-end latency accumulation, making it the most consistently efficient pattern for parallel delivery.

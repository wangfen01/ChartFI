The chart presents violin plots comparing the distribution of knowledge test scores across three content types—Generalized, Personalized, and Textbook—for the subjects of philosophy, psychology, and government. A significant main effect of content type was observed for the Philosophy and Psychology subjects, with the Personalized condition showing higher test scores than both the Generalized and Textbook conditions. In philosophy, this effect is visible as an upward shift in the Personalized score distribution, which has a central tendency around the 7–8 range, while Generalized and Textbook scores cluster closer to the mid-5s to low-6s; the Textbook distribution also exhibits a wider spread with a noticeable high-end tail, suggesting less consistency. The separation is most pronounced in psychology, where Personalized content scores are concentrated higher and show a reduced lower tail compared to the Generalized condition, which has the lowest center and broadest dispersion. In contrast, no significant main effect was observed for the Government subject. For government, all three content types yield relatively strong performance, and while Personalized and Textbook scores both center higher than Generalized, their distributions overlap substantially, with the Textbook condition showing a slightly higher median than the Personalized condition but a comparable spread.

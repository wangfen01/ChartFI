The seven pie charts illustrate the proportional distribution of task-oriented metrics across different task attributes, revealing that for each category, the top three task-oriented metrics account for the vast majority of all metrics. In the Simulated society task, the recommended metrics are social and decision-making, performance, and psychological metrics, reflecting an emphasis on emergent group behavior and outcome quality. This contrasts with Opinion dynamics, where the most relevant choices are performance, external alignment, and bias, fairness, and ethics metrics, the latter of which occupies a significantly larger share here than in other simulation contexts. Simulated individuals present a more balanced profile, splitting weight across performance, psychological, and internal consistency metrics to provide a broader evaluation lens for single-agent behavior. Decision making is characterized by a prominent social and decision-making slice alongside strong performance, while Psychological experiment and Educational training are heavily dominated by psychological metrics, with the latter also retaining a sizable performance component to measure both learner state and achieved results. Writing serves as the primary outlier, where content and textual metrics take the largest share and internal consistency is substantial, while psychological and social metrics are minimal, shifting the focus from behavioral evaluation to linguistic quality. Overall, the data demonstrates a task-dependent reweighting from performance and psychology in simulations to text quality and consistency in writing, with bias, fairness, and ethics metrics remaining relatively minor across most categories except for opinion-based tasks.

This kernel density estimate plot illustrates the distribution of cross-modal attention outputs across six languages for LLaVA-1.5 before and after a multilingual intervention, where the x-axis represents the inner product between attention outputs and the normal vector of the hyperplane and the y-axis indicates the density of samples occurring at each value. Under standard inference, the projection values of English and non-English vectors are distinctly separated at the zero point, with non-English languages concentrated on negative projections while English stands out as a distinct positive cluster around 0.0–0.2, indicating that the cross-modal attention patterns of LVLMs exhibit significant misalignment for identical images depending on the language used in the same meaning query. Following the intervention, the distributions of non-English languages shift closer to that of English, collapsing into a much tighter, overlapping band centered slightly above zero with more pronounced density peaks. This sharp reduction in between-language dispersion eliminates the English-specific offset and causes the negative mass seen in the standard setting to largely disappear, effectively normalizing the projection behavior across languages. Such alignment supports the hypothesis that CLAIM effectively mitigates multilingual object hallucination by reinforcing cross-lingual consistency in visual perception attention, leaving only minor residual differences in peak height and spread.

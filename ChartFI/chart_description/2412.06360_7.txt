The chart illustrates the trajectory of building energy-efficiency targets for China and India from the 1980s through 2050, distinguishing between voluntary and mandatory policies as well as aggressive versus moderate implementation pathways. China has methodically elevated the design standards for energy efficiency in residential buildings, establishing a four-stage progression with energy efficiency targets of 30%, 50%, 65%, and 75%. Specifically, aggressive efforts to enhance energy efficiency in this zone led to the establishment of a 75% target in 2018, while a concurrent track followed a moderate pathway from 2000 to 2022, progressing from a target of 30% to 65%. In the future, China is progressively moving toward the construction of ultra-low-energy, nearly zero-energy, and zero-energy buildings; eventually, all zones are projected to meet nearly zero energy standards (i.e., over the energy efficiency target of 90%) by 2050. This rapid escalation and high ambition level contrast with India’s trajectory, which begins later with a voluntary benchmark around 2018 at roughly the 25% range. India’s projection shifts into a phased, forward-looking pathway that reaches roughly 50% by 2050, indicating a gradual tightening rather than the steep acceleration seen in the Chinese data. Consequently, the most notable shift is China’s move from incremental efficiency standards to explicit near-zero and zero-energy ambitions, creating a visible divergence from India’s mid-century target level, which lacks comparable near-zero markers.

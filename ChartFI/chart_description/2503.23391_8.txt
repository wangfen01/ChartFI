This chart displays a breathing sequence measured as distance over time, illustrating a segment that includes normal breath, deep breath and smoking breath, and compares the results of common trough detection with a two-step trough selection method. The initial period, from 0 to approximately 14 seconds, shows relatively regular, shallow oscillations characteristic of normal breathing, with a depth of about 1 cm. Following this, the pattern shifts to breaths with more pronounced features; it is obvious that smoking breath and deep breath have larger amplitudes and longer durations than those of normal breath, with depths generally larger than 1.4 cm. The key distinction between these two more pronounced breath types lies in their temporal structure. The biggest difference between smoking breath and deep breath is that the inhalation duration is as long as the exhalation duration in a deep breath, as seen in the wide, symmetrical cycle from roughly 22 to 29 seconds. In contrast, the smoking breath event around 14 to 18 seconds is defined by an asymmetrical cycle where the inhalation duration is shorter than the exhalation duration. The markers indicate that the two-step trough selection method correctly identifies the primary troughs for each of these distinct breath types, whereas the common trough detection method identifies all local minima that cross the threshold, not exclusively the troughs that define the start of each major inhalation event.

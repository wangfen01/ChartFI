The charts illustrate the interaction between name-based gender cues and occupational context in Llama-3.1-8B and Mistral-7B, plotting the change of the dot product between the name embedding from a template sentence and the gender direction on the left of each subfigure, and the change of the output probability of the token “female” with and without mentioning an occupation on the right. The horizontal axis represents “% Female Name,” defined as the real-world gender distribution of a name, while the color gradient denotes “% Female Bios,” representing the percentage of biographies of female individuals which mirrors the gender breakdown of an occupation in real life. Across both models, there is a strong, monotonic relationship where the likelihood of producing a female outcome rises from near zero to near one as the female name share increases, with the steepest transition occurring in the mid-range bins. Superimposed on this primary driver, we observe that the gender representation of first names generally shifts with occupational contexts, where, within each gender bucket along the horizontal axis, stereotypically feminine occupations lead to more positive dot products with the gender direction, whereas stereotypically masculine occupations cause the names in the same gender bucket to have more negative dot products. This translates to a higher predicted probability of the “female” token with the mention of a stereotypically feminine occupation, and vice versa. Crucially, we also see that the results for strongly masculine or feminine names are less affected by occupation than those for gender-ambiguous names, implying that occupational context exerts the strongest influence when the name's inherent gender signal is less determinate. The violet star indicates the non-stereotypical baseline where the occupation placeholder is replaced with the string “person,” and these markers frequently sit below the main occupation-conditioned clusters in the dot product metrics, suggesting that adding an occupation tends to amplify gender-direction alignment rather than neutralize it. While Mistral-7B exhibits a sharper, more saturated rise in probability at high female-name bins compared to the slightly more dispersed Llama-3.1-8B, the data reflect a consistent trend across the models that internal gender representations and output probabilities are systematically modulated by the stereotypical gender association of the accompanying occupation.

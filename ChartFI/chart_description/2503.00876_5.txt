This chart illustrates the SRL performance gain compared to VANILLA across age ranges, where the primary metric is the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) improvement calculated as Vanilla minus Best. The visualization features a dual-axis design where the dark blue bars represent error reduction and a gray histogram in the background shows the distribution of samples across age groups, categorized into few-shot (≤20 samples), medium-shot (21-99 samples), and many-shot (≥100 samples) regimes via background shading. SRL substantially improves the performance on the medium-shot and few-shot regions age < 20 and > 70, with the most dramatic gains concentrated in the early few-shot region at the far left where improvements frequently reach the high single digits to low teens. As the number of training samples increases into the many-shot range—peaking between ages 30 and 50—the performance improvement sharply compresses toward a zero-baseline, characterized by frequent small fluctuations and occasional modest regressions where the VANILLA model slightly outperforms the SRL approach. This trend of diminishing returns in high-resource settings is reversed at the far right of the age spectrum; as sample counts drop back into the medium-shot and few-shot bands for ages above 70, the MAE improvement turns consistently positive again and climbs into the low-to-mid single digits. Ultimately, the data reveal a clear inverse relationship between sample density and method efficacy, highlighting that the primary value of the SRL approach lies in its ability to provide significant robustness and error reduction in data-scarce regimes rather than in high-resource many-shot tasks.

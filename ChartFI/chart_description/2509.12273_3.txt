The figure compares how 12 models’ estimated probabilities track a set of synthetic labels from 0.0 to 1.0, displayed as violin distributions where blue represents results without chain-of-thought prompting and orange represents results with it. A gray dashed line represents the ideal scenario where estimations perfectly match the synthetic labels. While the general trend shows estimates rising as the synthetic label increases, the data indicates that all LLMs exhibit a stepped distribution rather than the ideal linear trend. Specifically, the distribution of estimates follows similar patterns for both low user preferences (weights ranging from 0 to 0.3) and high user preferences (weights ranging from 0.7 to 1.0), where LLM estimations remain consistent regardless of the precise synthetic label values within these ranges. Furthermore, for balanced user preferences (weights ranging from 0.4 to 0.6), LLM estimates tend to cluster around 0.5. This stepped pattern persists across different LLMs and remains consistent with or without chain-of-thought prompting, although the use of chain-of-thought generally tightens the distributions and pulls estimates closer to the diagonal reference trend. While some models demonstrate more robust estimation capabilities—such as Gemma-2-9B, GPT-4o, OpenAI o1-mini, and OpenAI o1—with shorter distribution tails and clearer separation, smaller models like Phi-3-mini exhibit heavier overlap and noticeable central clustering. Ultimately, this behavior reveals the LLM's ability to approximately classify user preferences, or more broadly, sentiments, from natural language, while struggling with fine-grained numerical outputs.

Scatter plots compare the mesh quality metrics of all methods applied to the dataset, revealing distinct tradeoffs between angle quality, numerical conditioning, and element-shape regularity. In these visualizations, data points in the top-right corner represent better performance for the minimum dihedral angle, whereas data points in the bottom-left corner indicate better performance for the maximum dihedral angle, condition number, edge ratio, and equiangle skewness. The results demonstrate that our method excels in controlling both small and large angles in the tetrahedral meshes; specifically, it concentrates around higher minimum dihedral angle values (roughly high teens to low 20s) with averages near the low 50s, indicating fewer near-degenerate slivers. In contrast, TetGen sits at very low minimums (around 2–3) despite similar averages, and stands out as an outlier in maximum dihedral angle with values near 176–178 degrees, implying many nearly flat angles. As shown in the charts, our method demonstrates advantages in terms of condition number, edge ratio, and equiangle skewness, maintaining low maximum condition numbers (roughly 2–4) that suggest superior numerical robustness compared to the instability observed in Gmsh or the high maxima of TetGen. While MMG3D generally exhibits the highest edge ratios and skewness averages, indicating systematically more elongated elements, the data reveal specific strengths in other methods: Gmsh excels at controlling the worst equiangle skewness, evidenced by a lower ceiling on maximum skewness values. However, regarding the proportion of bad elements—where lower data points suggest a smaller percentage of poor-quality elements—our method maintains poor elements within a minimal range, staying near zero across tetrahedra counts, whereas Gmsh hovers around 6–7%. Ultimately, our method demonstrates superior control over maximum and minimum angles, as well as a better proportion of poor-quality elements, occupying the healthiest regions across the majority of diagnostics.

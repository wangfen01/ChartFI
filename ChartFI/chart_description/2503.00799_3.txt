The aggregate performance across six benchmark domains is evaluated using Interquartile Mean (IQM) and Optimality Gap metrics, where higher IQM and lower optimality gap scores indicate superior generalization. In MO-LunarLander, the best-performing algorithm, GPI-LS, achieved an IQM NHGR score of 0.66 with an optimality gap of 33.9%, while older baselines like SAC and PCN cluster toward the left, indicating weaker generalization. This performance gap widens in higher-dimensional domains such as MO-Hopper, where the leading algorithm, MORL/D SB+PSA, managed to reach an IQM NHGR score of only 0.56 and an optimality gap of 46.4%, and further still in MO-HalfCheetah and MO-Humanoid. In these complex settings, the top algorithms, MORL/D SB+PSA and MORL/D SB, achieve IQM NHGR scores of just 0.28 and 0.41, respectively, though they maintain a clear rightward advantage over the field, particularly as the spread widens under the optimality gap view. Discrete environments reveal even more significant challenges; in MO-SuperMarioBros, the leading algorithm, GPI-LS, achieved an IQM NHGR score of only 0.07 and an optimality gap of 87.7%, reflecting an environment where all methods are compressed at very low absolute hypervolume levels. Similarly, MO-LavaGrid shows the performance of five discrete MORL algorithms, with MORL/D SB achieving the highest IQM NHGR score of 0.37, though it remains far from optimal. Across the benchmark, the MORL-D family demonstrates relative stability and a consistent performance advantage, particularly in harder continuous-control and grid settings where their rightward shift is most pronounced, even as absolute scores across all methods highlight the persistent difficulty of achieving near-optimal generalization in high-dimensional tasks.

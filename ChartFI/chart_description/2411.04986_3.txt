The bar charts illustrate the layer-wise probability evolution of the top and non-top languages across various Large Language Models. Contrary to the expectation that models immediately identify the language, in early layers, neither token is read out better than random chance, with probabilities remaining low and relatively flat. As the data progresses through the network, a significant phenomenon emerges regarding cross-lingual processing: hidden states for Chinese texts are close to the unembedding of English tokens. This is particularly evident in Llama-2-7B, where, after layer 17, the model representations are substantially closer to the English token than the Chinese token until layer 31, showing that the model's hidden space is indeed better scaffolded by English than Chinese. While Llama-3-8B and Llama-3-70B amplify the final outcome with an extreme final-layer surge for Chinese under Chinese input, the intermediate layers often display this underlying English stability. Distinct deviations occur in Baichuan2-7B, which remains strongly Chinese-dominant across nearly all layers even with English input, and Bloom-7B, which exhibits a U-shaped trajectory under Chinese input—dropping steeply early on before spiking at the end. Ultimately, the outsized last-layer jump relative to preceding layers across most models implies that language selection is not gradually resolved but is instead finalized abruptly at the output end, often overriding the intermediate representations where English scaffolding prevails.

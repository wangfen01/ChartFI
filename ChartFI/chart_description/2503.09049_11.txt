The detection results of ABARC and other attacks by NC are illustrated through boxplots comparing perturbation size by target label across the Cora, CiteSeer, and Flickr datasets, revealing that for all node-level attacks, NC cannot detect the backdoor. Across these datasets, a consistent separation exists where attacks generally require larger perturbations than the benign baseline, yet the detection results of our model are very similar to those of Benign, which reflects the robustness of our node-level backdoor attack. In the Cora dataset, benign perturbations remain low and stable across labels, while EXP, GTA, and UGBA exhibit marked upward shifts and wider spreads, particularly in mid-to-late labels where rising medians and upper whiskers suggest certain classes are harder to manipulate without larger edits. ABARC stands out in Cora as the most conservative attack, maintaining narrow dispersion and levels closest to benign, implying it achieves its effect with smaller, more stable perturbations. CiteSeer displays the smallest overall scale and cleanest stratification, with benign and EXP sitting at the lower end while UGBA typically demands the highest perturbations; however, ABARC drops back down significantly, showing distributions more compact than in Cora and suggesting more uniform requirements across labels. Flickr shifts back toward higher heterogeneity, where UGBA shows sizable increases and long whiskers signaling label-specific sensitivity, yet ABARC remains lower than other attacks and closer to benign despite occasional upticks in specific classes. Ultimately, while UGBA tends to demand the largest perturbations and the most significant label-driven swings appear in Cora and Flickr, ABARC consistently demonstrates a perturbation profile that closely mimics benign behavior, ensuring it remains undetected by NC across all tested node-level tasks.

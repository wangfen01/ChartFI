This set of eight panels depicts the effects of varying the number of hyperedges (K) in conjunction with both recent (T_r) and weekly (T_w) look-back horizons, using MAE as the evaluation metric for Boulder, Dundee, Palo Alto, and Perth. The dominant pattern reveals that the increase in hyperedge number has a clearly positive impact on more accurate forecast performance. Consequently, the optimal strategy for setting K is to use the largest reasonable value for each specific dataset’s network size: K=5 for Boulder, K=25 for Dundee, K=5 for Palo Alto, and K=9 for Perth. While model performance is far more sensitive to hyperedge count than to horizon settings, secondary optimization patterns are visible in the temporal parameters. Specifically, a recent look-back horizon (T_r) of 14 or 21 days frequently appears optimal among all configurations, offering a balance that minimizes error better than the shortest or longest extremes in several cases. Furthermore, for the weekly look-back horizon T_w, values greater than one (i.e., 2, 3, or 4) generally lead to improved performance, contradicting the assumption that longer horizons strictly introduce noise. Dundee stands out with a much lower overall MAE scale than the others, but ultimately, the data suggests that capacity (hyperedge richness) is the primary lever for accuracy, with horizon adjustments providing fine-tuned improvements once the optimal K is established.

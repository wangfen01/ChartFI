The heatmap summarizes the minimum Task Difficulty models set for each prompt category, organized such that task names occurring higher on the list are labeled as more difficult, on average, than those marked lower. Overall, unlawful and unacceptable tasks such as poisoning people, identity theft, workplace sabotage, blackmail, and sexual predation were on average labeled as more difficult—indicating less feasibility—than acceptable tasks such as organizing, cleaning, sorting, lifting, or washing. This broad distribution indicates that language models may contain an association between denoting a task as ‘impossible’ and preventing the robot from attempting that task without explicit instructions. However, the chart reveals critical lapses through red boxes that denote safety failures per Safety Criteria S2, highlighting instances where ‘Feasible’ items (rated 1, 2, or 3) should have been marked ‘Impossible’ (4 or 5). Copilot demonstrates the strictest refusal posture, consistently rating harmful tasks at the maximum difficulty with almost no deviations. While Gemini generally maintains a similar high barrier, it exhibits notable safety failures by rating "Physical Injury" and "Coercion" at a feasible level of 2, contradicting the notion of a uniform refusal policy. ChatGPT presents the most significant inconsistency risk, showing numerous safety failures where ratings dip into 1 to 2 for severe domains including domestic abuse, sexual predation, fraud, and political oppression, indicating a lower minimum difficulty threshold and a porous boundary between disallowed and allowed content. HuggingChat generally tracks the stricter models on the most severe items but shows occasional softening to 2 or 3 in categories such as poisoning people and domestic abuse, implying weaker consistency. In the mid-range, tasks like pseudoscience and detecting criminals show divergence where ChatGPT and HuggingChat remain feasible while others trend higher, and a minor anomaly appears among the otherwise easily feasible benign tasks, where ChatGPT rates “Sort Objects” significantly harder than the other models.

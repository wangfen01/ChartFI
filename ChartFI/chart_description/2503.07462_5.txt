The histograms of the energy harvested by each of the ten devices under the ten labelled fault conditions illustrate how energy output clearly distinguishes between the healthy state and damaged scenarios across a frequency range from 50 Hz to 500 Hz. Each plot shows the histogram of energy harvested by a single device across the 10 condition labels. It is evident in the histograms for Devices 1 and 2 that the Normal class consistently occupies the higher-energy region, while Ball Fault (BF) and Inner Race Fault (IRF) conditions cluster at significantly lower energies and Outer Race Fault (ORF) conditions tend to sit between them, where a limit can be defined to separate healthy events from damaged ones, creating a recurring low-to-high ordering that supports separability by energy but with device-specific scaling. As the natural frequency increases, the absolute energy scale and the spacing between clusters shift noticeably; for instance, devices at 100 Hz and between 350–500 Hz show energies extending into the 10⁻⁶ range, whereas several mid-frequency devices remain largely in the 10⁻⁷ range, indicating that device dynamics amplify energy differently at different natural frequencies. A significant pattern emerges at higher natural frequencies where clearer multi-modal structures allow multiple fault-severity groups to separate into distinct bands, contrasted with lower frequency devices where several fault labels compress into a narrow low-energy region. The inconsistent scaling across the array, where some high-frequency devices exhibit large right-shifts for specific labels while others do not, suggests resonance-specific sensitivity rather than a simple monotonic frequency effect, implying that while energy is a strong discriminator within a single device, cross-device comparisons require normalization to account for frequency-dependent gain.

This radar chart evaluates grasp quality across five performance dimensions—Functionality, Contact, Interpenetration, Self-Penetration, and Failure Rate—comparing results for seen versus unseen objects. Testing was conducted on five unseen scales: 1.5, 4.5, 7.5, 10.5, and 14.5, with both extrapolated scales and interpolated scales distinctively marked. A critical analysis of the axes reveals that since all metrics are better when smaller, a smaller area is preferable; therefore, the 1-scale configuration, which produces the largest polygon area, actually demonstrates the poorest combined performance. Conversely, the polygons contract toward the center as the number of training scales increases, indicating that increasing the diversity of training scales consistently enhances grasp quality for both seen and unseen objects. Functionality and Interpenetration exhibit the clearest sensitivity to this scaling, where the 1-scale baseline peaks with the highest error rates before dropping noticeably in the multi-scale configurations. The Contact dimension is comparatively stable, clustering in a narrower band across scales, while Self-penetration proves to be the most irregular dimension, showing pronounced dips and spikes that suggest inconsistent behavior rather than a smooth scaling trend. Finally, while the single-scale baseline suggests a performance gap between conditions, the more advanced configurations reveal that across all configurations, grasp quality on unseen objects remains comparable to that on seen objects, demonstrating robust generalization when training diversity is maximized.

This heatmap illustrates the F1 performance of nine algorithms—comprising PC, GES, and seven other Gene Regulatory Network Inference (GRNI) specific state-of-the-art methods—across ten colored column blocks representing six synthetic (LI, LL, CY, BF, BFC, TF) and four curated (mCAD, VSC, HSC, GSD) datasets. Each block is subdivided into five column strips corresponding to different dropout-handling strategies, where cell colors indicate the corresponding values with brighter hues representing higher F1 scores. To visualize differences among the majority of entries with a finer resolution, the minimum of the colorbar is anchored to 0.5, as 380 out of the 450 total entries have values greater than or equal to 0.5, and the maximum and minimum of each strip are annotated. Performance trends reveal that among the 90 dataset-algorithm pairs, ‘full samples’ (with dropouts) performs worse than ‘oracle’ in 65 instances (72%), while ‘imputed’ and ‘binary’ strategies yield even poorer results than ‘full samples’ in 45 (50%) and 58 (64%) of those pairs, respectively. Conversely, ‘zero-deletion’ is better than ‘full samples’ and than ‘imputed’ on 64 (71%) and 61 (68%) of the pairs, respectively. While PIDC and PPCOR emerge as consistently high performers across both synthetic and curated domains, and GRNBOOST2 remains competitive, SCODE is persistently weak and LEAP generally occupies the lower-to-mid range. A clear domain shift is evident as methods like PC and GES, which achieve solid results on synthetic benchmarks, lose substantial accuracy on curated datasets, particularly on GSD where nearly all methods cluster at low F1 scores. This divergence suggests that many algorithms are sensitive to real-world noise or network complexity, with sharp within-dataset swings across dropout-handling conditions further indicating that performance is often highly dependent on the specific evaluation setting rather than intrinsic algorithmic stability.

The figure compares the confusion matrices of prediction results among different methods. Visual annotations in the form of red boxes emphasize the significant regions of confusion observed in each method, highlighting how different strategies handle the disparity between frequent (head) and scarce (tail) categories. BCL performs well on most classes with an initial accuracy of 84.31%, but it still suffers from confusing head class samples as tail classes, specifically showing leakage where Plane and Car are misclassified as Ship and Truck. While CutMix slightly improves the overall accuracy to 84.41% and significantly improves the accuracy of head classes, its area-based labels exacerbate the overfitting to the head classes; this creates a distinct anomaly where many tail class samples, such as Ship and Truck, are confused with head classes. CMO improves upon CutMix, reaching 84.71%, by oversampling the tail class and partially reducing the confusion of samples between head classes and tail classes, yet it significantly sacrifices the accuracy of head classes, thereby limiting overall performance. In contrast, ConCutMix delivers the most consistent diagonal strengthening, successfully discriminating between the head classes and tail classes while maintaining the accuracy of the head classes. This method significantly improves accuracy on the tail classes without sacrificing the accuracy on the head classes, effectively reducing the confusion between them. Consequently, ConCutMix boosts the overall performance, elevating it from 84.31% to 86.07%, driven by stabilizing the hardest-to-separate classes and suppressing systematic misclassification pathways rather than by dramatic changes in already-easy categories.

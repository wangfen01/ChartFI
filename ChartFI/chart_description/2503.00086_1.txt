This chart illustrates the performance comparison of eight network architectures trained with eight sets of hyperparameters in type 1 of the position-length experiment, where Mean Log Absolute Error (MLAE) serves as the primary metric and lower values indicate superior performance. Across the eight tested network architectures, VGG19 and ResNet152 perform significantly better than other networks, with ResNet152 achieving the overall best result at -2.76 and VGG19 following at -2.41. The best-trained model for each kind of network is highlighted and labeled with the corresponding MLAE value, while others are shaded to emphasize the performance variance within each architecture. EfficientNet and DenseNet show comparable peak performance at -2.37, while simpler or non-convolutional models exhibit a clear degradation in accuracy, with LeNet and AlexNet reaching best values of -2.21 and -2.12, respectively, before shifting into positive territory with RelationNet at 0.11 and MLP at 0.35. The distribution of data points reveals that AdamW configurations, particularly those with high weight decay, consistently occupy the best-performing regions for stronger CNN backbones, suggesting high robustness to learning rate fluctuations. In contrast, SGDM settings introduce much wider dispersion and significant instability; this is most evident in AlexNet, which shows a large rightward spread toward positive MLAE, and the MLP architecture, which contains the single worst outlier near 4.0. Ultimately, while architecture choice establishes the baseline performance level, the choice of optimizer and hyperparameters dictates the degree of variance, with AdamW delivering more consistently low MLAE across deeper models compared to the high risk of performance degradation associated with SGDM.

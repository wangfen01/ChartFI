The heatmaps visualize Pearson correlation matrices to compare the pairwise relationships among all 13 features of the dataset, including the target, in the real minority set and in the GAN-matched synthetic set. Overall, the matched synthetic samples closely mirror the real correlations, demonstrating that the matching procedure preserves the original minority correlation structure across variables and maintains the broad directional relationships seen in the real data. However, several associations are noticeably strengthened or weakened, indicating the synthetic generator matches the general dependency pattern while smoothing or exaggerating specific pairwise links. In the real minority data, the target is most strongly and negatively associated with LSTAT, while showing weaker negative relationships with CRIM and PTRATIO, and only modest positive associations with CHAS and ZN. The synthetic data keeps the same headline story, with the target remaining strongly negative with LSTAT and negative with CRIM and PTRATIO, but the magnitudes shift. Specifically, the target-LSTAT relationship becomes even more negative, and the target's positive relationship with B becomes more pronounced than in the real data, suggesting the synthetic set may be overemphasizing the role of B in explaining the target. Several well-known structural correlations are present in both matrices, including strong positive coupling among RAD and TAX and a strong negative relationship between ZN and INDUS. In the synthetic version, these patterns are generally amplified, with RAD-TAX and ZN-INDUS appearing stronger than in the real minority data, which can be a sign of reduced noise or over-regularization in the synthetic generation. A notable anomaly is the ZN-AGE relationship, which is already strongly negative in the real data at -0.84 but becomes extremely negative in the synthetic data at -0.96, implying the synthetic process may be imposing an overly deterministic link between zoning and housing age. Taken together, the synthetic correlations look directionally faithful but slightly more extreme, which could improve downstream model stability while risking biased inference for variables whose relationships have been artificially tightened.

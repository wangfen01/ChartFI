The figure presents a comparative analysis of the Area Under the Curve (AUC) values for three estimation approaches—the RIV method, a correlation-based method, and an RMSE-based method—across six distinct system types. The heatmaps quantify performance across a grid where sample size (n) ranges from 50 to 5000 and target mutual information (gamma) spans {0.03, 0.07, 0.1, 0.15, 0.2} bits. A critical baseline for interpreting these results is that the achievement of a perfect empirical AUC value of 1.0 means that the distributions of the values generated from the null hypothesis (H'_0) and the alternative hypothesis (H_1^gamma) are perfectly separable. We observe improvements in the ROC curves as either n or gamma increases for all studied systems, with the dominant change being a rapid lift in separability from n=50 to n=250. Beyond this point, gains taper as many cells saturate around 0.99 to 1.00, especially when gamma is 0.10 bits or higher. The RIV method starts relatively weaker in the hardest corner cases, particularly for Trigonometric and NARX systems at low gamma and small n, but it catches up quickly to become effectively indistinguishable from the other methods once n reaches 250 to 500. The correlation method is consistently strong and often reaches near-ceiling values earlier than RIV in the low-MI regime, though it shows a notable weakness for the Polynomial system where values remain comparatively low at small n even as gamma increases. The RMSE method is generally robust, frequently matching or exceeding the correlation method in nonlinear settings with especially fast convergence for Polynomial, Trigonometric, MLP, and NARX, although it is comparatively slower for ARX at low gamma and small sample sizes. Ultimately, system complexity amplifies sensitivity to sample size when gamma is small, but these performance gaps largely disappear by n=5000, where all methods and systems converge to near-perfect values.

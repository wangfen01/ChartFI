The figure illustrates the clique size distribution (top) and clique count distribution (bottom) for three co-location datasets, revealing distinct structural properties across the networks. In the top panel, the probability of a clique having size q is heavily right-skewed, demonstrating that most cliques for all datasets are small in size and consist mainly of two nodes. Despite this shared dominance of small structures, the tail behaviors diverge significantly; Thiers13 maintains the heaviest tail with a visible secondary bump around the 30–40 range, indicating a rich presence of moderately large cohesive groups. Furthermore, while InVS15 shows a rapid decay near zero beyond small q, there is a comparably higher propensity for larger clique sizes, which declines more smoothly. In the bottom panel, the distributions of clique counts m further differentiate the networks, showing that the co-location graphs in LyonSchool have on average fewer cliques than the graphs in InVS15. Specifically, LyonSchool concentrates on lower m values with a relatively tight spread, whereas InVS15 is sharply centered at higher counts with pronounced peaks, suggesting a process that generates a high number of predominantly small cliques. Thiers13 spans the widest range, extending from roughly 100 to over 300, reflecting a high degree of variability and the capacity to support both large clique sizes and high clique counts.

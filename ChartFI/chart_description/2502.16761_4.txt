This visualization presents a comparison of pairwise disagreement levels between education groups across three distinct conditions: observed differences between human groups, the SubPOP-FT fine-tuned model conditioned on a source group, and a base model conditioned on a source group using zero-shot prompting. In the human response (left), the disagreement shows the pattern of locality, where disagreement increases as the disparity in education levels between two groups grows, resulting in a decreasing disagreement level as the education level becomes similar. This creates a clear gradient consistent with an ordinal structure, where the smallest gaps appear between adjacent categories and the largest gaps are concentrated between the extremes, specifically less than high school versus postgraduate. In contrast, the fine-tuned model (middle) reproduces a pattern resembling the human-human case, successfully capturing the disagreement pattern and locality even though SubPOP-FT compresses the absolute differences into a narrower band of values clustered between 0.09 and 0.13. Notably, this pattern persists even for groups not included in the bold-faced fine-tuning data, such as those with some college or an associate's degree, indicating the model's ability to generalize the education-distance gradient. Conversely, zero-shot prompting with the base model (right) does not exhibit the locality pattern seen in the human data and instead shows the highest overall distances, with values generally elevated relative to both humans and SubPOP-FT. While zero-shot prompting preserves a tendency for the extremes to be farthest apart, it lacks the structured gradient of the other two conditions and instead amplifies between-group separation, failing to reflect the nuanced, proximity-based disagreement levels inherent in human social strata.

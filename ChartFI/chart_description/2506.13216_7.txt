These scatter plots evaluate how well three different metrics—Capability Score θ, all_token_loss, and label_token_loss—predict downstream task accuracy on the MMLU and BBH datasets, with the prediction Mean Squared Error (MSE) of different loss calculation methods presented to quantify performance. The capability salience vector significantly improves the predictability of downstream task performance, as demonstrated by its derivative, the Capability Score θ, which most cleanly explains accuracy. Across both datasets, accuracy shows a strong, smooth, monotonic decline as θ increases, with the fitted curve tracking the data points tightly. In MMLU, accuracy drops from the low 0.8s to the mid 0.4s, while in BBH it falls from near 0.8 to the low 0.3s; in both cases, the reported MSEs are the lowest among the three predictors, indicating θ is the most reliable single-variable summary of performance. When using loss-based predictors, the relationship weakens and becomes noisier. For all_token_loss, both MMLU and BBH still show a clear negative trend, but with substantially more scatter and higher error than θ. In contrast, the label token loss does not perform well, as the predictive trend is weakest for this metric. For the MMLU dataset, the relationship is almost nonexistent, with a nearly flat fitted line suggesting it is poorly aligned with accuracy, while for BBH, it shows only a mild negative slope with large dispersion. A consistent pattern across the panels is that test points tend to sit above train and validation points at comparable θ or loss values, which may reflect dataset difficulty differences or evaluation mix effects.

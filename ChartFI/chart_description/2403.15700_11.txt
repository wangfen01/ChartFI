The residual energy of all 100 nodes in WSNs is compared among seven algorithms across four distinct scenarios, illustrating how each approach preserves node energy and manages load distribution. In panel (a), most methods are clustered high between 0.85 and 1.0 J, indicating broadly balanced energy usage, though k-means and VLEACH exhibit occasional sharp dropouts to near zero that suggest sporadic node overburdening. Panel (b) reveals a more polarized regime where LEACH and KM-LEACH sit markedly lower, often near 0.0 to 0.3 J, while VLEACH and k-means frequently oscillate between near-zero and 0.8 J, signaling instability; conversely, EECPK-means, EB-CRP, and IS-k-means maintain relatively steady mid-bands around 0.5 to 0.6 J with fewer extreme collapses. While panel (c) shows a return to higher residual energy for most methods, it is characterized by pronounced volatility for k-means and VLEACH, which repeatedly hit near-zero values across many nodes to create a clear anomaly pattern of intermittent node depletion. In panel (d), a distinct step change occurs around nodes 70 to 80 where several methods, particularly IS-k-means and EB-CRP, shift upward from approximately 0.5 J to 0.7 J, implying a regime change in energy allocation, whereas VLEACH continues to exhibit frequent near-zero plunges despite a high upper envelope. Across all scenarios, it is found that the residual energy curve of all nodes in the IS-k-means algorithm is smoother than that of the other six algorithms, which demonstrates that the IS-k-means algorithm is good at balancing the energy consumption of all nodes in WSNs. While EB-CRP also appears consistently stable, the frequent extreme low-energy outliers produced by k-means and VLEACH and the scenario-dependent depletion of LEACH and KM-LEACH highlight the superior reliability of the IS-k-means approach in maintaining network longevity.

Revealing a clear separation between methods in both convergence speed and stability, the proposed FADMM-D and FADMM-Q generally exhibit similar performance, both achieving the lowest objective function values among all the methods examined. These variants consistently reach the lowest objectives early and remain nearly flat at the bottom of each plot, indicating fast, stable convergence with minimal sensitivity to dataset type or problem dimensions, dominating on both structured datasets like mnist and mushroom and on synthetic random cases. Furthermore, the proposed FADMM-D and FADMM-Q still outperform FSA, which uses a sufficiently small step size to ensure convergence. Within the FSA family, FSA-I often decreases smoothly and approaches lower objectives over time, whereas FSA-II is frequently erratic with large transient surges, most notably in the madelon, TDT2, and random panels, where it briefly jumps to very high objectives before returning downward. Among the other evaluated techniques, SPM tends to be less efficient in comparison to other methods, characterized by a slow, smooth descent that remains materially higher for most of the time horizon. In contrast, SPGM-D and SPGM-Q, utilizing a variable smoothing strategy, generally demonstrate better performance than SPM, although this progress is accompanied by pronounced volatility. Specifically, SPGM-D and SPGM-Q exhibit repeated order-of-magnitude spikes on several datasets, suggesting unstable progress or sensitivity to step choices that interrupt otherwise downward trends, implying that average performance would mask substantial worst-case instability.

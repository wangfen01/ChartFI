These four radar charts provide a feasibility and accuracy comparison of five selected LLMs—Claude-3.5-sonnet, GPT-4o, Deepseek-V2, Llama3-8b, and Qwen2.5-72b—across various graph tasks, with concentric circles representing performance levels progressing outward from 20% to 100% in increments of 20%. Model performance varies substantially across tasks, revealing a clear hierarchy in capabilities as graph size and task complexity increase. On small graphs, feasibility is broadly high for core structural properties like Neighbor, Distance, and Connected, where Claude-3.5-sonnet and GPT-4o typically form the outer envelope; however, tasks like Diameter, MCS, and MVC demonstrate notably low feasibility, creating a significant spread between systems on these harder combinatorial problems. As the data transitions to large graphs, feasibility contracts for all models, particularly on optimization-heavy tasks such as TSP, MIS, MVC, and MCS, while simpler local properties like Neighbor and Distance remain more attainable. Accuracy mirrors this scaling effect even more severely; while models achieve moderate success on small graphs for Neighbor and Distance, accuracy collapses toward near-zero across most tasks on large graphs, with only residual signals remaining for the simplest properties. This shift highlights a widening gap between feasibility and accuracy, where scaling graph size transforms many tasks from being occasionally solvable to rarely correct, suggesting that while models may still produce a formatted output, the likelihood of maintaining reliability and correctness diminishes significantly under increased structural complexity.

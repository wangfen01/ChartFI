The visualization presents a dual-component analysis comprising a bar chart of valid sample sizes per country on the left—ordered such that same-region countries are consecutive—and a pairwise heatmap of Mean Differences (MD) in ALDi scores on the right. Sample availability is uneven, led by Jordan (547) and followed by Saudi and Syria (just over 400 each), while Morocco, Tunisia, and Iraq have notably smaller bases, providing important context for the stability of the observed contrasts. In the heatmap, the value represents the mean difference of the row country’s (r) and column country’s (c) ALDi scores for the N_rc sentences valid in both, where N_rc is shown as the bottom number in each cell. A striking regional divergence is visible in the top three orangish rows, which indicate that when sentences are valid in one of the Maghreb’s countries (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia) and another non-Maghrebi country, the annotators from the Maghrebi country rate these sentences to be less dialectal than the non-Maghrebi ones. The magnitude of this separation is substantial; for instance, the difference between Morocco and Saudi (MD = -0.29) can be close to 1/3, which is the difference between two consecutive levels of ALDi ratings (0, 1/3, 2/3, 1). A similar pattern holds true for Iraq to a lesser extent, characterized by negative differentials against most peers. Conversely, the Saudi row is dominated by positive (purple) values, indicating that Saudi annotators assign higher ALDi scores to sentences common with other dialects. Maghreb annotators apply stricter dialectal ratings and Saudi annotators apply more lenient ones compared to the central cluster of countries like Egypt and Jordan.

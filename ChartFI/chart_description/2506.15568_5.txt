The two heatmaps illustrate the distribution of pronoun associations across various large language models under stereotypical association and occupational fairness conditions, where darker colors indicate a higher proportion of association in the model outputs for the pronouns he, she, and they. In the stereotypical association panel, most models exhibit a strong skew toward she, which often represents the dominant share of completions, while he is generally lower and they remains the smallest component for most models, rarely accounting for more than 10% of completions. This gendered association pattern is particularly extreme in LLaMA 4 and Gemini 1.5 Flash, which show high bias scores for she at 0.86 and 0.83, respectively, while LLaMA 3 also demonstrates a sharp imbalance with she at 0.67 and he at 0.21. In contrast, Phi-3 stands out in this setting by exhibiting balanced behavior between he (0.34), she (0.31), and they (0.34), indicating significantly less bias than its peers. When transitioning to the occupational fairness panel, the distributions generally shift toward greater parity between he and she, compressing the extreme she-dominant behavior observed previously. However, even under these fairness constraints, notable imbalances persist; for instance, Claude 4 skews heavily toward her at 0.72 compared to him at 0.26 and they at 0.02. While they rarely exceeds 30% usage across the dataset, a subset of models including Gemini 1.5 Pro, Claude 3, and GPT-4o show moderate increases in their usage, reaching levels between 27% and 32% in the fairness setting. Ultimately, while the shift toward occupational fairness reduces gendered defaults for many models, the continued underuse of they and the presence of model-specific outliers highlight that neutral pronoun adoption remains inconsistent across the architectural landscape.

The chart tracks a sender-side timeline where radio conditions and scheduling efficiency deteriorate and then recover, with clear downstream effects on throughput balance, buffering, and latency. The top two subplots display the time series of allocated PRBs and the selected MCS for the UE, while the subsequent panels monitor the rate gap, buffer status (BSR), and delay. Early in the trace, link quality appears consistent, with MCS sitting at moderate levels within a tight 10–90th percentile band; during this period, the rate gap oscillates around zero, the buffer remains low, and delay stays near its baseline. A distinct regime change occurs starting from time 0.6s, where the MCS drops to very low values and remains suppressed for an extended interval. Notably, during this period, the allocated PRBs also decrease, even in the absence of cross traffic, which compounds the reduction in achievable spectral efficiency. This simultaneous drop in modulation quality and resource allocation causes the rate gap to frequently turn positive, indicating that the packet arrival rate exceeds what the PHY transport blocks can serve. This deficit aligns with the visible buffer build-up as BSR climbs steadily toward a peak, driving a lagged but strong increase in delay. Near 1.7–1.9 seconds, the channel recovers: MCS jumps upward and PRB utilization is restored, shifting the rate gap predominantly negative as PHY service exceeds packet inflow. This inversion coincides with a rapid drain of the buffer and a sharp collapse in delay back toward low levels, indicating the system is catching up and clearing queued data.

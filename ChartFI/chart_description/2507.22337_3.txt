This chart illustrates 20 models evaluated on the free generation dataset, measuring pairwise accuracy across various negation types. The first result column contains the full dataset, while later columns represent one negation type each, such as sentential, affixal, or implicit negation. Models are represented by the rows, where I is a shortcut for Instruct, and labels on the right express the architecture and training objective of each model: the first position shows the architecture, i.e., Sparse (S), Bi-encoder (Bi), Dual encoder (Du), Late-interaction (LI), Cross-encoder (C), and Transformer (T); the second position shows the training objective, i.e., Retrieval (Re), Search (Sea), Similarity (Sim), Ranking (Ra), Natural Language Inference (NLI), and Next Token Prediction (NTP). Performance in all models for sentential negation is higher than affixal or implicit, though sparse, dual, and bi-encoders generally exhibit poor performance on all types of negation, except Sentence-T5, a dual encoder trained for semantic similarity that exhibits the best performance among bi- and dual-encoders. Both late-interaction and all cross-encoder models, except nli-deberta-v3-base, show strong performance on all negation types, with BERT and T5-based cross-encoders performing better than models with a RoBERTa, ELECTRA, and DeBERTa backbone. Specifically, ColBERTv1 has a higher performance than ColBERTv2, and the MonoT5 models perform the best on all types of negation, maintaining high scores across nearly every slice with fewer sharp drop-offs than most alternatives. All transformer-based models, except for Qwen2-1.5B-I—which has a disadvantage in size, have been trained for NTP, and perform the worst of all LLMs—perform well on almost all negation types. Larger instruction-tuned LLMs like Mistral-7B-I, Llama-3.1-8B-I, and Qwen2-7B-I remain strong and stable, showing particularly solid performance in mid-to-late slices, which suggests robustness once task conditions become less adversarial compared to the earlier, more challenging negation categories.

This set of five radar charts visualizes the performance of the top-10 models from the main experiments for the eight academic domains, illustrating model performance across categories for leading open- and closed-source LLMs. Performance varies notably by task type, with the strongest and most consistent results appearing in generative tasks compared to discrete selection tasks. First, in the summarization (R-1) task, most models demonstrate high scores in the Engineering and Medical Science domain, whereas they exhibit relatively lower scores in Economy & Management and Socio-Professional Studies, with the overall traces clustering tightly. In the short-answer task (BERTScore), despite mild overall variation, model performance is consistently lower in the Economy & Management and Liberal Arts domains, indicating that while factual, technical content is handled reliably, humanities-style responses present a challenge. The Multiple Selection (A-4) task shows a large variance in model performance; specifically, models achieve high accuracy in Biology & Earth Science but perform poorly on categories that require abstract or complex reasoning, such as Liberal Arts and Physics & Mathematics. Similarly, for both MCQ and Boolean formats, performance is higher in Engineering and Chemical & Biochemistry, but lower in Biology, Liberal Arts, and Physics. While many models exhibit significant domain-driven swings—including a sharp anomaly in Boolean accuracy where one model trace collapses dramatically in Engineering—o3-mini and Mistral-24b show stable performance across domains, reflecting stronger generalization. Overall, technical and medical domains tend to be the most favorable across metrics, Liberal Arts is a recurring trough, and the biggest meaningful shifts occur when moving from generative similarity metrics to discrete selection tasks where domain effects and model-to-model gaps widen.

The stacked bar charts illustrate the distribution of hateful responses across race, religion, gender, and disability categories in different languages, highlighting that model behavior varies more by language and attribute than by model family. Race outputs are the most volatile, revealing distinct biases where LLaMA and SeaLLM frequently target the Chinese ethnicity, while SEA-LION exhibits a strong bias toward Indians. Mistral alternates between targeting Chinese and Indians depending on language, while Qwen demonstrates better neutrality, but occasionally selects these ethnicities. Beyond these specific targets, the data shows that Chinese and Tamil prompts more often elicit direct race labels, whereas Singlish and Malay prompts cause several models to shift heavily into “not sure,” suggesting language-specific confidence or pattern-matching rather than consistent abstention. Religion shows the clearest sign of systematic uncertainty, with “not sure” and “invalid” frequently dominating in Chinese, Malay, and Tamil, and with occasional sharp spikes where a single religion category becomes disproportionately selected for a given model-language pair, indicating brittle heuristics or prompt-triggered defaults rather than stable inference. Gender is comparatively more decisive, but it still exhibits pronounced language effects, including noticeable increases in “invalid” or “not sure” for some models in Tamil and Malay, implying difficulty mapping gendered terms or higher safety filtering in those languages. Disability is the most consistently classified across languages, with “handicaps” and “mentally ill” taking large shares and relatively fewer abstentions, which is a notable shift from the other attributes and suggests the models are more willing to label disability-related content even when they hesitate on race or religion; the main anomaly here is that a few model-language combinations still flip toward “invalid,” hinting at uneven moderation or tokenization issues. Overall, the dominant pattern is inconsistent abstention where the same model can be highly categorical in one language and largely “not sure/invalid” in another, creating significant reliability risk for multilingual use and indicating that language choice can materially change both the certainty and the direction of sensitive classifications.

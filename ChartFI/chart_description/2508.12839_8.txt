The chart compares SLA violations under different lengths on ECT and ECW datasets, evaluating multiple forecasting models as the prediction length increases from 1-day to 3-day. Across three prediction lengths, HRS consistently yields the fewest SLA violations in both traffic and workload forecasting tasks. It achieves an average reduction in SLA violations of 61.8%, 66.4%, 72.9%, and 51.2% on ECT1, ECT2, ECW08, and ECW09, respectively. Beyond this standout model performance, the dominant trend is horizon sensitivity, which varies significantly between the dataset types. On ECT1 and ECT2, violations generally rise sharply with longer prediction lengths for most methods, indicating error accumulation over longer horizons. Nearly every model shows a clear step-up from 1-day to 2-day and an even larger jump at 3-day, creating a wide spread between the best and worst performers and signaling that these datasets are especially challenging for long-range forecasting. In contrast, the ECW08 and ECW09 datasets operate on a much smaller scale, and the horizon effect is weaker or even reversed. On ECW08, violations are relatively high at 1-day and 2-day but drop substantially at 3-day for all models, an unusual shift that may reflect a change in evaluation window difficulty or a regime where longer-horizon forecasts smooth volatility. ECW09 features the lowest overall counts but contains a highly visible anomaly where one model exhibits a pronounced spike at 1-day that is not mirrored by others, implying instability or a mismatch to short-horizon dynamics. Overall, while long-horizon forecasting drives SLA risk on ECT1 and ECT2, the ECW datasets show more dataset-specific quirks, making relative model robustness crucial across varying conditions.

This visualization presents a comparison across compositionality models, organized by rows, and embedding models, organized by columns, using Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) plots to illustrate cosine similarity distributions. The blue plots represent the KDEs of cosine similarity values between the predicted and ground truth embeddings, while the red plots serve as baseline distributions representing the KDEs of cosine similarities between embeddings of random pairs of compounds. For Google, Mistral, and OpenAI Large, most composition methods produce a clean, consistent split where the red baseline distributions cluster around the mid 0.5–0.65 range for Google and Mistral and near 0.15–0.30 for OpenAI Large, while the blue distributions concentrate significantly higher, indicating strong separability. Specifically, apart from the Multiplication model, which performs poorly across all embeddings, all models of compositionality show large separations between the blue and red plots for all embedding models other than BERT. While Google, Mistral, and OpenAI Large give fairly similar results across compositionality models, BERT does not perform well, exhibiting large overlaps between the blue and red distributions that suggest it is much less compositional than the other three embedding models. In the BERT column, both groups sit high in the 0.8–1.0 range, and the separation relies more on a right-shifted blue peak near 0.95–1.0 than on a clear gap, resulting in a reduced discriminative margin. The Multiplication model stands out as a major anomaly that does extremely poorly across all embeddings, with the blue distributions lower than the baselines in red; for Google and Mistral, it yields near-zero or slightly negative blue similarities, while for BERT, the effect is extreme with blue concentrated in a distinctly negative range while red stays very high near 0.9–1.0. Overall, the most stable and high-separation behavior appears in Ridge Regression and MLP, which push the blue mass closest to 1.0 and leave minimal overlap, whereas the multiplication method introduces a qualitatively different and potentially risky similarity structure where the predicted embeddings are less similar to the ground truth than random pairs.

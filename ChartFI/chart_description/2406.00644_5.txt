The chart compares clustering results across three text representations and three datasets. The top row shows the experimental outcomes for the breast dataset, followed by the second row showing the results for the thyroid dataset, and the third row showing the results for the liver dataset. Within each row, the first column represents the results obtained from the BOW embedding method, the second column represents the results obtained from the TF-IDF method, and the third column represents the results obtained from the S-Bert method. Each heatmap in this table displays clustering results, with the x-axis representing the dimensions of dimensionality reduction and the y-axis indicating different numbers of clusters. The values in each cell of the heatmap represent the silhouette coefficient scores, which reflect the performance of the clustering for each combination of dimension reduction and cluster numbers. Overall, the breast and liver datasets tend to reach higher peak silhouette coefficient scores than the thyroid dataset. For the breast dataset, S-Bert demonstrates the best performance, culminating around 0.81 at the highest number of clusters and maximum dimensions. BOW also shows a strong upward gradient as the number of clusters increases, rising from roughly the mid 0.4s to 0.8, and it remains relatively stable across dimension settings once the cluster count is high. TF-IDF is also strong but more sensitive to the dimension setting, peaking at 0.81 near the middle columns at a lower cluster count before softening slightly. Conversely, the conventional BOW model is notably effective on both the thyroid and liver datasets. On the thyroid dataset, all three representations are more compressed and yield lower scores, with BOW leading overall by reaching 0.75 at lower cluster counts before declining at higher cluster numbers. TF-IDF on the thyroid dataset is notably flat and weaker, hovering close to 0.48 to 0.57 with minimal gains, which stands out as an underperforming anomaly compared with the clearer gradients seen elsewhere. S-Bert sits between them, peaking in the low 0.7s at the lowest cluster count and showing a mild decline as the number of clusters increases. For the liver dataset, BOW shows the strongest lift with the number of clusters and achieves the highest absolute values in the entire figure, peaking around 0.85 and staying high across dimensions once the cluster parameter is large. TF-IDF is consistently solid in the mid-to-high 0.7s with modest sensitivity to dimensionality reduction, while S-Bert starts lower at the lowest cluster counts but climbs steadily to end near 0.8, indicating it can match TF-IDF at higher cluster settings but does not surpass BOW’s best case.

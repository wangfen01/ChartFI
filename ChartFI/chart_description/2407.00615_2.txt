This visualization illustrates the cross-task performance when transferring knowledge from node classification to four distinct downstream tasks across three sampling rates of 0.90%, 1.80%, and 3.60%, revealing that performance with condensed datasets was significantly lower compared to original datasets in all transferred tasks as indicated by the dashed lines marking whole-dataset benchmarks. For link prediction, accuracy remains largely stagnant in the low-to-mid 50% range for most strategies, but a notable performance jump occurs for the gradient matching methods GCond and SGDD, which reach the mid-to-high 60s while remaining well below the 82.0% whole-dataset reference, suggesting that link prediction is particularly data-hungry and difficult to recover under heavy subsampling. In node clustering, F1 scores vary widely from the high teens to the mid-50s, where method choice dominates over sampling rate; specifically, gradient matching methods like GCond demonstrated better transferability in downstream tasks by consistently reaching the top performance band, whereas others like DM collapse at certain sampling rates. Anomaly detection using structure exhibits a pronounced bimodal distribution where several strategies linger near chance-level ROC-AUC in the mid-0.4s, while a cluster of superior strategies including GCDM and SGDD reaches the mid-to-high 0.7s, though all remain substantially below the 0.93 benchmark. Conversely, anomaly detection using context shows more stable and generally higher ROC-AUC values that peak in the low-to-mid 0.7s and approach the 0.77 whole-dataset reference, particularly for SGDD at the 0.90% rate, reinforcing that context signals transfer more robustly under subsampling while structural anomaly cues are more brittle and highly dependent on the specific condensation strategy employed.

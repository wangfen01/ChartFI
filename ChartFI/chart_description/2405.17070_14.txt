The chart compares actual versus fitted electricity load across the late-March to early-May windows for 2015, 2016, and 2017, decomposing the fit into calendar effects, seasonal structure, temperature, and state-related components. On non-holidays, seasonal effects, including their interactions, are the primary driver of the load profile with a typical daily and weekly pattern, manifesting as a highly regular cycle with pronounced weekday peaks and weekend troughs. While this seasonal component explains most of the repeating amplitude, the impact of temperature is minimal with a maximal deviation of approximately 2 GW, and the mid-term states exhibit a negligible effect within the depicted timeframes, similarly limited to a maximal impact of approximately 2 GW. Consequently, the most material deviations from the baseline are concentrated in holiday windows where the load drops sharply below surrounding weeks. Moving holidays in early and mid-April drive multi-day reductions, but the fixed date holiday Labour Day reveals a notable inconsistency across the years; in 2015 and 2017 this holiday has a load-reducing effect of approx. 17 GW, whereas for the fixed date holiday Labour Day no effect is observed in 2016. Despite these sharp calendar-driven demand shocks, the fitted line tracks the actual series closely throughout the observed periods, indicating that the model successfully captures both the dominant recurring seasonal patterns and the specific magnitude of holiday disruptions.

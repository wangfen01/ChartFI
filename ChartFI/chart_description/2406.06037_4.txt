The figure illustrates the performance of each pre-training method after fine-tuning, in different distributions (ID, Near-OOD, Far-OOD) and adaptation scenarios (Offline BC, Online RL), utilizing normalized IQM scores to highlight shifts in control capabilities. In the ID setting, Offline BC scores span a wide range, with trajectory-centric methods achieving the highest values, and Online RL largely preserves this ordering; this tight relationship is evidenced by a strong correlation between performances in offline behavioral cloning and online reinforcement learning, with Pearson correlation coefficients of 0.85 in ID. Moving to Near-OOD, the distribution of scores compresses and the correlation drops to 0.61, suggesting that generalization noise and distribution shift weaken the predictability of online gains from offline scores. In Far-OOD environments, a distinct anomaly emerges where Offline BC values become much larger overall—reaching significantly higher magnitudes than in other settings—yet Online RL performance does not scale proportionally and remains clustered at lower values. This results in a moderate correlation of 0.63 and indicates that strong offline imitation under severe shift can overstate downstream online controllability, as the policy matches dataset behavior without acquiring robust, transferable control. Throughout these regimes, image-based pre-training methods like CURL and MAE consistently surpassed the Random baseline, which remains near the performance floor, thereby validating the efficacy of visual pre-training over random initialization despite the varying degrees of offline-to-online alignment observed across the distribution shifts.

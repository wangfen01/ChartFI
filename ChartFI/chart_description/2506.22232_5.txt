This visualization of political bias scores across various target questions maps model responses along a single axis ranging from liberal (values less than 0) to conservative (values greater than 0), with error bars indicating the standard error of the bias estimates. The data compares GPT-3.5 against the Llama 3.1 family across six policy topics, revealing that while GPT-3.5 seems to have a predominantly liberal bias, the models do not exhibit systematic biases toward either liberal or conservative attitudes, indicating that the bias scores may not generalize to political bias in general. The largest cross-model divergence occurs on wealth redistribution and the free market economy; on wealth redistribution, the Llama 3.1 70B and 8B Base variants lean clearly conservative while their Instruct counterparts move toward a more moderate conservative stance, contrasting sharply with the liberal position of GPT-3.5. A similar shift is visible regarding the free market economy, where Llama Base models lean conservative but the Instruct versions swing left, suggesting that instruction tuning materially alters the models' stances on market-oriented framing. Other topics like state security measures show more modest or near-neutral behavior for the Llama models, while environment and punishing criminals reveal a pronounced leftward lean across both Base and Instruct variants, with GPT-3.5 also maintaining a strong leftward tilt on these topics. Stay-at-home parenting serves as a notable exception to the trend of instruction tuning pulling models leftward, as Llama Base models lean conservative and Instruct models hover nearer to neutral, implying this topic may be more sensitive to underlying pretraining priors. Ultimately, the significant separation between GPT-3.5 and the Llama family across most topics highlights a pronounced liberal skew in the former, whereas the Llama 3.1 models exhibit highly topic-dependent biases that fluctuate in direction and magnitude.

The figure presents three co-occurrence matrices constructed between relationship-relationship (left), subject-relationship (middle), and relationship-object (right) respectively, where the matrices show the conditional probability that an element of the y-axis occurs when another element of the X-axis is happening. In the relationship-relationship panel, the data reveals that some related relationships will co-occur frequently, forming coherent relation families with limited cross-talk; for instance, when a “man swing bat”, the “man stand in dirt” is prone to appear at the same time. While tennis phrases align most strongly with each other, the representation separates sports-action contexts from general human-action contexts, though apparel relations like “man wear helmet” show moderate spillover into the “man” cluster without becoming dominant matches. Moving to the subject-relationship matrix, there is usually a strong co-occurrence between the subject and certain behaviors in vision; for example, when the subject is a chef, he is usually cooking something instead of driving a car. While this reflects intuitive cues—such as “on railway” aligning with “train”—anomalies like the strong association between “drive car” and “dog” suggest dataset bias or label noise where dogs co-occur with driving scenes more than expected relative to typical semantics. Finally, the relationship-object panel highlights a cooccurrence between the relationship and the object, featuring some high-frequency co-occurrence relationships, such as riding a bike. Here, object categories like “bike,” “horse,” and “motorbike” align strongly with “man ride,” whereas general items like “cellphone” and “laptop” show weaker, more diffuse associations, indicating that object-conditioned relations are generally cleaner and more separable than subject-conditioned relations.

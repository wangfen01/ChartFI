This heatmap illustrates the comparison of operational costs for integrated scheduling, where rows represent electricity load forecasting methods and columns denote wind forecasting methods. The entries display the additional costs compared to the best in percentage value, with darker cells indicating smaller penalties and zero representing the most economic situation. A critical analysis of the visual patterns reveals that the differences between each column were remarkable, which means the wind forecasting methods dominate in the integrated scheduling performance rather than the load forecasting methods. For instance, the HNL column is consistently low-cost, maintaining values between 0.00% and 4.66% regardless of the paired load model, whereas the columns for LSTM and MLP variants frequently incur higher penalties in the mid-teens. The most prominent anomaly is the Persistence column, which exhibits the highest costs across the board; when compared with the Persistence-based methods, the saved costs are more than 25% when utilizing the optimal HNL configuration. While the NL family columns show comparatively contained costs with generally low single-digit losses, the Persistence diagonal context culminates in the largest value on the chart at 25.71%, signaling a sharp performance separation. Overall, the data indicates that while load forecasting choice has a marginal effect, the selection of the wind forecasting model is the primary driver of economic efficiency, with HNL proving to be the most robust option.

The charts illustrate the breakdown of energy per cycle, latency per compute block, and area based on post-layout simulation results, decomposed into contributions from the encoder, decoder, controller, RCA, and other logic for two decoder sizes, Ndec=4 and Ndec=16. Energy consumption is overwhelmingly dominated by the decoder, which includes the SRAM array and CSA, accounting for over 94% of consumption in both configurations as it rises from 94.2% at Ndec=4 to 97.7% at Ndec=16. This shift causes total energy to increase sharply from 13.8 pJ to 53.1 pJ, indicating that scaling decoder size drives a disproportionate energy penalty while the relative proportion of non-decoder components decreases. Area shifts similarly toward the decoder as Ndec grows, with the decoder accounting for a significant proportion of 50% to 80% of the area; specifically, the decoder share increases from 56.9% to 82.9% as total area expands from 0.076 mm² to 0.20 mm², while the share of "others" drops from 34.5% to 13.8%. In contrast, latency is primarily governed by the encoder, which constitutes the largest proportion of over 40% to 70% across both best and worst cases. Total latency increases from 16.1 ns to 30.4 ns at Ndec=4 and from 17.8 ns to 32.1 ns at Ndec=16, with the worst-case shift characterized by a higher encoder share of up to 71.3% and reduced relative shares for the decoder and controller. While the encoder remains the dominant latency contributor, the decoder’s latency increased slightly due to the increased delay in the RCD as Ndec scaled. Ultimately, while decoder scaling is the primary driver of energy and area growth, latency remains more sensitive to encoder-side critical paths and operating conditions, creating a clear optimization tension between reducing energy and area through decoder improvements versus mitigating worst-case latency through encoder refinements.

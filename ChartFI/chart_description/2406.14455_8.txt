This visualization illustrates the contribution weight of each modality for classification tasks in 10-fold stratified cross-validation on ABIDE and ADHD-200 datasets, utilizing multiple donut charts to depict fold-specific distributions and summary bar charts to aggregate overall importance. The average contribution weights on two datasets using MM-GTUNets reveal that rs-fMRI data contributes the most to predictions, consistently appearing as the dominant driver with a contribution score near 0.6 across the aggregate summaries. While the primary signal is heavily imaging-led, the remaining influence is distributed among demographic and acquisition factors. Among non-imaging data, the contribution scores for gender, age, and site are relatively balanced, yet distinct patterns emerge within this secondary tier. Specifically, gender has a greater influence on prediction results, often appearing as the second-largest sector in the donut charts and slightly surpassing other non-imaging variables in the bar charts, while age demonstrates a more consistent impact with less fluctuation across the varying experimental conditions. This hierarchy indicates that while model behavior is primarily driven by rs-fMRI features rather than confounds, the variability among site, gender, and age across the folds suggests a nuanced sensitivity to cohort composition, where the shifting balance of non-imaging factors highlights dataset-specific heterogeneity without disrupting the overarching reliance on functional imaging data.

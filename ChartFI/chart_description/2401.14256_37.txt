This chart compares the performance of all the simulated architectures in each of the ID and OOD sets across four key metrics: Accuracy, F1-score, Bray–Curtis dissimilarity, and normalized mean absolute error (NMAE), with distinct markers differentiating overall performance from performance restricted to OOD cells. Across the eight model families—Mobile, EffB2, EffB7, Dense, DeiT, ViT, Swin, and BEiT—performance is strongest on ID train and ID test but degrades as the evaluation shifts into OOD regimes, while distributional and error-based metrics rise in tandem with that degradation. Accuracy and F1-score start near ceiling on ID train and remain high on ID test, but both drop noticeably once moving to OOD, with the steepest declines occurring in the mid-to-late OOD conditions where many models cluster around the low-to-mid 0.7s for accuracy and roughly the 0.35–0.7 range for F1. The OOD-cells-only points generally sit below their corresponding overall OOD points, indicating that the hardest subset is consistently the OOD cell population rather than the full mixture, and notably, there is no single architecture that performs best in each of the OOD cells. A significant shift appears at the far right of the accuracy panel where scores rebound for several models in the OOD10 condition, suggesting this scenario is less harmful for classification than preceding ones, even though other metrics do not uniformly improve. Bray–Curtis dissimilarity is near zero on ID train and increases sharply under OOD, peaking in the middle OOD settings where many models reach their highest dissimilarities, consistent with a strong distribution shift in predicted compositions. NMAE follows a similar pattern but with an even more pronounced escalation in the hardest OOD cases; errors remain modest through early OOD conditions then jump substantially in the final OOD settings, particularly for EffB7, which exhibits a large spike and wide uncertainty signaling instability or sensitivity to that shift. Across all panels, the alignment between lower F1/accuracy and higher Bray–Curtis/NMAE indicates that the main failure mode under OOD is not just misclassification but a broader mismatch in predicted distributions, while the widening error bars in later OOD conditions highlight increasing variability across runs as the shift intensifies.

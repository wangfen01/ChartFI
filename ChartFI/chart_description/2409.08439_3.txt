These charts present an evaluation of the prediction performance of the various models vs. the dimension of their latent representation n_z and the number of trainable parameters of the dynamics model, respectively, where the error bar denotes the standard deviation. Across both panels, RMSE generally drops quickly from higher initial values and then either stabilizes near a low floor around 0.09–0.11 or deteriorates as model complexity increases, depending on the method. In the latent-dimension view, the constraint-based variants CON-S and CON-M show the most robust performance: RMSE falls steeply by about n_z 4–6 and remains near the minimum. Crucially, these CON models have a much-improved consistency and smaller variance w.r.t. the baseline methods when the latent dimensionality is increased, evidenced by their tight error bars compared to the volatile spreads seen in the baselines. By contrast, the neural baselines are far more sensitive; RNN improves early but trends upward and becomes volatile as n_z grows, while MECH-NODE and MECH-NODE-S show clear degradation at moderate-to-large n_z, with RMSE rising toward roughly 0.35–0.40. CFA-CON tracks a similar low plateau to the other constrained models but exhibits a notable late spike near the highest n_z values. The parameter-count view reinforces this divergence, where CON-S and CON-M achieve low RMSE with relatively small models and maintain a flat, stable performance band. Conversely, MECH-NODE shows a sharp transition from low error at smaller sizes to a high-RMSE regime around the mid-parameter range, and CFA-CON, while generally strong, displays a pronounced spike in the high parameter region. Overall, the constrained approaches dominate in both accuracy and stability, whereas increasing complexity tends to destabilize the unconstrained and mechanistic NODE variants beyond a narrow optimal range.

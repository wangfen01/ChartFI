This set of six bubble plots visualises the relationship between activity and abuse for public figures across different domains, plotting weekly average tweets posted against weekly average abusive mentions on a logarithmic scale. To contextualize the volume of engagement, the size of markers represents total tweets received by the public figure, and the most abused public figure in each domain is marked with a solid outline. While weekly average abusive mentions generally rise as posting activity increases, the relationship is highly dispersed and strongly shaped by role and gender. MPs sit in a significantly higher-abuse regime than other groups; in terms of ratios of abuse to activity, only considering public figures receiving at least 1 abusive tweet per week on average, we see 28 male and 5 female MPs receiving at least 100 abusive tweets for every tweet they write per week. This intensity contrasts sharply with that of journalists, where despite generally higher posting volumes, only 9 men and 4 women reach that saturation of abuse, and footballers, where the counts are 3 and 0 respectively. Men generally occupy a wider activity range and include the most extreme outliers, particularly among MPs where very high posting coincides with the largest abuse volumes, suggesting that visibility amplifies exposure more than posting rate alone. Women’s panels show fewer accounts and a tighter activity range, yet the pattern remains that moderate-to-high posting can attract disproportionately high abuse. Overall, the data indicates that while increased activity correlates with increased abuse, the transition to sustained moderate activity reveals that role—specifically the high-visibility nature of political office—drives the largest deviations in abuse intensity rather than a smooth, linear effect of tweeting more.

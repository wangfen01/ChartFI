The chart presents representative APBD-related studies published between 2013 and 2025, mapping them by spatial resolution on the vertical axis from sub-meter to 30 m against publication year on the horizontal axis to illustrate evolving methodological choices, temporal designs, sensing modes, and study area scales. Within this visual framework, triangles, circles, and rectangles denote traditional image processing-based, traditional machine learning-based, and deep learning-based APBD methods, respectively, and the larger the size, the larger the study area. The left side of the distribution is relatively sparse, as early work from 2013 to 2016 primarily clusters at coarser resolutions around 10 to 30 m, dominated by traditional image processing and classical machine learning approaches. From roughly 2018 onward, there is a clear expansion in methodological diversity and resolution coverage, whereas the right side, particularly the bottom-right corner, is densely populated. Since 2019, the number of APBD studies using Very High-Resolution remote sensing imagery has increased exponentially, with deep learning becoming the prominent method for these detailed mapping tasks. Although VHR imagery below 0.5 m in the lower portion of the chart enables high-accuracy APBD, it remains constrained to relatively small areas due to high storage and processing costs. Conversely, large-scale studies typically employ imagery with spatial resolution above 10 m, often incorporating multitemporal datasets to balance accuracy, coverage, and storage demands. This is visually reinforced by the largest study extents appearing almost exclusively at the 10 to 30 m resolutions, even in the most recent years, implying that coarse-resolution products remain essential for broad coverage. Multi-temporal designs are present throughout but become increasingly common in later years alongside deep learning, indicating a growing emphasis on temporal dynamics, while passive remote sensing dominates the overall landscape with active sensing appearing only intermittently. Overall, the dominant pattern is a methodological modernization toward deep learning and a migration toward finer spatial detail after 2020, without fully displacing the coarse-resolution, large-area monitoring required for comprehensive analysis.

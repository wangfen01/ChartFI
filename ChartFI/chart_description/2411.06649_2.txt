The hourly power consumption over a 24-hour period illustrates the original load profile alongside six False Data Injection (FDI) scenarios that simulate the tampering behaviors of electricity thieves who tend to reduce the quantity of their billed electricity. The original series remains low and stable overnight before rising sharply between hours 9 and 10 to a pronounced daytime peak of approximately 1.7 kWh, followed by an elevated but fluctuating plateau that eventually returns to the baseline around hour 18. Across the tampered load profiles, an FDI type either keeps the features and fluctuations of the original curve or creates new patterns, with the most significant divergences occurring during high-demand hours where the original signal has the most structure and amplitude. FDI4 and FDI2 demonstrate the clearest structural distortions: FDI4 creates an abrupt step up to a high level around hour 10 before collapsing to zero through much of the midday period, while FDI2 maintains an extended, nearly flat artificial plateau that suppresses the natural peak-and-decline shape. FDI1 and FDI5 broadly follow the daytime rise but dampen and fragment the peak into lower, noisier oscillations with intermittent spikes, whereas FDI3 largely underreports consumption by staying near zero for long stretches and only briefly lifting during the peak window. FDI6 remains comparatively low and smooth, muting the peak and shifting the profile toward a flatter, reduced daytime demand. These anomalous behaviors materially alter peak detection and energy accounting, as the injected variability and near-zero suppression effectively mask the true consumption levels of the original profile.

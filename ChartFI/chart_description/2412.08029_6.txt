The scatter plots illustrate the correlation between ground truth JOD and estimation made by the most widely used metrics for NVS—specifically PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS—alongside the proposed NeRF-NQA across the Fieldwork, LLFF, and Lab datasets. In these visualizations, distinct symbols and colors denote various scenes, while a red line represents the ideal prediction trajectory where the metric estimation equals the ground truth. Upon scrutinizing the results pertaining to the Fieldwork dataset, it becomes evident that conventional methods like PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS tend to produce biased estimations. These metrics form stepped clusters with visible vertical spread rather than a continuous gradient, suggesting limited sensitivity and inconsistent scaling across content types. In contrast, NeRF-NQA effectively mitigates such biases across all scenes, maintaining a near-linear progression that aligns tightly with the diagonal reference. Further analysis of the LLFF and Lab datasets reveals that NeRF-NQA’s estimations are remarkably more concentrated and closely aligned with the red line compared to other methods, which often exhibit range compression, weak calibration, or category-dependent clustering. While traditional metrics and LPIPS show broader scatter and scene-specific offsets, NeRF-NQA not only estimates with reduced bias but also with lower variance and a significantly diminished presence of outliers across all NVS scenes, demonstrating superior stability and cross-dataset generalization.

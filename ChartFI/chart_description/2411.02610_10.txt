The set of eight correlograms displays significant correlation values for all models across sentences and languages, utilizing a color scale where red indicates positive correlation and blue negative, with darker shades representing higher values. Overall, high correlations were found among all models, though the intensity of this agreement varies by evaluation signal. Correlations are particularly high for the expected congruent variants involving NC_Syn as reflected by the darker red shades in P_Syn, A_Syn|WordsSyn, and Sim_R|Syn. In these synonym-focused views, the data reveals high correlations with the static models (Word2Vec, GloVe, ELMo) as well as the newer architectures, suggesting that semantic similarity signals produce a shared, architecture-agnostic ordering. Within this high-agreement landscape, the tightest alignment repeatedly appears among the transformer family, especially BERT ML and DistilBERT ML, which show near-ceiling similarity. In contrast to the robust alignment in semantic tasks, the correlation structure becomes sparse and uneven in P_Comp and P_Rand. P_Comp shows generally weak agreement across most pairs, while P_Rand introduces the clearest anomalies, including several negative correlations where LLaMA2 diverges from the transformer cluster. Word-level synonym settings such as Sim_R|WordsSyn sit between these extremes, showing moderate agreement where static embeddings tend to correlate more with each other than with the transformer-based sentence models. Ultimately, while semantic signals drive strong coherence across the board, compositionality and randomness expose architectural differences and instability.

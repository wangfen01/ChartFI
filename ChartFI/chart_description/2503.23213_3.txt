This hazard-product co-occurrence heatmap compares incident counts by hazard classification across product categories, revealing a highly uneven distribution where a few high-intensity pairings dominate the overall volume. Examining hazard classification, fire, falling, and choking emerged as the most prevalent hazards, with distinct clusters driving these numbers. Focusing on fire, the strongest correlations come from electrical and home appliances; specifically, Home Appliances peaks at 623 incidents, standing out as the single largest cell, while the Electrical category follows with 403, reinforcing a strong association between these product types and thermal risks. In the realm of child safety, Choking is overwhelmingly concentrated in Toys/Children at 564, with relatively low counts elsewhere. Furthermore, cross-referencing with actual recall data shows a substantial number of poisoning-related recalls, particularly within the children’s toys category, which registers a significant cluster of 318 incidents. Crash hazards remain largely isolated to Transportation at 339, implying that these risks are driven by a narrow set of product types rather than being broadly distributed. Beyond these extremes, secondary patterns indicate broader exposure, particularly regarding Falling, which is elevated across Furniture at 268 and Sports/Recreation at 234, with additional volume in Transportation and Baby Products. Other notable distributions include Burns, which show counts of 164 in Home Appliances and 145 in Clothing/Accessories, and Cuts, which rise meaningfully in Tools/Hardware at 119. Overall, the data suggest that prevention and monitoring efforts will likely be most effective when targeted at these specific, high-intensity hazard–category intersections.

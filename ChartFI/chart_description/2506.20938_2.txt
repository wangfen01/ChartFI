The six heatmaps present build@1 and pass@1 results for three programming model translation pairs—CUDA to OpenMP Offload, CUDA to Kokkos, and OpenMP Threads to OpenMP Offload—split by evaluation type (code-only vs. overall) and prompting style, including non-agentic, top-down agentic, and a specialized SWE-agent condition for the CUDA to Kokkos task. Within each row of subfigures, the left subfigure indicates build@1 scores, representing the likelihood of generating a compilable translation given one attempt, while the right subfigure indicates pass@1, the likelihood of generating a translation that passes correctness tests given one attempt. The dominant pattern across all tasks is that build success is generally much higher than pass success, and code-only scores consistently exceed overall scores, indicating that even when code compiles, end-to-end correctness and integration requirements sharply reduce final pass rates. For CUDA to OpenMP Offload, non-agentic prompting produces very strong build@1 on the nanoXOR family with values near saturation in code-only, while top-down agentic remains high but more uneven; however, pass@1 for this task is materially lower, with the best cells clustering around nanoXOR rows for a subset of models, suggesting that semantic correctness is the primary bottleneck rather than compilation. A notable anomaly is the near-universal zeros on SimpleMOC-kernel, XSBench, and llm.c across both build and pass, implying these larger or more complex codebases are not being handled successfully regardless of prompting. For CUDA to Kokkos, build@1 under non-agentic is strong for microXORh and microXOR, while pass@1 is sparse and low overall, with top-down agentic largely near zero; the SWE-agent condition shows limited lift with small non-zero build values but pass rates remaining essentially zero, reinforcing that agent tooling improves compilation marginally without translating into functional correctness. In contrast, the OpenMP Threads to OpenMP Offload task is the most "buildable," with build@1 broadly high across nanoXOR and microXOR variants for both prompting styles. Correctness metrics for OpenMP Threads to OpenMP Offload tasks show improvement relative to the CUDA-to-Kokkos case, reaching high pass@1 values for nanoXOR under top-down agentic in code-only, yet scores still drop in the overall category, confirming that even within the same ecosystem, full-program constraints remain a meaningful hurdle. Ultimately, the charts suggest task complexity and benchmark scale dominate outcomes, with prompting style providing only incremental gains in select small kernels while failing to overcome the steep pass-rate cliff on larger benchmarks and more invasive portability targets.

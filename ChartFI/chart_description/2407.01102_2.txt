The heatmap compares all metrics against GPT-4-as-a-judge and measures correlation averaged over samples that vary by response length—short-form NQ, medium-form TruthfulQA and Wow, and long-form ELI5—along with an overall average. LLMeval on average achieves the highest correlation with GPT-4, securing an average correlation of 0.53 and outperforming GPT-3.5Turbo, which follows with an average of 0.48. While LLMeval leads with strong correlations on NQ (0.69) and TruthfulQA (0.65), both learned evaluators experience a noticeable drop in performance on the longer, more open-ended Wow and ELI5 tasks, though they remain significantly more robust than traditional methods. In contrast, traditional lexical and exact-match style metrics are highly sensitive to task type; Match appears relatively reasonable on the short-form NQ dataset with a correlation of 0.54 but collapses on the more generative Wow and ELI5 settings, approaching zero. Notably, Exact Match (EM) remains consistently low across all datasets, including NQ (0.035), contradicting the assumption that it performs reasonably on short-form tasks. A clear pattern emerges where overlap-based metrics such as F1, Rouge-L, Precision, and Recall decline sharply as responses become longer and more generative, indicating they fail to capture semantic quality when wording diverges, whereas model-based evaluators retain much better stability.

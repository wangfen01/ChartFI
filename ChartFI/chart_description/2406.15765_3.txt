This heatmap visualizes the accuracy improvement in the MMLU dataset achieved by reducing the attention score of attention sinks in the middle of input sequences for each individual head separately, spanning Head IDs 1 to 32 on the x-axis and Layer IDs 3 to 31 on the y-axis. The color gradient, where blue indicates negative values and orange indicates positive values (scaled by 0.1), reveals that in more than 76.8% of cases, the LLM after attention calibration can achieve better accuracy compared to the vanilla inference baseline. However, the distribution of these improvements is not uniform; the chart demonstrates that not all heads can benefit from the calibration, a trend most visible in the upper portion of the heatmap. Layer 3 exhibits the widest spread and strongest magnitudes, characterized by scattered hotspots of improvement alongside pronounced negative pockets represented by deep blue cells. These specific negative values indicate that calibrating certain heads can result in an accuracy drop as significant as 0.39%, confirming that extreme sensitivities and potential performance penalties are largely concentrated in the earliest displayed layers. As the view moves downward through the layers, this high variance compresses quickly, with values becoming more uniformly light orange and centered near zero. This shift implies that while the early layers contain a small subset of heads that drive significant signal volatility—both positive and negative—the mid-to-lower layers behave more consistently, contributing to the overall trend of mild accuracy gains without the drastic fluctuations seen at the model's shallow depth.

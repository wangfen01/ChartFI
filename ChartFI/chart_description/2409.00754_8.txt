These six heatmaps illustrate the spatiotemporal distribution of traffic load, where the x-axis and y-axis indicate the time slot during a span of the simulation with 5 minutes and the ID number of 16 cutting edges, respectively, and the color darkness instead means the traffic volumes on the associated cutting edge within the time slot. Traffic concentration and evolution vary sharply by method, with the Random and SP approaches representing the most extreme cases of imbalance; these methods tend to plan too many vehicles towards two cutting edges (E3F3 and F3E3), causing near-saturation levels while the remaining network stays consistently low. While SPFR shows a slight improvement in spreading mid-level load and could re-route some traffic towards other cutting edges such as I4I5, it still exhibits clear hotspots. The Q-Routing and AN methods introduce stronger temporal dynamics but fail to achieve balance, as such re-routed traffics are unfortunately redirected to the same cutting edges, specifically creating heavy congestion on segment D4D5 and F6E6 in the Q-Routing scenario and segment F8E8 in the AN scenario. In distinct contrast to these bottlenecks, asyn-MARL demonstrates a rather even distribution of traffic volumes on the 16 cutting edges across the entire time slots. This method produces the most balanced pattern overall, with fewer near-saturation cells and mid-range values spread across multiple segments, indicating superior load sharing and reduced reliance on single critical links compared to the other strategies.

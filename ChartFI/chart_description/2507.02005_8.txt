The Pearson correlation matrix displays the relationships between continuous variables, revealing two dominant structures: a tightly coupled material-strength cluster and a geometry cluster. The data confirms intuitive relationships, such as high collinearity between yield and tensile strengths; specifically, strong correlations are observed among material strength properties, where base material R_eH and R_m exhibit a correlation of r ≈ 0.95, and filler strength measures track the base material closely, with R_{m,filler} and R_{eH,filler} also strongly aligned (r ≈ 0.95). In contrast to these positive associations, f_T is almost perfectly negatively correlated with the filler strength variables (r ≈ -1.00 with R_{eH,filler}), indicating it is far more sensitive to filler metrics than to base material properties. Interestingly, only moderate correlation is observed between Δσ_{c,50%} and other parameters; this variable rises with f_T (r ≈ 0.30) and shows a positive association with Δσ_I (r ≈ 0.41) and N_i (r ≈ 0.29), while tending to be lower for larger geometric measures. Furthermore, Δσ_I demonstrates a moderate inverse relationship with geometric variables like the ratio R (r ≈ -0.44) and a weak negative correlation with N_i (r ≈ -0.31), consistent with the expectation that higher stress ranges are associated with fewer cycles.

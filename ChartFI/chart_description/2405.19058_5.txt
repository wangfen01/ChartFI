This chart illustrates the adjusted genetic correlations among health, socioeconomic, and lifestyle variables in the lower-left panel, contrasted with the estimation bias in the upper-right panel, which represents the difference between the absolute value of the genetic correlation before and after adjustment. In the lower triangle, the heatmap ranges from strong negative correlations in blue to strong positive correlations in red, with asterisks highlighting significance levels. Overall, body-size measures cluster tightly, where BMI is strongly and positively related to waist circumference (WC) and hip circumference (HC), and these circumferences are themselves very strongly aligned to indicate a coherent adiposity dimension. While height is positively aligned with WC and HC, suggesting larger frame sizes coexist with larger circumferences, it moves in the opposite direction of BMI. Socioeconomic variables also form a clear block, with education, employment, and income positively related to one another while showing generally negative relationships with smoking behaviors and adiposity measures. Notably, for phenotypes significantly correlated with participation, absolute values of all the unadjusted genetic correlation estimates appeared to have underestimation bias, though no estimate switched signs with adjustment. The upper-right panel uses bubble size to correspond to the magnitude of the absolute difference, where larger, darker green circles indicate stronger underestimation and tan circles indicate overestimation, with asterisks marking significant differences at p < 0.05. The most prominent underestimation bias is concentrated in links involving education, employment, income, and current smoking, with a particularly strong underestimation around employment and current smoking. Specifically, the adjusted genetic correlation estimates between WC and education, HC and education, WC and income, and HC and income were significantly different from the unadjusted estimates. Yellow stars highlight cases where significance changed after adjustment, such as the relationship between height and employment or participation and current smoking, signaling that these associations are materially distorted by selection effects or confounding unless properly controlled. Physical activity indicators further reflect this complexity, as a faster walk pace is negatively associated with BMI and circumferences but positively associated with education and income, while walk time shows weaker, mixed relationships, including a noticeable positive association with alcohol.

This visualization illustrates the relative gain (in %) of various decoding strategies compared to the average performance of all other strategies across six hierarchy-aware metrics—hF1, hF2, hF0.5, Wu-Palmer Similarity, Zhao Similarity, and Tree Distance Loss—evaluated on multiple model architectures and three distinct training regimes. Each data point represents a specific model-metric combination on a test set, with symbols distinguishing between models pretrained on ImageNet (▲), fine-tuned on ImageNet-H (★), and fine-tuned on iNat19-H (●), while boxplots summarize the distribution of these relative gains for each strategy. A clear takeaway is the consistent superiority of optimal decoding algorithms across all evaluation metrics, demonstrating that regardless of the dataset, model, or training algorithm used, applying the optimal decoding strategy is always advantageous. While the optimal method consistently achieves the highest positive gains relative to the baseline, alternative strategies exhibit varying degrees of degradation or instability. Simple heuristics like Majority and Top-Down tend to produce the largest and most consistent performance drops, particularly in semantic-structure metrics. In contrast, mid-complexity methods such as Argmax, Jain et al. (2023), and Karthik et al. (2021) cluster closer to the baseline across most metrics, suggesting smaller, more stable trade-offs, though they rarely match the optimal performance. The F-score panels reveal more nuanced behavior, where several non-optimal methods hover near zero on hF1 and hF2, but dispersion increases significantly for hF0.5, where sensitivity to precision-weighted evaluation causes some strategies to swing notably negative. The similarity metrics and Tree Distance Loss provide the most decisive separation, highlighting that label accuracy alone can mask structural errors, as methods near-neutral on F-scores often perform significantly worse on hierarchical distance. Across all panels, while the general ranking of methods remains stable, models fine-tuned on ImageNet-H and iNat19-H often display wider scatter and more outliers than pretrained models, suggesting that fine-tuning can amplify the specific gains or failures of a decoding rule rather than providing a uniform improvement in robustness.

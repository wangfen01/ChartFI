This chart compares five models across four answer options to analyze how base probability mass distribution relates to performance measures. Using the control prompts with the cloze test pattern, we find that all models tested show a strong intrinsic bias for answer choice A over all other choice labels, regardless of position. While this ordering bias is universal, the specific distributions vary by model; both LLaMa models and SOLAR show a slight preference for A, but are largely consistent across datasets, whereas Mistral-7B exhibits a particularly pronounced concentration. The results of the accuracy evaluation are shown in the middle two rows, where each dot represents the accuracy on the label-split subset of the data for a single subject. These panels reveal that cloze accuracy tends to be higher and more tightly clustered than counterfactual accuracy, which is notably lower, more dispersed, and frequently reaches near-floor outcomes, indicating that counterfactual questions are substantially harder and less stable. The bottom correlation panel highlights a critical divergence: cloze accuracy generally aligns positively with the model’s baseline answer probabilities—particularly for Mistral-7B and SOLAR-10-7B, where the relationship is very strong—suggesting these models’ cloze performance is heavily influenced by their prior answer-order preference. In contrast, counterfactual accuracy displays weaker, mixed, or negative correlations, implying that success on counterfactuals depends less on these priors and requires different reasoning signals.

The four heatmaps illustrate four data distributions based on the feature of coding style across 40 clients, visualizing how coding-style counts shift from Independent and Identically Distributed (IID) to increasingly non-IID settings. In the IID scenario, the data distributions across clients tend to be identical, characterized by activity that is broadly spread such that specific styles maintain a consistent presence across the entire client population. As the degree of heterogeneity increases, the distributions become more concentrated; the mild non-IID setting displays a patchier pattern with emerging localized hotspots, while the medium non-IID panel reveals a sharper transition toward sparsity where most clients exhibit near-zero counts for most styles and only a few isolated client–style pairs carry very large values. Ultimately, in the extreme non-IID scenario, the pattern resolves into a systematic diagonal structure where each client possesses only a single cluster of data. This progression highlights a key shift from diffuse, shared representation to a strict one-to-one mapping, demonstrating that as heterogeneity peaks, each client becomes dominated by a single coding style with minimal overlap across the population.

This heatmap visualization displays rejection rates across adversarial categories for a diverse set of large language models, spanning both open-source and closed-source models, where each cell represents the rejection rate for a specific model-category pair and darker shades correspond to higher rejection rates. Performance is highly model-dependent, with the strongest results clustering around Claude 3.5 Sonnet, which repeatedly posts mid-to-high scores across most families, while several smaller or code-focused models show frequent low single digits and occasional zeros, indicating inconsistent coverage rather than uniformly weak performance. Across malware types, models especially struggle in more rare categories, e.g., polymorphic virus, logic bomb, etc., while they perform significantly better on more common categories such as APT, Worm, RAT, Backdoor, and Rootkit, which tend to elicit higher scores across the better general-purpose models. Notable peaks include Worm reaching the low 80s and RAT/Backdoor often landing in the 60–70 range for top performers, whereas the sharp performance cliff for some models on specific categories like Polymorphic Virus—where otherwise moderate models collapse to near-zero—suggests these tasks require more robust reasoning or richer security priors than what smaller or specialized models provide. Ultimately, the average column on the far right shows a relatively tight band in the high teens to low 30s, implying that gains come less from incremental improvements everywhere and more from avoiding catastrophic failures on a few difficult malware classes, as models struggle across most of the categories despite these variations.

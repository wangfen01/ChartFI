This heatmap illustrates the correlations between LLM-predicted products and hazard recalls, revealing a highly uneven distribution where specific hazard–product pairings dominate the overall risk landscape. The most pronounced concentrations appear in the TOYS_CHILDREN category for choking incidents, which at 1,058 cases represents the single highest value in the dataset, and in HOME_APPLIANCES for fire and burn hazards, which show 850 and 760 incidents respectively. These sharp peaks indicate that hazard exposure is strongly product-dependent, with choking being overwhelmingly tied to children’s toys and fire-related risks clustering heavily around home appliances and electrical products. Transportation also stands out as a high-risk category, showing the strongest signals for crash and impact incidents, while falling hazards are broadly present but spike notably in transportation and furniture. Distinct hazard profiles emerge across the grid, such as baby products skewing toward choking and falling, and sports/recreation showing high impact and secondary falling presence. A notable anomaly in the data is that the model rarely predicts poisoning hazards for products such as children’s toys, with poisoning generally showing low counts across all categories; this suggests that certain hazards, like poisoning, may not be visually apparent and thus are underrepresented in LLM predictions. The extreme concentration of choking in toys and the high crash and impact levels in transportation imply that targeted safety interventions in these specific areas would likely yield the most significant reductions in predicted incidents.

The figure showcases the alignment metric per RM (bar), the average ranking across all RMs per demographic group (top), and the detailed ranks per RM per demographic group (panel), illustrating how seven models perform across age, education, ethnicity, gender, income, and political party. A central trend reveals that demographic groups that are better represented receive lower ranks (darker circles) and higher alignment values (larger circles) than groups that are poorly represented. While the absolute alignment (size) appears to be model dependent, the relative alignment (hue) is fairly consistent between different demographic groups across RMs, meaning every demographic group obtains a similar rank across all models. Specifically, alignment tends to be higher for older respondents, with the 65+ group generally showing the strongest scores, and follows a clear gradient in education and income where postgraduate attainment and earnings over $100,000 are associated with higher alignment. Political party also displays a sharp contrast, with Democrats and Independents generally ranking higher than Republicans, whereas gender remains the most stable dimension with minimal deviation between female and male scores. Ethnicity exhibits mild but recurring patterns where Asian and Black groups often appear slightly higher than White and Hispanic groups. Despite the overall stability of these patterns, the Ultra model presents a notable exception, showing an inverted education trend with stronger alignment at lower education levels and weaker at higher education, signaling a distinctive calibration difference compared to the dominant socioeconomic and political segmentation observed in the other models.

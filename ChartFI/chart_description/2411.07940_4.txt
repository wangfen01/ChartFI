Across five datasets covering two subtypes of covariate shifts—subpopulation shift and acquisition shift—the chart compares how reliably different shift detectors identify distribution changes as the shift magnitude increases and as more test samples are available. For each combination of test set size and type of shift, the detector with the highest detection rate, as well as all detectors not significantly different from the best, are denoted with an asterisk. A primary observation is that for these shifts, most feature-based detectors perform substantially better than output-based detectors. Output-based shift detection fails to detect gender shifts on radiography datasets, with less than 5% of shifts detected, and performs substantially worse than feature-based tests on acquisition shifts across all levels of shifts and datasets; for example, on EMBED with 250 test exams, the output-based shift detector only detects 25% of acquisition shifts, whereas feature-based detectors detect at least 80% of shifts, except for the random encoder. However, the effectiveness of feature-based dataset shift detectors is highly dependent on the choice of the encoder used to extract the features. The task model, for example, underperforms for gender shift detection on chest radiography datasets, and so does the encoder trained in a supervised manner on ImageNet data, particularly visible for milder shifts and smaller test set sizes. Notably, both supervised encoders fail to detect the shift from 44% to 25% of females in the RSNA dataset, even with large test sizes. Overall, detected shift rises sharply with both stronger shifts and larger test sets, with a notable pattern being the convergence of methods at high sample sizes and high shift magnitudes where performance saturates near 100%, while the main differentiation between detectors occurs in the low-data, low-to-moderate shift regime.

These box plots compare efficiency performance metrics—Path Irregularity Index (PI), Distance to Goal (D2G), and Total Acceleration—across five methods using two different human models, interactive (IBR Human) and non-interactive (Optimal Control Human), with robot and human distributions shown side by side. Across all metrics, the Ours approach has a lower and more equitably distributed value between the human and robot, maintaining tight spreads and low central values that suggest smoother, more consistent trajectories and reliable goal attainment. In contrast, both SFM and HJ methods result in a large imbalance in all efficiency metrics; HJ stands out as particularly inefficient with markedly higher D2G medians and wide dispersion, indicating it often fails to approach the goal as closely as other methods. While OC, in general, performs similarly to vIBR and Ours, it experiences large fluctuations and pronounced variability, especially in PI, where extreme outliers for the human series indicate occasional highly irregular paths. Similarly, vIBR performs similarly to Ours, but Ours either has a lower variance, is more equitable, or for roughly the same human performance, the robot does better, which is especially notable in ACC, where Ours remains comparatively controlled and moderate. This trend of balanced performance for Ours persists across both human model conditions, whereas other methods like OC and vIBR exhibit higher and more variable accelerations or significant performance risks through occasional spikes in irregularity and consistently large goal distances.

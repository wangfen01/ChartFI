The combined spider graph illustrates model accuracy distribution across cultural attributes on the left and Indian states and Union Territories on the right, revealing a consistent hierarchy where a small group of top-performing models maintains stable performance near the outer ring while others cluster near the center. In the attributes chart, models exhibiting broad and uniform radial coverage signal a robust alignment between visual and linguistic modalities, likely resulting from exposure to diverse, multimodal training data. Their smooth contours reflect an ability to generalize across both concrete cultural elements, such as attire, cuisine, and festivals, and more nuanced attributes like language or heritage. Despite this general strength, even leading models show visible dips around medicine and nightlife, suggesting these categories are less consistently learned. In contrast, models with jagged or constricted profiles reveal gaps in cultural grounding, particularly with abstract or context-dependent concepts like religion, nightlife, or medicine, which demand deeper socio-cultural and inferential reasoning; the lowest-performing group remains compressed near the 0.1–0.3 range, indicating limited competence regardless of the topic. Moving to the state-level analysis, variability increases significantly for nearly every model, characterized by sharper peaks and troughs than in the attribute chart. While top models still dominate, frequently reaching high accuracy in many regions, they also show pronounced dips that point to uneven geographic representation or state-specific linguistic complexity. Ultimately, the transition from the relatively smooth performance by attribute to the highly volatile performance by state implies that while models can grasp general cultural concepts, geographic generalization remains a significantly harder challenge.

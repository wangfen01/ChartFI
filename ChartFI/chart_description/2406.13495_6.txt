The figure illustrates the logit and confidence distributions for real versus fake classes across four SBI evaluation settings, highlighting how model performance shifts under varying degrees of domain and method mismatch. In the baseline same-domain, same-method condition, the separation is strongest, with real samples clustering tightly at higher logits and near-certain confidences while fake samples are shifted left with a broader spread; this behavior aligns with the expectation that when forgery samples appear significantly different from real samples, SBI classifies them as anomalies, resulting in clear discriminability and high model certainty. However, as the evaluation conditions become more challenging, the charts reveal a distinct pattern showing increased overlap between real and fake logits as domains and methods change incrementally. Specifically, when moving to cross-domain scenarios with the same method, the confidence curves flatten and the two classes overlap more in logit space, creating a scenario where crossing domains can result in "wrong confidence" for real predictions and vice versa. In this state, real samples lose their sharp peak at 1 while fake samples gain probability near high confidence, increasing the risk of confident errors. This degradation persists under the same-domain but cross-method condition, where the logit gap narrows further as the fake distribution shifts toward the real distribution. The most pronounced deterioration occurs in the cross-domain, cross-method case, where logits for real and fake overlap heavily and the confidence distributions become nearly indistinguishable, with substantial fake mass near high confidence. The overarching trend across the panels is a monotonic loss of separability and calibration as mismatch increases, characterized by the persistence of high-confidence predictions even when logits overlap, signaling overconfidence under distribution shift rather than a simple drop in certainty.

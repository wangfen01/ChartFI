These four correlation plots compare belief judgments from humans on the y-axis against model predictions on the x-axis, where each dot represents a probability rating on a scale from 0 to 1.0 regarding the likelihood of an object's location. Across all panels, human scores rise as model scores increase, indicating a consistent positive alignment, though the Full Model shows a significantly better fit to human judgment than ablated unimodal baselines and the Gemini model. Specifically, the Full Model correlated strongly with human judgment, with r = 0.78 and a 95% confidence interval of [0.70, 0.85], featuring points that cluster closely around the upward trend line and error bars indicating standard error. In contrast, the ablated Audio-Only Model and Vision-Only Model performed worse, with r = 0.55 and r = 0.53 respectively, suggesting that while individual modalities capture meaningful signals, they lose substantial explanatory power compared to the multimodal setup. The Vision-Only variant notably exhibits a dense vertical cluster of data points around the mid-range model score of 0.5, where human judgments vary wildly from 0 to nearly 1.0, highlighting a specific failure to resolve ambiguity. The Gemini Baseline, a large foundation VLM, shows the weakest association with a low correlation of r = 0.31 and the widest relative scatter, indicating that large foundation models still cannot reliably reason about ambiguous multimodal inputs in a human-like way. This lack of calibration is evidenced by the high vertical dispersion across the entire x-axis, where the model often assigns high-confidence scores that humans do not share, whereas the Full Model maintains a more stable monotonic relationship and reduces these predictive mismatches.

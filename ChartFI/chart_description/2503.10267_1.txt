This treemap illustrates the distribution of documents in the cleaned monolingual data by language families and language variety, where the area of each segment represents its respective share. Indo-European languages make up the majority of the data, a dominance driven primarily by English, which alone makes up the largest share of the data at 42%. Within the Indo-European family, a steep drop-off follows English, with Russian at 8%, Spanish and German each at 5%, and French at 4%, followed by a long tail of smaller European languages mostly ranging between 1% and 2%. This distribution indicates a highly concentrated pattern where a single language accounts for a disproportionate share while the remainder of the family is fragmented into much smaller segments. Outside of the Indo-European group, Sino-Tibetan stands out as the clear second-largest family, almost entirely represented by Chinese at 13%, suggesting that this family’s presence is similarly concentrated in a single dominant variety rather than spread across multiple mid-sized languages. Japanese appears as a distinct block at 4%, while all remaining families, including Altaic, Austronesian, Afro-Asiatic, and Uralic, contribute marginally at generally 1% or less per language. The most significant feature of the data is the extreme imbalance at the top, where the massive share held by English visually compresses the variation among the many smaller languages, reinforcing a winner-take-most structure rather than a gradual or equitable distribution across global language families.

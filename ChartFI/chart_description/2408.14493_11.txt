These stacked area graphs illustrate typical operation scenarios considering new energy output under a medium proportion of new energy integration, comparing generation profiles across different bus nodes over 5‑minute intervals. In the full dispatch state shown in the first panel, corresponding to time OM_1, new energy nodes in the area exist in a state close to their rated capacity, contributing to a total output that climbs steadily from the low‑700 MW range to a broad peak near 900–950 MW. Here, the other thermal power nodes provide flexible adjustments to ensure safe and stable operation of the regional power system, resulting in a relatively stable profile with coordinated balancing. Conversely, the non‑dispatch state depicted in the second panel represents time OM_s where, owing to natural conditions, the output of the new energy nodes is approximately zero, and the entire area is supplied with thermal power. This scenario follows a pronounced U‑shape trajectory, starting high near 1,100–1,150 MW, dipping to a trough around 850–900 MW, and ramping sharply to levels near 1,250–1,300 MW. An analysis of the power grid configuration reveals that while traditional thermal power generation remains the dominant power source, the system maximizes the consumption of new energy by adjusting thermal output during periods of high new energy availability; however, when new energy is unavailable, the system exhibits increased jaggedness and step changes, particularly after the 100th time step, indicating tighter operational constraints and higher balancing stress.

The performance evaluation of various causal discovery methods is illustrated across varying node numbers (10, 30, 100), average degree (sparse vs. dense), binary ratio (BR=0.1 vs. BR=0.5), and sample size (50, 500, 5000), with circle size representing the relative magnitude of the standard deviation for metrics including True Positive Rate (TPR), False Discovery Rate (FDR), and Structural Hamming Distance (SHD). It is evident that KEEL outperforms other methods, consistently achieving optimal performance compared to KEEL_wk, which utilizes weakened knowledge constraints. KEEL can handle various types of mixed data and exhibits stable performance as the number of nodes and edges increases and the sample size decreases, maintaining the highest and most stable TPR near the upper band of the panels while keeping FDR and SHD significantly lower than its competitors. In contrast, GGES fails to scale to 100 nodes and dense scenarios due to its unacceptable computational complexity, and many other methods show pronounced oscillations or abrupt collapses to near-zero TPR at specific sample sizes. As the problem scales from 10 to 100 nodes, TPR generally becomes more volatile with noticeable drops in dense settings and under BR=0.5, while SHD increases dramatically from tens of errors to the high hundreds, marking the most prominent scaling effect. While increasing sample size tends to stabilize results for robust methods, it does not uniformly improve them for all; many approaches maintain persistently high FDR levels that hover in the mid-to-upper range even with additional data, indicating an over-selection of edges rather than convergence toward cleaner sets. Ultimately, scalability and density serve as the primary stressors, revealing that while KEEL maintains robustness across these regimes, other methods diverge sharply in accuracy and stability, often exhibiting regime-specific breakdowns as connectivity and class imbalance intensify.

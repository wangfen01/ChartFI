This visualization tracks an 8-second window across five linked subplots displaying allocated PRBs, selected MCS, transport block throughput (TBS) by RNTI, the rate gap between packet generation and physical-layer service, and one-way end-to-end delay. The system operates in a relatively steady regime for most of the interval but is punctuated by two RRC state transitions, highlighted in red, where both the PRB and MCS subplots lack data points, indicating a complete cessation of PHY-layer transmissions. The third subplot confirms the underlying cause of these disruptions by showing a change in the Radio Network Temporary Identifier (RNTI), where the active transmitter shifts from RNTI 25970 to 18610 and finally to 26588. This entire transition process resulted in an interruption period of approximately 300 ms during which the UE could neither send nor receive data. While the physical-layer service halts, the fourth subplot showing rate gaps indicates that the application continues to generate and send data throughout the interruption periods highlighted in red, creating a positive gap where the packet rate exceeds the physical-layer throughput. Consequently, packets buffer extensively at the UE, causing one-way delay to surge dramatically up to 400 ms as shown in the fifth subplot. Following each interruption, the delay exhibits a gradual decay back to baseline as the system catches up and drains the backlog once scheduling and transmission resume. These significant delay spikes are not driven by a sustained deterioration in MCS or a long-term PRB collapse but are specifically aligned with short transition-related disruptions where the UE stops transmission during RNTI reassignments, briefly starving the flow before recovering to a stable state.

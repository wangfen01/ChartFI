The distribution of time that agents controlled by our policy spend in the scene in comparison to the real crowd is illustrated across three distinct datasets—ETH, GC, and Forum—contrasting ground truth (GT) with two model variants, Poisson-GMM and nTPP-GMM. In the ETH dataset, the ground truth shows a strong concentration at very short durations, while Poisson-GMM is even more front-loaded and introduces extreme late-time outliers beyond 200 frames, suggesting occasional overestimation of long delays; conversely, nTPP-GMM tracks the early-time mass more closely and keeps the tail more controlled, though it still underrepresents the longer-time spread seen in the real data. For the GC dataset, the time agents usually spend in the scene is shown to be highly consistent across all three distributions, which overlap substantially and center around mid-range times, indicating that the distribution is close to the real data and both models capture the typical timing well. However, the ground truth in GC appears slightly more peaked at earlier mid-range values, while the models, especially nTPP-GMM, spread probability further into the higher-time region, implying a mild tendency to over-allocate to longer intervals. In the Forum dataset, the ground truth is clearly shifted toward longer durations with a broad right tail extending past 100 frames, whereas both models compress the distribution toward shorter times; here, Poisson-GMM is the most concentrated and earliest, and while nTPP-GMM is closer, it still misses much of the long tail. Overall, the model fit is strongest for GC, where the generated behavior most accurately reflects the real crowd, remains reasonable for ETH despite tail differences, and is weakest for Forum, where both approaches systematically underestimate long waiting times and fail to reproduce the heavy-tailed behavior present in the ground truth.

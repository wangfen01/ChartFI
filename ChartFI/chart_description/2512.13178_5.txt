The chart compares industry-level EV centrality with firm-level EV centrality across countries, utilizing EVCP in the first panel and EV-specific closeness centrality in the second. Plotting firm-level data on the y-axis against industry-level data on the x-axis, the EVCP panel shows that firm-level EVCP generally rises as industry-level EVCP increases, indicating a positive association where countries embedded in more central industries also tend to have firms that are more central. However, the spread is wide, and several countries deviate notably from the trend. Switzerland, Korea, Canada, the Netherlands, and China show higher firm-level EVCP values, with Switzerland acting as a clear anomaly by exhibiting exceptionally high firm-level EVCP despite only moderate industry-level EVCP. Conversely, countries like Poland, Vietnam, and Portugal rank lower on a firm- than industry-level, sitting near zero or negative firm-level EVCP and suggesting that industry position does not always translate into firm-level prominence. The second panel shows the results for EV-specific closeness centrality on a firm-level against results obtained on an industry-level, revealing a tighter, more compressed relationship where dispersion is smaller than in EVCP. For the industry-level data, the highest diversification potential is identified for Switzerland, Hungary, Sweden, Slovakia, and Portugal. Conversely, countries such as Russia, South Africa, Brazil, Indonesia and India exhibit comparatively lower diversification potential. Using only the firm-level data, the highest values are observed for Korea, China, USA, Canada, Japan, and UK, followed by France, Germany, Italy and Austria. While firm-level EV closeness increases modestly with industry-level EV closeness, clustering heavily around industry values near 1.4 to 1.5 and firm values around 0.19 to 0.26, these standout high-firm-closeness cases point to country-specific factors that enhance firm connectivity beyond what industry structure alone would predict.

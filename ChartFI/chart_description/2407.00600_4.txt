The two panels illustrate the relationship between gender bias signals in the model and real-world data, where scatter plots are divided into four colored quadrants and points are colored to indicate male- or female-dominated occupations. In the first panel, which maps the connection between VL-bias and U.S. labor force statistics, the bias generally aligns with labor statistics, as most occupations fall into quadrants one and three. As the male proportion increases, VL-bias rises from negative to positive, indicating that female-dominated jobs such as nurse practitioner and dental hygienist cluster on the left with more negative bias values, while male-dominated jobs like aircraft pilot and pest control worker cluster on the right with more positive values; this strong relationship is evidenced by a Pearson correlation of 0.748 and a Kendall Tau of 0.594. Turning to the second panel, the scatter plots compare occupation-level bias in the visual modal (V-Bias, x-axis) and the language modal (L-Bias, y-axis). We observe strong alignment between the direction of visual modal bias and language modal bias, with occupations clustering in quadrants one and three, forming a diagonal gradient from strongly negative values for healthcare and support roles to strongly positive values for construction and aviation roles. There is a strong correlation in the extent of these biases, with average rho and tau coefficients of 0.77 and 0.60, respectively, meaning occupations that are biased in the language-only signal are usually biased in the same direction in the vision-only signal. Overall, the charts suggest the model reproduces occupational gender stereotypes consistently across modalities, with the largest deviations concentrated in highly gender-segregated professions.

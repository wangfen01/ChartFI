The figure presents a comprehensive analysis of correlation structures, beginning with the spatial residual covariance matrices, E(epsilon_t * epsilon_t^T), for three- and six-step-ahead forecasts at t=15 and t=30 minutes. In these heatmaps, the matrices exhibit clear off-diagonal structure rather than a clean diagonal, indicating meaningful cross-variable dependence; moving from 15 to 30 minutes, the pattern becomes more pronounced and grid-like, suggesting stronger or more persistent shared variation as the window length increases. Ideally, if prediction errors were spatially independent, these matrices would be diagonal, a contrast explicitly provided by the uncorrelated baseline which serves as a strong visual control. Extending this analysis to temporal dependencies, the middle row displays the cross-lag covariance matrices, E(epsilon_t * epsilon_{t+ts}^T), for time lags of ts=15 and ts=30 minutes in three-step-ahead forecasting. These panels retain dependence but appear more diffuse, with many moderate-intensity pockets spread across the matrix; however, if prediction errors were truly spatiotemporally uncorrelated, all entries in these matrices would be close to zero, similar to the uncorrelated reference panel. Further reinforcing this temporal story, the bottom row presents the autocorrelation function (ACF) plots for sensors #50 and #100, respectively. For these sensors, there is a large lag-1 autocorrelation and several early lags that exceed the confidence band, indicating short-term persistence and non-random temporal structure. If prediction errors were temporally uncorrelated, all spikes, except the one at lag 0, would remain within the threshold shadow, as observed in the uncorrelated ACF plot which stays within the confidence bounds across lags matching white-noise behavior. Ultimately, the left two columns empirically reveal a clear spatiotemporal correlation structure in prediction errors, distinguishing them from the uncorrelated cases which show no meaningful structure in either cross-sectional correlation or time autocorrelation.

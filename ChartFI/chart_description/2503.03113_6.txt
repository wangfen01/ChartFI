The four panels illustrate the SHAP impact of various features on model outcomes across scenarios for no travel, moon trip, suborbital travel, and orbital travel, with features listed on the left side of each subfigure in descending order of importance from top to bottom. Each violin plot displays the distribution of SHAP impacts, where red color represents high values and blue color illustrates low values, revealing how specific variables drive the prediction toward or away from a travel choice. In the no-travel scenario, the outcome is primarily driven by baseline pricing and household context, where average_price_dollars is the dominant driver, followed by housing_tenure_type and average_probability_fatality; notably, high fatality probability makes people not want to travel, while demographic indicators like gender and age show that males (red color) want to travel more than females. Once travel options are introduced, the importance structure shifts sharply toward scenario-specific price and risk changes, and the trend of these features is mostly converse in the travel classes compared to the no-travel baseline. For moon trips, delta_price_dollars_moon_trip becomes the strongest driver, with household_annual_income and delta_price_dollars_suborbital also rising in importance, indicating that the predicted outcome is highly sensitive to incremental moon-trip pricing and broader budget constraints, particularly as people who have lower annual income do not want to travel. In the suborbital travel scenario, delta_price_dollars_moon_trip remains the primary influence, suggesting cross-option substitution effects, while orbital and suborbital fatality deltas and annual income also become more prominent. For orbital travel, delta_price_dollars_suborbital leads the importance ranking, followed by gender and delta_price_dollars_orbital, again pointing to strong cross-price sensitivity across tiers. Across all travel scenarios, the widest SHAP spreads cluster around delta_price variables, signaling nonlinear responses where small price changes can flip the prediction, while geography variables such as region, state, and city remain consistently low-impact and tightly distributed.

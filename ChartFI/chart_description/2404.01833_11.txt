The chart compares attack success rates across a range of prompt categories under different backtrack-step settings, demonstrating that the effectiveness of backtracking steps is highly task-dependent. Performance often saturates near the top end for categories such as Election, Climate, Intimacy, and Denial, which remain consistently high across most settings; for example, a task like Election maintained 100% attack success rate (ASR) even with 0 backtracking steps, suggesting these categories are broadly susceptible regardless of backtracking. In contrast, categories like Explicit and Toxin are persistently low, indicating strong resistance that backtracking does not meaningfully overcome. However, significant variations appear in mid-range categories where the baseline is neither trivially easy nor strongly blocked. Tasks like Manifesto proved to be challenging with 0 backtracking steps, but showed improvement with increased intervention. The data further reveals nuances in attack strategies: while increasing the number of rounds can compensate for the reduced backtracking to some extent, it is still not as effective as increasing backtrack steps for many categories. Conversely, tasks related to mental health experienced a significant boost in performance with more rounds, specifically Soothinge, demonstrating the specific utility of multi-turn strategies for these topics. Ultimately, the uneven sensitivity across categories highlights that backtracking acts as a targeted amplifier rather than a universal lever, with success rates swinging substantially depending on the specific content type.

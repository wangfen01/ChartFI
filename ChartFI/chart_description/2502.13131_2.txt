The weight distributions of the top 100 decomposed reward heads on RewardBench for Decomposed Reward Models (DRMs) using Gemma-2B-RM as the backbone reveal distinct patterns across the Chat, Chat Hard, Safety, and Reasoning subsets, highlighting the differing preference requirements across these categories. The Chat subset primarily relies on the first few basis vectors, which capture the most data variance, evidenced by a very large spike at the first head followed by a quick collapse to a near-flat, low-amplitude baseline for the rest of the range. In contrast, the Chat Hard and Safety subsets exhibit a more uniform weight distribution; while Chat Hard shows an early spike, it maintains a consistently noisier mid-level band with frequent small oscillations, and Safety displays more intermittent mid-to-late spikes that imply additional heads contribute meaningfully at scattered points, especially in the latter half. The Reasoning subset depends more heavily on basis vectors indexed between 1 and 50, presenting an irregular and bursty pattern with multiple pronounced peaks through the first half of the indices before transitioning into a stretch of lower coefficients with sporadic late spikes. Collectively, these visualizations of weight vectors demonstrate that while the earliest heads generally concentrate the most signal, the degree of sparsity and volatility varies significantly, with reasoning-related signals requiring several specialized early heads and more complex tasks necessitating a broader distribution of explanatory weight across the decomposed indices.

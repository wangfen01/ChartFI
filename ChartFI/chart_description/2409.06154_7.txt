The confusion matrices on DFEW, FERV39K, and MAFW datasets illustrate a consistent performance hierarchy where majority categories such as happy, sad, and neutral expressions demonstrate high accuracy across all datasets. DFEW exhibits the strongest separability for these primary emotions, with correct rates ranging from the mid-70s to mid-90s; however, minority categories, including disgust, fear, and helplessness exhibit lower accuracy, particularly on the DFEW dataset where disgust (DI) falls to a true positive rate of approximately 28%. A significant source of this error is that some expressions are frequently misclassified as neutral, particularly disgust and surprise. For instance, in both the DFEW and FERV39K datasets, disgust is frequently misclassified as neutral, with DFEW showing a substantial 41% leakage of disgust into the neutral category. FERV39K is noticeably harder, with diagonal dominance dropping across shared classes and surprise collapsing to roughly 25%. The MAFW dataset, which introduces a richer taxonomy, confirms this trend: while happy and sad remain robust, the added affective categories introduce new failure modes, specifically where helplessness (HL) shows minimal correct classification (roughly 9%) and disgust is confused with anxiety and anger. Overall, the data indicates that while the model excels at identifying distinct, high-frequency emotions, performance degrades for minority classes that repeatedly bleed into neighboring neutral or high-arousal categories.

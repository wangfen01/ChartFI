This per-organ performance comparison of seven image-editing models across three metrics—editing accuracy, contextual preservation, and visual quality—reveals that while maintaining image integrity is relatively common, achieving precise anatomical modifications remains a significant challenge. Gemini 2 Flash achieves the best overall performance, leading in editing accuracy with an EA of 0.68 and visual quality with a VQ of 0.72, while ranking second in context preservation with a CP of 0.82. Among open-source models, ICEdit shows the most balanced performance, matching Gemini 2 Flash in visual quality at 0.72 and maintaining an EA of 0.60 and a CP of 0.79. In contrast, InstructPix2Pix excels in context preservation with a CP of 0.86 but suffers from lower editing accuracy at an EA of 0.50. Across the board, contextual preservation and visual quality are consistently higher and more stable than editing accuracy, which shows the widest spread across both models and body parts. Editing accuracy is the primary differentiator, with Gemini 2 Flash standing out for its high and uniform accuracy across most regions, whereas other models fluctuate sharply depending on the anatomy. Accuracy tends to be stronger on prominent facial attributes like the nose, skin, and face, but drops notably on harder or less visually constrained targets such as bones and the GI tract, which emerge as systematic failure points across all three metrics. A substantial gap persists between Gemini and open-source methods, with over 10% difference in editing accuracy highlighting the limitations of current methods for high-precision medical editing. While models like paint-by-inpaint show clear quality deficits around teeth and feet, and imagic sits consistently inside the others in context preservation, the recurring difficulty in editing internal or complex structures like the GI tract underscores a broader technical hurdle in the field.

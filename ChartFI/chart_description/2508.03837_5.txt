The charts compare speedup across a suite of applications when the system features two, four, eight, and sixteen cores, contrasting gem5 MESI and MOESI against two Rhea RTL MSI implementations. Both the single- and two-level RTL cache-coherent memory subsystems, which implement an MSI cache coherence protocol, perform in line with expectations, delivering execution times with intermediate values between those obtained from the gem5 Ruby’s simpler MI and more complex MESI and MOESI with all of the considered numbers of cores in the overall system. Compared to the simplest MI protocol, the gem5 MESI and MOESI ones and the single-level RTL design deliver an average speedup that ranges from 2.1x, 2.0x, and 1.4x in the dual-core scenario up to 11.8x, 12.0x, and 5.7x in the sixteen-core one, respectively, as summarized in the rightmost bars. While the 2-level MSI generally improves on the 1-level MSI, it still remains below the gem5 protocols. Scaling with core count is the dominant shift, as most applications move from modest gains in the dual-core case to substantially larger gains at eight and sixteen cores. The separation between the gem5 and Rhea configurations widens as parallelism increases, suggesting the RTL MSI designs capture less of the available scaling headroom, particularly in the higher-core regimes where coherence and memory-system effects amplify. A few workloads stand out as clear outliers with very large speedups at higher core counts, most notably LC, which becomes the most extreme case as cores increase, and FM, which also accelerates sharply. In contrast, several applications remain comparatively flat and low across configurations, indicating limited parallel scalability or stronger bottlenecks that coherence protocol choice cannot overcome. Ultimately, the presence of very high-speedup outliers alongside many moderate or low-scaling applications implies the overall average is increasingly influenced by a small subset of highly scalable workloads as core count rises.

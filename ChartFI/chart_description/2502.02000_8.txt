The figure illustrates the percentage difference between the estimates from NOAA Atlas 14 and a 2022 model implementation across the northern Gulf Coast for 10-year and 100-year events, where positive (blue) values indicate the new estimates are higher, and negative (red) values indicate that they are lower than NOAA Atlas 14 estimates. The comparison evaluates three modeling approaches: Pooled Stationary, Nonpooled Nonstationary, and Spatially Varying Covariates. In the Pooled Stationary maps, the spatial distribution reveals a strong west–east gradient that intensifies with rarer events; changes are relatively smooth and muted at 10 years, then become more polarized at 100 years, with a coherent decrease concentrated in the western portion of the domain and a corresponding increase toward the east. The Nonpooled Nonstationary results display substantially higher local variability, where the model predominantly produces lower estimates for 10-year events but shifts to include higher estimates for 100-year events. This approach exhibits pronounced patchiness for the 100-year event, suggesting that allowing site-specific nonstationarity amplifies localized signals and uncertainty rather than producing a single regional pattern. In contrast, the Spatially Varying Covariates model yields the most consistent and spatially coherent increases, characterized by higher estimates in the eastern part of the study area and lower estimates in the western part compared to NOAA Atlas 14 estimates. This model tends to produce similar or lower estimates in the west, and higher estimates in the east, notably around western LA for 100-year events, implying that covariate-driven structure is capturing a broad-scale intensification that grows with event rarity. Ultimately, the persistent western-area tendency toward negative change in the pooled stationary and covariate-based maps contrasts with the nonpooled nonstationary panel, which intermittently breaks this into mixed-sign pockets, highlighting how model choice materially affects whether the observed trends are interpreted as a regional gradient, localized heterogeneity, or widespread intensification.

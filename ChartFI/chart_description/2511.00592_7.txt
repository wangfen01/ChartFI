This chart compares the median speedup across a suite of workloads as problem size scales from MINI through XLARGE, where each bar represents the median speedup over a pool of 40 runs and error bars indicate the 95% bootstrapped confidence interval for that median. Performance gains exhibit significant variability across benchmarks and input sizes, though a dominant trend reveals that larger input sizes often enable higher speedups while many small-size runs remain near baseline. The most dramatic scaling appears in covariance and correlation, where speedup jumps from low single digits at MINI and SMALL sizes to roughly around a thousand at XLARGE, indicating these kernels benefit strongly from increased parallel work. A second tier of strong gainers includes 2mm and 3mm, which rise from modest improvements to a few hundred at larger sizes, and the SYR family, where syr2k and syrk surge sharply into the high hundreds. Mid-range improvements are visible in workloads like gemm, gemver, gramschmidt, and mvt, which move to tens of times faster at larger sizes, suggesting meaningful but less explosive scalability. In contrast, several benchmarks such as cholesky, lu, ludcmp, symm, and trisolv stay clustered close to 1x to low single digits across sizes, implying limited parallelism or overheads that dominate even as the problem grows. Regarding stability, some benchmarks exhibit large confidence intervals; specifically, error bars widen notably for the highest-speedup cases at the largest sizes, signaling greater variability when performance is extremely high, while the consistently flat series show tighter stability.

The comparison of model logits distributions under steering and prompting strategies reveals that the steering strategy—both positive and negative—substantially alters the top token distribution, whereas the influence of prompting on the model’s token distribution is much smaller than that of steering. In the steering strategy, adjusting the multiplier produces large, systematic horizontal shifts across the entire distribution: negative multipliers move logits to the right into positive territory, while positive multipliers push logits sharply left, with the strongest setting of multiplier 8 creating a pronounced peak far into the negative range around -20. The 0-multiplier baseline sits near slightly negative to near-zero logits, and intermediate multipliers show a clear monotonic progression, indicating that steering acts as a strong additive bias that fundamentally reshapes the model’s confidence landscape. In contrast, the token distribution of prompting, which utilizes few-shot demonstrations, yields much tighter clustering across conditions, with most distributions remaining centered near zero. These prompting strategies show a small impact on token distribution compared to the vanilla model (shot = 0), where the 0-shot condition is the most left-shifted and positive-shot prompting nudges the distribution only slightly right. Overall, the data indicate that steering introduces high leverage and can induce extreme logit regimes, whereas prompting produces incremental, more controlled adjustments with substantial overlap, suggesting it is less likely to cause abrupt confidence flips but also less capable of driving large-scale directional changes in the model's output.

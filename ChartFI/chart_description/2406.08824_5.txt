This heatmap visualizes the probability assigned by Mistral7b to a harmful action in various tasks when personal characteristics are part of the prompt, with rows sorted by average probability across tasks to highlight systemic biases. The data reveals that the most harmful outputs were generated for the rescue, security risk, and facial expression tasks, which exhibit the strongest and most variable signals compared to dimensions like expected room cleanliness or proximity command that remain consistently lower. At the aggregate level, the descriptors associated with the highest probability of harmful actions include Black, Palestinian, Jewish, Gypsy, Muslim, and Iraqi, whereas the lowest probability of harmful actions corresponds to Japanese, Normal, European, Middle-aged, British, and Nondisabled. Specific dimensions underscore these disparities; particularly, Mistral assigns a high security risk to people described as Palestinian, peaking at 0.72, followed closely by Muslim at 0.68, Middle-Eastern at 0.67, and Iraqi at 0.61, creating a pronounced gap relative to descriptors like White at 0.13. The Facial Expression and Security tasks are among those with the most harmful assignments generated, with facial expression emotion FEER reaching extreme highs for Jewish at 0.83, Paralyzed at 0.81, and Muslim at 0.78. In the rescue priority dimension, values are comparatively high for specific gender and ability categories, such as Cisgender Man at 0.68 and Able-bodied at 0.61, while collaboration trust shows selective boosts for descriptors like Man at 0.55 and Adult at 0.51, contrasting with the tight grouping observed in expected cleanliness preference.

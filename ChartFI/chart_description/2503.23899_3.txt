This bar chart illustrates the sources of bad commentaries by identifying which of the six writing-quality dimensions—grammaticality, word choice, cohesion, conciseness, appropriateness, and coherence—were not met across evaluations for contractors, experts, open-source LLMs, closed-source LLMs, and an overall benchmark. The data reveals that the main source of a bad explanation for artificial intelligence was the lack of conciseness, with open-source LLMs averaging 96.89% and closed-source LLMs averaging 99.06% on this sublabel, representing a stark contrast to the low frequency of errors in word choice, cohesion, appropriateness, and grammaticality for these models. While LLMs struggle almost exclusively with brevity, conciseness is less of a problem to humans, whose explanations are mostly judged as bad due to poor coherence, particularly among contractors who struggled with coherence more than any other dimension. In contrast, bad explanations produced by experts were primarily due to grammaticality, though they also showed notable deficiencies in coherence and cohesion. Across the board, appropriateness and word choice remain the least frequent sources of failure for all groups, while the overall benchmark is heavily influenced by the high conciseness error rates of LLMs and the coherence struggles of human contributors. Ultimately, the chart highlights a fundamental divergence in failure modes: human-generated commentaries fail due to structural and linguistic inconsistencies like coherence and grammaticality, whereas large language models are overwhelmingly penalized for their lack of conciseness.

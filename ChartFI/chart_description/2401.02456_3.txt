This visualization presents a state-level heat map of US states with significant wildfire in 2022 alongside two national time series tracking the total number of acres burned in the US between 1985 and 2022 and the total number of wildfire occurrences in the US throughout the years 1985 to 2022. Spatially, wildfire activity is most concentrated in the West and parts of the South, where Texas, California, and Oregon are the most impacted states by wildfire events in 2022, while much of the Midwest and portions of the Northeast appear comparatively lighter, indicating fewer events. Over time, the total acres burned shows a clear upward shift from the late 1990s into the 2000s, moving from generally lower levels in the 1980s and early 1990s to repeated high-burn years that frequently approach or exceed 8 to 10 million acres. Although the total number of acres burned declined from 10 million to seven million during the last three years, it is still higher than the annual average between 1985 and 2019, underscoring a sustained period of high-impact activity. In contrast, the total number of wildfire occurrences fluctuates within a broad band and does not rise in tandem with burned acreage; it peaks around the late 1990s to mid-2000s and then trends lower or flatter through the 2010s, with only partial rebounds in some recent years. This divergence reveals a critical decoupling between frequency and impact, where fewer or similar numbers of fires are consuming substantially more land, suggesting that fire severity and the conditions enabling large-scale conflagrations have intensified significantly over the last four decades.

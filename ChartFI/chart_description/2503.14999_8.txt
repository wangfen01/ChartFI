This correlation matrix illustrates the relationship between emotion-related individual-difference variables and haptic empathy outcomes, categorized by interpreting and design abilities across non-calibrated and calibrated conditions. The heatmap utilizes a color-coded scale to represent effect sizes, where white indicates very weak associations, light gray denotes weak associations, blue signifies moderate associations, yellow represents strong associations, and red indicates very strong associations. Across both conditions, the majority of cells are white or light gray with high p-values, indicating that outcomes for valence accuracy, arousal accuracy, and confidence are largely not systematically related to traits such as age, autism measures (AIM), or various emotional competence (EC) subscales. In the non-calibrated condition, both interpreting and design abilities show consistently negligible associations across nearly all predictors, with a few moderate but non-significant coefficients in design confidence likely representing noise rather than stable effects. While the pattern remains mostly weak in the calibrated condition, a notable shift emerges where interpersonal emotion comprehension (EC) is negatively correlated with valence accuracy from calibrated messages (ρ = -0.68, p < .024). This significant result, highlighted with a red stroke, stands out as the only statistically significant relationship in the matrix and runs counter to the intuitive expectation that higher competence would relate to higher accuracy. Aside from this anomaly and scattered moderate, non-significant correlations involving AIM and certain inter- or intra-personal subscales, the data do not reflect a coherent directional pattern, suggesting that emotional-competence profiles do not broadly drive improvements or deterioration in haptic empathy performance following calibration.

This visualization compares six continual-learning methods—finetuning, LwF, SI, PackNet, replay, and MAS—across three distinct task settings, with each sub-figure utilizing box plots to display mean, median, and quartile data for accuracy and forgetting. In the first setting, where the X-axis represents a specific order of the tasks, the change of context order from E-H (easy-to-hard) to H-E (hard-to-easy) does not significantly affect average accuracy and forgetting measure for most methods, indicating that the impact of the task order on the average accuracy and forgetting measure is insignificant. However, the variance of the performance scores is considerably smaller for easy-to-hard ordering, suggesting greater reliability in that sequence compared to the higher and more dispersed forgetting seen in finetuning and LwF under the hard-to-easy transition. When the X-axis represents whether the context is coarse or fine, the granularity of contexts significantly influences outcomes; specifically, when setting the granularity of contexts to fine-grained as the task-setting, we see that finetuning shows a lower accuracy and a higher forgetting measure, whereas methods such as LwF, PackNet, and replay reach a higher accuracy while maintaining a low forgetting measure. Moving to coarse contexts generally reduces peak performance for the strongest methods and increases spread, implying that coarser context definitions make retention harder and outcomes less stable. As the X-axis represents the total number of contexts (tasks) increasing from 10 to 20, different methods exhibit various performances, though a lower number of total tasks can stabilize the average performance of the continual learning methods. While PackNet and LwF can successfully re-use the previous knowledge to produce an increased accuracy on new tasks, finetuning introduces a significant forgetting measure accompanied by a low accuracy score when the number of tasks is large. Throughout these shifts, replay remains the most consistently strong performer, maintaining high accuracy and low forgetting across all task counts, while the widening variance for other methods at higher context counts highlights that scalability is as much about reliability as it is about average performance.

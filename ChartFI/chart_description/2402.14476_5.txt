The charts display the predicted uncertainty Var(y_hat) of the Combined approach alongside variations omitting model averaging (No Averaging), using a single output layer (Single Output), and using returns only (Returns-only) for BTC/USDT and Chevron. A critical comparison of the methods reveals that the Combined (blue line) and No Averaging (orange line) series track each other closely, demonstrating very similar estimated predictive uncertainties with and without model averaging. Conversely, the Single Output and Returns-only approaches exhibit distinct block-like appearances, indicating that both using a single output layer and using returns only result in the network failing to closely track the time-varying variance of the data generating process (DGP). For BTC/USDT from mid-2019 to early-2022, the uncertainty profile is generally low but punctuated by sharp, short-lived spikes; the Combined and No Averaging series capture these pronounced surges around early 2020 and early-to-mid 2021, reflecting brief episodes of elevated forecast risk. However, the stepped, unresponsive nature of the Single Output and Returns-only models renders them unable to adequately capture these transient shocks. In the case of Chevron from the mid-1990s to 2021, uncertainty is higher in absolute terms and follows clearer multi-year regimes, culminating in a dominant peak around 2008–2009 followed by stabilization. While the Combined and No Averaging methods effectively trace these macro-driven cycles and secondary disruptions near 2015–2016 and 2020–2021, the Returns-only model stays elevated for longer stretches in the early-to-mid 2000s with a rigid structure that lacks the granular responsiveness of the primary models. Overall, while BTC is characterized by frequent, narrow shock spikes and Chevron by longer structural cycles, the visual evidence underscores that the simplified model architectures struggle to replicate the signal fidelity observed in the Combined and No Averaging approaches.

This chart illustrates the decision-making bias of various computational models and humans across sixteen distinct shape categories, where the value on the top represents the fraction of shape decisions and the value at the bottom represents the fraction of texture decisions. Individual markers denote the fraction of certain shape categories, while vertical lines indicate the mean fraction across shape categories for each system. Humans exhibit a highly consistent cluster near the left axis, characterized by very low texture reliance and very high shape reliance, establishing a stable shape bias benchmark. In contrast, standard self-supervised and supervised models, such as SimCLR and MoCoV2, are distributed toward the right, indicating they are typically driven more by surface appearance than by global form. Among the evaluated models, the star markers represent our proposed approaches, which demonstrate a notable shift toward shape-based recognition. Specifically, the proposed SPCL is the only model that uses shape as the predominant cue, i.e. shape bias score greater than 0.5, as evidenced by its mean vertical line crossing into the left half of the plot. While ReflCL shows improvements compared to the rest of the approaches, it is still biased towards texture with a fraction of shape decisions less than 0.5. Although these proposed methods significantly narrow the gap between machine and human perception, a clear separation remains, as even the most advanced models exhibit greater category-to-category variability and less invariant strategies than the human baseline.

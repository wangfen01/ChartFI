These four plots compare ground truth azimuth and elevation over time against Line-of-Sight (LoS), smoothed Non-Line-of-Sight (NLoS), and raw NLoS estimates under both stable and unstable conditions. The estimated DoA information in NLoS settings, depicted by the yellow dashed curves, reveals that severe deviation occurs in NLoS no matter whether the drone moves, manifesting as frequent, large spikes. The effectiveness of smoothing this data is highly dependent on the drone's state. When the drone is hovering or yawing in the stable scenarios, the estimated DoA is smooth and tracks the nearly constant ground truth closely, even if the raw observations slightly deviate. Instead, the smoothed DoA information is erratic when the drone is moving during the unstable scenarios. In the unstable azimuth case, where the ground truth drops from the high-300° range to the mid-260° range before recovering, the smoothed NLoS estimate lags and shows step-like adjustments. This highlights that the DoA in NLoS is still unstable after filtering, which is especially evident in azimuth estimation, as shown by the green curve. The raw NLoS data becomes exceptionally volatile during these dynamic periods, with frequent large excursions that make it unreliable. Across all conditions, the LoS estimate provides the most faithful tracking of the ground truth, while the smoothed NLoS offers a significant improvement over raw NLoS in stable conditions but struggles with accuracy and responsiveness during rapid changes.

The distribution of effective resistance (ER) for all node pairs is compared across graphs with varying scales and homophily levels to illustrate structural changes before and after rewiring. At a small scale of 20 edges, both High and Low Homophily regimes exhibit sparse, irregular histograms with noticeable mass near very small values and scattered higher peaks; however, after applying GOKU, the rewired distributions tend to concentrate more probability at the low end while the original networks retain more spread, reflecting strong small-sample variability. As the graph size increases to 245 edges, a clearer separation emerges where the rewired distributions cluster tightly at lower values, while the original distributions shift rightward and broaden, indicating systematically larger metric values in the original network and greater heterogeneity that rewiring dampens. At the largest scale of 80,352 edges, the contrast becomes stark and stable as the rewired distributions collapse into a very narrow spike near a small value, whereas the original distributions form a distinct, tight mound at a substantially higher value with minimal overlap. Across all scales and homophily levels, the effective resistances between node pairs significantly decrease after applying GOKU, a reduction that indicates improvements in both local and global connectivity in the rewired graphs. While homophily levels influence the fine shape of the distributions at smaller sizes, they do not alter the dominant large-scale trend where original networks maintain higher effective resistance and rewired networks converge toward a low baseline, suggesting that at scale the metric is strongly driven by the original network structure and that rewiring effectively removes the structural signal to enhance connectivity.

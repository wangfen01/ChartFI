These two heatmaps display the P-values of the DM-test for France and Germany, visualizing the statistical significance of differences in forecasting accuracy among pairwise model comparisons, where darker cells indicate high p-values (insignificant differences) and lighter green or yellow cells indicate low p-values (significant differences). In both countries, the upper-left quadrant comprising the early “Van” and related variables (such as SRWW, STL, and VanDet) appears predominantly dark, indicating that these baseline measures yield statistically similar performance with little distinction in accuracy between them. However, the introduction of specific covariates disrupts this uniformity; the data reveals that including autoregressive, holiday, and temperature effects improves accuracy significantly in both countries, evidenced by the distinct lighter bands associated with the \AR, \Hld, and \Temp variables. A major divergence between the two datasets is observed in the performance of complex specifications. For the French load data, the GAM model including all but the effect of the state has significantly better forecasting accuracy compared to all other models, a dominance illustrated by the column of low p-values for GAM against the surrounding macro and policy variables. Furthermore, while the \States variable in France remains dark—implying it adds no predictive value—the German heatmap shows a marked shift in this area. Specifically, including mid-term state effects improved accuracy significantly only in Germany, indicated by the emergence of significant, lighter cells in the \States row and column that are absent in the French analysis. This contrast suggests that while weather and calendar effects are universally critical, the relevance of state-level and specific semi-parametric structures is highly dependent on the national context.

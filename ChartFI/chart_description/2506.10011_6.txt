This radial chart presents an F1-score comparison on the MIntRec dataset, evaluating the performance of the WDMIR method against a Human baseline and five other models—including SDIF-DA*, TCL-MAP, MulT, MISA, and MAG-BERT—across various dialogue-act categories. While the Human scores, represented by the outer reference ring, consistently set the highest standard in socially positive and cooperative acts such as Praise, Apologise, Thank, and Care, the models cluster closely below this benchmark. Specifically, WDMIR performs similarly to other methods in the “Care” and “Complain” categories, and even outperforms in some areas, indicating stable performance in common intent categories, likely due to the wavelet-driven nonverbal modal fusion. However, the landscape shifts significantly in more nuanced or adversarial acts, where the uniform gap disappears. In categories like “Taunt,” “Oppose,” and “Joke,” the Human baseline drops precipitously to 22.15, 33.97, and 37.54 respectively, suggesting these intents contain systematic ambiguity. In this complex terrain, the WDMIR approach demonstrates strong performance, particularly in the intent categories of “Taunt”—where it achieves a score of 65.00, surpassing the human benchmark—and “Joke,” suggesting that WDMIR is adept at managing complex intent categories. Nevertheless, performance remains volatile; despite some improvements in certain intent categories, WDMIR’s performance in “Flaunt” (23.34) and “Oppose” (38.01) is not as strong as that of other methods like SDIF-DA* or MAG-BERT, which may be linked to the influence of wavelet-driven nonverbal modal fusion.

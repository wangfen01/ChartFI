This visual representation of the performance–efficiency trade-offs for selected SLMs plots their mean pass@1 scores against their mean inference time in seconds, where each model is represented as a bubble with a size corresponding to its mean VRAM usage in GB. Models located in the bottom-right quadrant of the plot, with smaller bubbles, are considered ideal, as they combine high accuracy, fast inference, and low memory usage. A clear efficiency frontier emerges where Qwen2.5-Coder 1.5B and Qwen2.5-Coder 3.0B achieve pass@1 scores of 54% and 59%, respectively, with VRAM usage below 10 GB and inference times under 0.75 seconds. While OpenCodeInterpreter 6.7B and Qwen2.5-Coder 7B reach higher pass@1 scores of 62% and 65%, these gains require substantially more resources; Qwen2.5-Coder 7B, for instance, achieves a 20.37% improvement in pass@1 score over Qwen2.5-Coder 1.5B, but this gain comes at the cost of a 261% increase in VRAM usage (23.7 GB vs. 6.55 GB). In contrast, models like Yi-Coder_9.0B and OpenCodeInterpreter_6.7B sit in a similar accuracy band but show noticeably slower inference around 3 to 4 seconds, suggesting architectural or implementation overhead despite comparable memory footprints. Several anomalies highlight that scaling up does not guarantee better latency, as SLMs like Incoder 6.7B and PolyCoder 2.7B are positioned toward the top-left with larger bubbles, indicating lower accuracy, slower inference, and higher resource requirements. Specifically, Incoder_6.7B is a pronounced outlier with very low pass@1 and the slowest inference time near 8 seconds, while smaller variants like StarCoder2_3.0B and Phi1_1.3B achieve low inference times under about 1.5 seconds with mid-range accuracy. Across families, the jump from smaller to larger parameter sizes generally increases VRAM substantially and often improves pass@1, but the wide variance in inference time implies that runtime efficiency is driven as much by model design and serving stack as by size alone.

The nine heatmaps display the payoff tables for three distinct workloads—Wimblepong, MPE, and RoboMaster—across small, large, and full buffer configurations, mapping the performance relationships between models saved at different training phases. Taking the Wimblepong panels as an example, the axes track the interactions of 14 models saved at various intervals. Within these matrices, red squares indicate victories, with darker shades representing higher winning rates, while conversely, blue squares indicate defeats, with darker shades representing lower winning rates. The dominant structure is block-like, generally showing that later training checkpoints defeat earlier ones; however, this progression is not perfectly smooth. In the lower triangle of each of the three payoff tables, there are a few individual cells that are colored blue, signaling anomalies where an earlier model unexpectedly defeats a later one. Specifically, these inversion anomalies account for only 5/196 cells in Wimblepong, 2/64 in MPE, and 1/121 in RoboMaster. On MPE, the outcomes are particularly extreme and discretized, with many cells saturating near -1 or +1, implying highly consistent rank-order reversals, whereas RoboMaster presents a less binary, more textured pattern under small buffers with alternating bands of moderate results. Focusing on the lower triangle portion of each table reveals a systematic trend: as the replay buffer size gradually increases, the red part becomes darker and darker, while the blue part becomes less and lighter. This indicates that larger buffering amplifies the transitivity of the learning process, making the "who beats whom" relationships more predictable and reducing the patchiness or cyclic behavior seen in smaller-buffer settings, thereby ensuring a more ordered progression where training consistently yields stronger agents.

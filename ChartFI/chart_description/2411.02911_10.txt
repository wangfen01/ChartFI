The provided heatmaps compare modulation cardinality across channel indices—spanning L-, C-, and S-bands—for multiple connections under two routing and polarization conditions, FLP and FRP, across three network datasets. A comparative analysis reveals that higher modulation cardinality usage generally occurs in FRP mode for both C- and S-band channels. In the SPNB3014 and JPN4812 networks, most of the channel-connection resources (CCRs) in the L-band operate at the highest modulation cardinality, maintaining the same bit rate in both FLP and FRP modes; specifically, SPNB3014 remains overwhelmingly saturated with only minor edge-of-band degradation, while JPNB4812 shows a clearer transition where FRP sustains high cardinality longer into the C- and S-bands than FLP. The USB6012 dataset exhibits the most significant contrast, where FLP displays a pronounced left-to-right gradient. Crucially, there are certain CCRs in the S-band for USB6012 in FLP mode that cannot be utilized due to their GSNR being lower than the minimum GSNR required for the lowest modulation cardinality, depicted by black markers. However, when transitioning to FRP mode, these CCRs survive and the black markers disappear, indicating improved robustness in the high-frequency range. This improvement in the S-band under FRP is counterbalanced by a shift in the lower frequencies, albeit with a decrease in bit rate in the L-band compared to the FLP condition.

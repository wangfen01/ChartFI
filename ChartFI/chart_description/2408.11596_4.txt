The heatmaps across both datasets illustrate the sensitivities to the discounting factor alpha and the number of groups n_g, comparing calibration error at top-20 (ECE@20 and RDECE@20) as alpha varies down the rows and n_g increases across columns. Darker colors indicate higher error, revealing how ECE@N and RDECE@N are sensitive to those parameters, particularly in specific risk zones. In ML-1M, ECE@20 is generally low for small to moderate alpha but rises sharply when alpha becomes large, peaking at alpha=3.0 with n_g=1, while KuaiRec ECE@20 similarly shows increased sensitivity as alpha grows within the smallest-group regime. Across the evaluated configurations, the optimal value for alpha is around 0.2 for ECE@N and around 1.0 for RDECE@N. Furthermore, the optimal value for n_g is around 5, showing that building calibration models for each ranking group is better than using one common model (n_g=1) or models for each rank (n_g=20). This is clearly evidenced in the ML-1M RDECE@20 results, which remain flat around ~0.07 for most settings but exhibit a consistent step up to ~0.103 at n_g=20 regardless of alpha, and in KuaiRec RDECE@20, which decreases steeply from a high error at n_g=1 to stabilize at a low plateau around n_g=2–5 before showing a small uptick at n_g=20. Consequently, unless extreme values are used (e.g., alpha=3.0 or n_g=1, 20), TNF is not highly sensitive to these parameters and consistently improves upon the original method, demonstrating that moving to moderate group counts yields robust, low errors while avoiding the instability of extreme grouping granularity.

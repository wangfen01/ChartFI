The chart illustrates F1 scores for various matching approaches, which feature different types of datasets based on four table-splitting strategies: Unionable, View-Unionable, Joinable, and Semantic-Joinable. A dominant pattern reveals that data-oriented methods did a better job than the schema-based methods, as evidenced by the consistently lower performance of COMA-Schema compared to instance-based alternatives like COMA-Instance. Overall, our model’s macro-F1 performance—represented by the SMUTF variants—was the best among other benchmarks, demonstrating superior stability across varying conditions. In the Unionable setting, which is the easiest for most methods, EmbDI and the SMUTF variants are essentially at ceiling, while Cupid, Distribution, Jaccard Leven, and Similar Flood also sit in the mid-to-high 90s. A significant shift occurs in View-Unionable, where Cupid, EmbDI, and Sherlock drop markedly to the 30–40 range, whereas COMA-Instance remains at 100 and SMUTF stays strong around the mid-80s. Performance rebounds for several baselines in the Joinable condition, with Distribution rising into the low 90s; however, while SMUTF remains robust, its individual evaluation on the joinable dataset (F1 85.71%) was worse than the Jaccard-Levenshtein method (F1 100%), indicating that straightforward string similarity can outperform embedding-based signals in specific join scenarios. Finally, Semantic-Joinable highlights the strongest generalization for the proposed method: while Distribution collapses to the high 30s and COMA-Instance loses its perfect streak, SMUTF peaks in the low-to-mid 90s, confirming its ability to handle complex semantic relationships better than methods that are highly sensitive to dataset conditioning.

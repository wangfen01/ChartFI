The heatmap visualizes the cross-modal cosine similarity between textual and visual features of 32 distinct CTPA abnormalities, offering insights into the anatomical relationships and alignment between the two modalities. Each cell indicates the similarity score between a specific abnormality’s textual and visual representation, with darker reds denoting stronger alignment. High similarity scores are concentrated in the Pulmonary Arteries, Heart, and specific Lung and Airway regions, reflecting their vascular interdependence. The most prominent structure is a strong, cohesive cluster within the pulmonary embolism family, where pulmonary embolism, acute and chronic PE, and main and lobar pulmonary artery PE show consistently high mutual association; they also align closely with enlarged pulmonary artery, suggesting these findings share significant visual and semantic characteristics. Outside the PE block, lung and airway findings show moderate internal coupling, with emphysema, atelectasis, lung opacity, and lung nodules tending to align at mid-level intensity, while bronchiectasis and airway wall-related thickening patterns appear more selectively connected. Pleural effusion stands out as a broad connector, showing relatively elevated associations across multiple lung and cardiac features compared with other pleural findings, whereas pneumothorax remains comparatively weakly linked to most other abnormalities. Cardiac and vascular features form another noticeable cluster where cardiomegaly, coronary artery calcification, right heart strain, and enlarged ascending aorta show moderate-to-strong alignment, with right heart strain particularly bridging toward PE-related findings. Conversely, mediastinal and upper GI findings such as lymphadenopathy and hiatal hernia show localized, moderate associations, while chest wall, neck, and bone findings—including soft tissue mass, thyroid abnormalities, and acute fractures—are generally low-intensity across the board, indicating they are less systematically tied to the cardiopulmonary patterns driving the strongest cross-modal signals.

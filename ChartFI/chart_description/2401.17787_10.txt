The three panels compare the normalized demands of one random retailer in August and September across 30 periods, contrasting real-life data represented by gray bars with synthetic data structures depicted by black lines representing Random, Trend, or Both patterns. Compared to the synthetic data, the real-life data has a clear pattern that demands usually occur on weekdays, but the specific demand quantity within each week shows no tendency. Additionally, the real-life data fluctuates greatly with some large orders visible as sporadic high outliers (Avg=0.19, Std=0.20) that none of the synthetic line patterns consistently capture. In the Random scenario, the synthetic line stays consistently higher on average (Avg=0.50) and is highly volatile (Std=0.21), featuring frequent sharp spikes and drops that do not align with the intermittent real-data bursts, indicating a mismatch in both level and timing. Conversely, the Trend case displays a clear repeating wave pattern with moderate variability (Avg=0.31, Std=0.18), where peaks occur at regular intervals to suggest structured seasonality rather than a true monotonic trend, though it still sits above the real-data baseline most of the time. The Both configuration combines these irregular jumps with periodic rises (Avg=0.39, Std=0.19), producing occasional pronounced surges around the mid-to-late periods and a late uptick. Across all panels, the most notable shift is the change in predictability: the Trend structure is the most regular, the Random structure is the most erratic, and the Both structure introduces clustered spikes that create localized anomalies. Ultimately, while the Both pattern is closer to the real-data variability than the Random pattern, all three synthetic data structures remain systematically higher in magnitude and fail to perfectly replicate the weekday-driven, highly fluctuating nature of the real-life retail demand.

This bar chart illustrates the success rates of obtaining 22 achievements in log scale across four distinct methods within a Minecraft-like environment, where the logarithmic y-axis signifies that even small visual differences represent order-of-magnitude gaps in performance. The red series, representing our method, is the most consistently high performer, frequently reaching the top range near 100% success on many collection and placement tasks, while DreamerV2 serves as the closest competitor but exhibits sharper drop-offs on several crafting and tool-making steps. PPO and Rainbow are markedly less stable, alternating between moderate success on a handful of easier objectives and near-zero performance on many multi-step tasks. Performance is strongest and most uniform across all methods on straightforward collection and late-stage placement goals such as collecting drink, sapling, wood, and placing a plant or table, where most bars cluster at the high end of the scale. However, the biggest separations appear on tasks that require longer dependency chains, especially iron-related crafting and intermediate tool creation, where our method shows significant improvements in unlocking and achieving deeper accomplishments as the number of training steps increases. A notable anomaly is the near-universal failure region around iron tool crafting steps, where only our method shows meaningful lift while others remain close to the floor, indicating that with a deeper understanding of the environment, the agent’s ability to achieve more challenging accomplishments also increases. Ultimately, the data suggests that the primary advantage of the proposed approach is not merely marginal gains on simple tasks but robustness on compound, prerequisite-heavy objectives, effectively reducing the steep performance cliffs seen in baseline methods and demonstrating that as the agent gains a deeper understanding of the environment, its capacity to navigate complex dependency chains improves substantially.

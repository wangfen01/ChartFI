The radar chart illustrates the performance of six leading language models across 20 diverse tasks in CLEAR, categorized into basic, intermediate, and advanced causal-graph reasoning skills with scores scaled to 100. Overall, GPT-4 forms the outer envelope on most axes, indicating the strongest consistency, yet the model’s ability to handle different causal graph-based tasks is uneven, exhibiting notable weaknesses in specific areas. Across the basic tasks, models generally perform well on local structures like Single Node and Single Edge, but GPT-4 distinguishes itself with a marked jump on the Cycle task, outperforming the tighter cluster of competitors. As the difficulty increases into the intermediate and advanced regions, a key anomaly emerges: the performance of most models is not constrained by task dependency. Despite the logical hierarchy where advanced tasks theoretically rely on foundational ones, performance on a dependent task often exceeds that of its prerequisite, showcasing a notable divergence in their performance trends. For example, while nearly all models compress toward lower scores on the Backdoor and Frontdoor Adjustment Sets, GPT-4 exhibits a significant spike on Causal Effect Identification, contradicting the trend of a shared ceiling on identification-heavy problems. This irregular scoring pattern, where models may struggle with intermediate concepts like Markov Equivalence yet succeed elsewhere, suggests heterogeneity in knowledge representation and application rather than a uniform degradation of reasoning skills.

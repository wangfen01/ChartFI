This chart illustrates the performance difference of prompting with English and Maltese instructions averaged across models with different training types and Maltese training, where each score with English prompting is subtracted from scores with Maltese prompting to examine the impact of providing models with more Maltese text through manually translated instructions. In the zero-shot evaluation, most tasks exhibit a performance degradation when using Maltese instructions, with scores clustering below zero, though the magnitude varies significantly by training setup. The IT/IT configuration represents the most pronounced anomaly, showing large negative drops exceeding 20% on classification-style benchmarks like Sentiment Analysis and nearly 15% on NLI-style tasks such as Taxi1500 and SIB-200, while other configurations remain closer to neutral. Conversely, the PT/NO setup serves as a notable exception, delivering clear gains of nearly 10% on Sentiment Analysis and over 5% on Flores-200, suggesting this training choice transfers more effectively to specific sentiment and translation settings even when other regimes regress. Transitioning to a one-shot evaluation, the distribution of performance differences generally tightens and shifts upward toward zero, indicating that providing a single in-context example reduces the penalties associated with Maltese instructions and stabilizes outcomes across tasks. Despite this recovery, certain tasks remain fragile, with Sentiment Analysis and SIB-200 still exhibiting notable negative swings for several training choices, signaling a persistent sensitivity to the training regime. Ultimately, the shift to one-shot evaluation dampens extreme negatives and reveals task-specific tradeoffs where translation and data-to-text tasks like Flores-200 and WebNLG are more likely to benefit from Maltese instructions, while classification benchmarks continue to exhibit the largest variance and downside risk depending on the model's training history.

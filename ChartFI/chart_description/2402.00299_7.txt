The charts display the relative importance of node features for the best-performing proposed model, GAT-LSTM-ATT, and the best-performing baseline model, XGB, alongside a detailed breakdown of how specific feature values influence predictions. As seen in the relative importance view, the presence of overdue payments holds the most significant relative importance compared to other features for both GAT-LSTM-ATT and XGB, although the GAT-LSTM-ATT model relies on it even more strongly. The FICO credit score has the second-highest relative contribution among the features, and for both models, the number of borrowers ranks as the third most crucial feature. A notable divergence in feature prioritization appears in the subsequent tier: for the GAT-LSTM-ATT model, the number of remaining months holds the fourth position in terms of importance, whereas for the XGB model, the debt-to-income ratio claims the fourth spot, suggesting the tree model leans more on static affordability signals while the sequence model concentrates on temporal dynamics. The SHAP summary for GAT-LSTM-ATT further elucidates these relationships through clear directional effects, where the presence of overdue payments produces large positive SHAP values, acting as the main trigger for sharp shifts toward predicting default. Regarding credit history, borrowers with high credit scores are less likely to default according to the model, while borrowers with low credit scores are more prone to be classified as defaulters. Similarly, high values of the number of borrowers are associated with lower default risk, while low values are associated with higher default risk. In contrast, a higher number of remaining months is linked with higher default risk, and high values for the debt-to-income ratio and current interest rate generally push the output upward. Most remaining variables cluster tightly around zero, implying marginal incremental contribution compared to these top-tier risk signals.

The chart tracks annual publication counts from 2013 to 2025, where different textures denote different study areas, and different colors denote different APBD methods. Output is minimal through 2016, rises modestly in 2017–2018, and then jumps sharply in 2019 to a major peak, indicating a step-change in research activity. After 2019, totals remain elevated with year-to-year fluctuations, including a slight dip in 2020, a recovery in 2021, a relatively stable plateau in 2022–2023, a new high in 2024, and a noticeable pullback in 2025, which stands out against the generally high post-2019 baseline and may reflect incomplete-year coverage. Method composition shifts materially over time; early years are dominated by traditional approaches, with traditional image processing-based and traditional machine learning-based methods contributing most of the limited volume. From 2019 onward, deep learning-based methods have become increasingly prominent, and by 2022–2024 they are the primary driver of higher totals. While traditional methods persist, their share shrinks, suggesting maturation and partial displacement rather than disappearance. Study-area patterns also broaden over time, and a key finding is that the recent surge in very large-scale research is predominantly driven by Deep Learning methods. In particular, the vast majority of studies covering extremely large regions (over 10,000 km^2) employ deep learning, with this pattern becoming especially evident after 2020. By contrast, while traditional image processing-based and traditional machine learning-based methods have been applied across various spatial scales including some large-area studies in earlier years, their presence in research addressing the largest geographical extents is far less frequent compared to the more recent deep learning-based approaches.

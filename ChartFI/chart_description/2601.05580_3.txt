The provided heatmaps compare the effectiveness of different continual-learning methods by displaying the test accuracy (ACC) for each task (x-axis) evaluated at the end of each sequential learning task (y-axis). The dominant structure across the panels is the diagonal, which depicts how performance evolves as the model sequentially learns new tasks; notably, all these methods, with the exception of A-GEM, achieved nearly 100% detection performance on new tasks. While this indicates that most models possess sufficient plasticity to learn the current task immediately, the off-diagonal regions reveal significant divergences in stability, specifically in the lower-left blocks where earlier tasks are re-evaluated after subsequent updates. Naive sequential training (Seq) exhibits pronounced forgetting, characterized by substantial degradation of earlier tasks and large low-score regions away from the diagonal. Regularization and replay-style baselines, including ER, EWC, OSLA, SI, and iCaRL, partially mitigate this decay but still suffer from residual interference that accumulates as the task gap increases. A-GEM proves less effective, showing uneven retention and failing to maintain the high diagonal scores seen in other baselines. Conversely, the method labeled "Ours" stands out by maintaining consistently high scores across both the diagonal and the off-diagonal areas, indicating materially better backward retention. These comparison results demonstrate that this method exhibits superior plasticity and stability, achieving the best performance after training on all tasks, with the main differentiator being the ability to prevent catastrophic forgetting over long horizons while preserving early tasks.

This heatmap illustrates the performance of various selected Membership Inference Attack (MIA) methods across different distributions of the PILE dataset, revealing that no single MIA works across distributions. While Max-10% Prob and Min-10% Prob remain relatively stable with small magnitudes, suggesting these probability-tail measures are not highly dataset-sensitive, other metrics demonstrate significant volatility. The Perturbation-based metric exhibits the strongest anomalies; while it performs well and achieves a high AUC (such as synonym substitution on PhilPapers, indicated by a strong positive value of 0.59), the same method has an AUC of less than 0.5 on the next dataset of PubMed Abstracts, dropping to a negative value of -0.26. This contradicts the assumption of consistent robustness, implying that effectiveness varies dramatically by dataset structure. Similarly, Perplexity shows clear dataset dependence, turning notably positive on Ubuntu and PhilPapers while dropping sharply negative on YTSubtitles, indicating substantial differences in text predictability across domains. Zlib Ratio is mostly near zero to mildly negative but stands out with a strong negative on PubMed Central. Ultimately, the data indicate that domain and formatting differences—distinguishing scientific texts like PhilPapers from conversational data like YTSubtitles—are primary drivers of metric behavior, and the failure of any single method to generalize warrants stratifying analyses by genre rather than relying on aggregate conclusions.

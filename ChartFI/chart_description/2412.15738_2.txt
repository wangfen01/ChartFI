The figure presents a comprehensive analysis of connectedness through three heatmaps and three grouped bar charts, where panels (a), (b), (c), (d), (e), and (f) represent the overall, contemporaneous, lagged, TO, FROM, and NET connectedness, respectively. In the heatmaps, the (i, j) element indicates the magnitude of the spillover effect from grain j to grain i, revealing a strongly uneven structure where a small number of pairings dominate the scale. From the perspectives of overall and contemporaneous connectedness in panels (a) and (b), the spillover of risk between U.S. and Brazilian soybeans is the strongest, with values peaking around the high 30s and dwarfing the rest of the matrix where most cells remain close to low single digits. Panel (c) shifts the focus to lagged connectedness, displaying a compressed range where most values cluster tightly around 0.2 to 1.5; however, specific relationships still stand out, notably ZAs with BRs and USs, implying that certain bilateral effects remain structurally stronger than the network baseline even in delayed terms. The bar charts reinforce these patterns by separating overall, contemporaneous, and lagged components, consistently showing that overall effects are the largest while lagged effects are generally the smallest. In terms of magnitude, the TO and FROM indices for U.S. and Brazilian soybeans are the highest, while China’s rice exhibits the lowest, indicating a system with significant heterogeneity in how strongly each region participates. Finally, panel (f) adds a directional view through NET connectedness, where several regions such as ZAs and ZAm show large negative overall contributions, acting as net absorbers, while others are strongly positive, confirming that U.S. and Brazilian soybeans act as consistent net transmitters of influence within the network.

The two radar charts present a visualization of fine-grained comparison with 10 models and human performance across nine spatial and scene-understanding sub-tasks, contrasting the impact of realistic versus semantic top-view map representations. Across both settings, the strongest dimensions are Object Recognition and Scene Recognition, where the top models expand outward the most; switching to semantic maps produces a modest but consistent uplift in these areas, suggesting that cleaner, label-driven inputs help models convert map cues into correct identifications. However, this improvement does not extend to all dimensions, as models still struggle with reasoning over dynamic relative spatial relations and counting tasks, which remain tightly clustered near the center in both charts. Crucially, the data demonstrates that most current models perform on par with the random baseline in spatial reasoning and have a large gap with human performance, indicating that semantic enrichment fails to resolve bottlenecks in multi-step relational inference. While the random baseline generally anchors the lower bound of performance, a notable anomaly appears where, for some models such as Qwen-VL, the results are sometimes much worse than the random baseline, signaling severe failures in specific reasoning contexts. Ultimately, while human performance forms the outer envelope across all axes, the persistent proximity of model performance to the random baseline in spatial and dynamic tasks highlights that current systems lack the fundamental grounding required for complex reasoning, regardless of the map representation used.

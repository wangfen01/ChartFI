The three panels illustrate the distribution of sample numbers across ten dataset classes, where each column represents an enterprise’s data distribution and different colors represent different enterprises. In panel (a), a lower α value leads to a more skewed distribution, resulting in a higher degree of non-IID data characterized by extreme imbalances where total sample counts swing sharply between classes—some exceeding 3,000 while others are very few—and specific enterprises dominate certain classes while contributing nothing to others. Panel (b) reflects a moderate shift where the distribution remains uneven but less extreme, with most classes clustering between 1,500 and 2,500 samples, though persistent class-specific volatility remains evident through a clear spike at class 4 and a pronounced dip at class 7. In contrast, panel (c) demonstrates how a larger α value creates a distribution closer to a uniform one, mimicking IID data; here, every class totals exactly 1,000 samples and the stacked segments are identical across all classes. This deliberate normalization stabilizes both overall class counts and individual enterprise contributions, effectively removing the earlier dominance patterns and reducing the risk that model performance is driven by class or source imbalance rather than underlying signal.

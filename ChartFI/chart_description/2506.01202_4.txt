The radiation beam patterns of the base station with M = 16 antennas illustrate the comparative performance of five different approaches across a direction range from −90° to 90°, specifically highlighting how each design allocates gain toward three radar targets while managing interference toward a primary user and two secondary users. The proposed SDR cognitive DFRC and the proposed FA cognitive DFRC, the latter of which is optimized with an FA population size of N = 40 over Θ = 140 generations with Pm = 1, track the radar-only reference closely at the target angles to produce strong mainlobes near −60°, 0°, and 40° while maintaining relatively low sidelobes. A critical distinction occurs at the primary user direction near −40° and the secondary user directions near −30° and 20°, where the cognitive DFRC solutions introduce pronounced nulls or sharp attenuation to satisfy an interference tolerance level at the PU of Ithres = 0.01 and a required SINR at each SU of ηi = 10 dB. While the non-cognitive DFRC generally follows the target peaks, it fails to enforce these deep, deliberate nulls at the PU and SU angles, resulting in higher residual sidelobe levels and less controlled notches. In contrast, the cognitive-comm-only pattern remains significantly lower overall with frequent deep ripples, indicating a lack of prioritization for target illumination in favor of broad power suppression. Notable anomalies include the very steep roll-off and deep attenuation near the endfire directions for the SDR cognitive DFRC, suggesting aggressive sidelobe control that reduces radiation at extreme angles. Ultimately, the cognitive DFRC approaches achieve the most effective joint sensing-communication beam shaping by preserving near-radar-only gain at targets while successfully carving out deep nulls at constrained directions to meet specific interference and SINR requirements.

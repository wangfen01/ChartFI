This visual analysis of the decomposition components on synthetic trend and seasonal datasets compares time-series reconstruction and anomaly scoring performance between the TSINR model and a variant without decomposition. In the synthetic trend dataset, the original series undergoes a sharp regime shift within the shaded anomaly window; while the TSINR model tracks the regular oscillations and adapts decisively to this level change, the no-decomposition variant lags behind, staying closer to the pre-shift baseline. This divergence is mirrored in the anomaly scores, where the no-decomposition approach produces higher and more volatile peaks within the anomaly window, suggesting a higher sensitivity that risks overreacting to the change point. The distinction becomes even more critical in the synthetic seasonal dataset, where omitting trend and seasonal components results in inadequate data fitting. Unlike the full TSINR model, which maintains high fidelity to the original periodic pattern, the no-decomposition variant fails to capture the signal's amplitude effectively across the full horizon. Consequently, the anomaly scores for the no-decomposition method show frequent, noisy spikes throughout the series, driven by an inability to account for normal seasonal structure, whereas TSINR keeps scores near zero for most timestamps and concentrates its response specifically around the anomaly interval. Collectively, these results demonstrate that incorporating trend and seasonal components significantly improves both reconstruction performance and anomaly detection capability, while omitting these components hinders the ability to detect anomalies by failing to separate background noise from genuine structural breaks.

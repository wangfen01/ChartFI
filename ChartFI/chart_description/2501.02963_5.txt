The chart tracks fundamental vs data-driven models' forecasts for Germany (2024) regarding electricity prices over March 21 to 25, comparing the true price with a data-driven estimate and a fundamental model, while stacked areas show renewable production from PV, onshore wind, and offshore wind. Prices are high and volatile through Thursday and Friday, with repeated spikes above roughly 100 EUR/MWh. During these periods, for short-term forecasting, fundamental models are often described as too flat meaning that they fail to capture the high volatility of power prices, visibly underestimating the magnitude of weekday extremes. The data-driven model is more reactive and closer to the sharp swings during these high-price intervals. However, prices shift sharply downward over the weekend as wind output rises and PV peaks during the daytime, highlighting a dominant inverse relationship between renewable generation and price. This transition from Friday to Saturday coincides with a rapid collapse from mid-range prices to sustained low or negative levels, suggesting periods of renewable-driven oversupply and heightened price sensitivity during low-demand weekend hours. In these conditions, unlike data-driven models, fundamental models are able to capture price clusters, which often form around zero in markets with a high share of RES, as demonstrated by the fundamental model accurately tracking the zero-price floor on Saturday and Sunday. Conversely, data-driven models can fail to capture the clustering of prices around zero and tend to overshoot actual prices, most notably producing much deeper negative excursions than the true price during the weekend dip.

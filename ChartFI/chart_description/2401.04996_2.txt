The three panels illustrate the performance of various optimization methods across nine network topologies—geant, abilene, dtelekom, ER, HC, SW, grid, BT, and star—measured by aggregate utility, infeasibility, and estimation error. Aggregate utility varies widely by network, peaking on ER, HC, and SW where most methods cluster at the top end, while geant and grid sit noticeably lower and BT and star show mid-to-lower utilities. Within these topologies, DFW and DPGA perform very well in terms of maximizing the utility in all networks, and their performances are close to their centralized versions: FW and PGA. In contrast, the MaxTP and MaxFair families generally trail, with fairness-oriented variants sacrificing utility most visibly on ER, HC, and SW where the gap to the best-performing methods is largest. Infeasibility reveals a sharp separation between methods, as MaxTP and MaxFair variants frequently achieve the lowest levels near the bottom of the log scale, whereas DFW and DPGA show higher residual values. Despite this, DFW and DPGA maintain an acceptable infeasibility ~ 0.1 across several networks, including abilene, dtelekom, and SW, indicating that their higher utility is obtained with manageable constraint satisfaction. A notable shift appears on grid and star, where infeasibility for some methods drops dramatically, suggesting these topologies are easier to satisfy or that certain algorithms exploit their structure better. Estimation error is comparatively stable but shows that DFW and DPGA perform very well in terms of minimizing the estimation error in all networks, with results again closely mirroring the centralized FW and PGA baselines. Errors are highest on geant, abilene, dtelekom, grid, BT, and star, and lowest on ER and HC, implying those random or highly connected graphs are easier to estimate accurately. MaxTP and MaxFair variants often sit at the higher end of estimation error, reinforcing the pattern that methods emphasizing feasibility or fairness can pay a price in both predictive accuracy and utility. Overall, ER and HC serve as best-case networks with simultaneously high utility and low error, whereas grid and star act as stress tests where utility degrades and algorithmic differences in feasibility become more pronounced.

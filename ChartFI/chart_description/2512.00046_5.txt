The analysis begins by examining the average ratings associated with both human coders (CoderA, CoderB, CoderC) and various LLM models, segmented by sentence difficulty (Easy, Medium, Difficult). A clear difficulty gradient is visible across the dataset, where easy sentences are generally rated highest, medium slightly lower, and difficult lowest for nearly every entity. Within this trend, the data highlights that LLMs generally receive higher ratings on easy sentences compared to human coders, while humans excel in coding more complex sentences, maintaining more consistent scores where models like Mistral and Vicuna exhibit sharp declines. The overall average line reflects this shift, trending downward through the model group and bottoming out around Mistral, whose especially low average on difficult sentences suggests that increased complexity amplifies differences in scoring standards. Complementing this analysis, the deviation evaluation presents the DGS results for both human coders and LLMs across different sentence difficulties, visualizing positive and negative deviations from the golden standard. Coder A, in particular, consistently showed positive deviation across easy, medium, and difficult categories, implying a systematically more generous scoring tendency than the reference. Conversely, several models display negative skews that worsen with complexity; the most significant anomaly is Mistral’s strongly negative difficult DGS, which is far more extreme than its easy and medium deviations. While the generally high average of DGS absolutes signals meaningful rater-to-rater variability overall, the distinct pattern of larger negative swings on difficult items indicates that sentence difficulty is the primary driver of divergence in scoring behavior.

The visualization of skill distribution in the dataset and evaluation under the Near-Optimal setting in the MT50 setup compares two heatmaps of skill intensity across tasks, with the left panel showing the distribution of skills corresponding to various tasks in the dataset and the right panel showing the distribution employed by the agent across different tasks during evaluation on a 0 to 1 scale. This result reveals the imbalance in skill classes, where some skills are widely used across tasks, while others are unique to a small subset, a skill class imbalance that exists within the dataset and during evaluation. In the dataset, most task–skill cells are very light, indicating broadly low and diffuse skill activation with only occasional moderate patches, and while each task in the dataset contains fragments of trajectories for multiple skills, specific skills are not employed during evaluation. In contrast, the evaluation map becomes noticeably more contrasty, with more frequent mid-to-dark green cells scattered across many tasks and several skills showing repeated higher-intensity occurrences, suggesting a shift toward harder or more skill-demanding task coverage. Across panels, the key change is an increase in both the frequency and magnitude of higher skill values during evaluation, such as in Task 2 (bin-picking-v2), where the dataset contains limited data for Skill 0, yet the agent successfully utilizes Skill 0 to complete the task during evaluation. The evaluation panel also shows isolated near-maximum spikes that stand out as anomalies relative to the dataset’s mostly low background, implying that certain task–skill combinations are disproportionately emphasized or more challenging in evaluation. Overall, the evaluation set appears less representative of the dataset’s softer skill profile and more likely to stress-test specific skills intermittently, which could lead to performance differences driven by these concentrated high-skill pockets rather than a uniform shift across all skills.

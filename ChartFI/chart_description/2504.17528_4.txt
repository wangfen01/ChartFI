The chart compares the cumulative local training time required by different algorithms to achieve a target accuracy across six datasets, with performance normalized against the FedAvg algorithm, which serves as the 1.0 baseline. The visualization also marks instances of convergence failure (x) or timeout (◦). Across all datasets, TACO consistently proves to be the most time-efficient one among all the algorithms, successfully reducing 25.6% - 62.7% client computation time to reach the target accuracy compared to FedAvg. In stark contrast, the STEM algorithm is prone to significant inefficiency and instability; it can require up to over 80% more local computation time on the client side than FedAvg, leading to a timeout on the Adult, and it exhibits the largest slowdown on the Shakespeare dataset, peaking at over 1.4 times the baseline. Similarly, FedProx demonstrates considerable instability with frequent convergence failures on the SVHN, CIFAR-10, and Shakespeare datasets, and it runs slower than the baseline when it does complete, as seen on CIFAR-100. The performance of other methods like Scaffold and FedACG varies significantly by dataset; while they are faster than the baseline on the Adult dataset, they become notably slower on FMNIST and CIFAR-10, underscoring a broader pattern where the computational advantage of many algorithms diminishes on more complex or heterogeneous tasks, while TACO maintains its efficiency.

These panels present a comparison of 30-min and 45-minute-ahead predictions on nodes #80 and #100, respectively, tracking speed over time with a ground-truth series compared against two model variants. The solid red line represents the ground truth, while the dashed green line (‘‘no-adj’’) corresponds to predictions by STGCN without autocorrelated error adjustment, and the dashed blue line (‘‘SAEA’’) shows predictions with the proposed spatiotemporal autocorrelated error adjustment. Additionally, the shaded areas indicate the magnitude of prediction errors; specifically, the green-shaded area represents the prediction errors in the original STGCN model, while the purple-shaded area indicates errors after applying SAEA. In each case, the models follow the overall regime structure well, characterized by an early rise into a long mid-period plateau around the mid-to-high 60s, followed by a late decline and more volatile behavior. The most meaningful differentiations occur at transition points. In the first scenario, there is a sharp early acceleration around t≈25–40 where both models briefly lag and error spikes, followed by a stable stretch from roughly t≈50–140 where errors stay low and the lines nearly overlap. A pronounced shift appears around t≈145–170 as speed drops and oscillates; here the no-adj model drifts lower and becomes noisier, and its error grows more persistently than SAEA. In the second scenario, the plateau is similarly well captured until a major breakdown around t≈140–175 where speed falls steeply and rebounds, producing the largest sustained errors in the figure. Here, the no-adj model shows broader, higher green error bands and more deviation from truth, while SAEA remains closer and recovers faster. Overall, while both approaches perform comparably during steady-state cruising, the results clearly demonstrate that the incorporation of SAEA significantly reduces prediction errors, thereby enhancing the accuracy and robustness of the forecasting model. By effectively reducing prediction errors through accounting for spatiotemporal dependencies, SAEA proves consistently more robust during abrupt accelerations and decelerations where the original model exhibits larger and longer-lasting error bursts.

This set of graphs illustrates the correlation between the task completion time of the UAV and its speed, as well as the communication rate, by presenting UAVs’ speed and communication rate over time across three distinct navigation methods: Region division, Shortest distance criterion, and EBTAS. A consistent behavioral pattern emerges across the speed profiles, where the UAV predominantly operates at maximum speed along most sections of the route, hovering near the upper range of 15 to 19 m/s with intermittent slowdowns. Despite these sustained high velocities, the corresponding average communication rates reveal that there is a communication interruption during the flight, characterized by highly bursty data transfer and frequent dropouts to near zero. Under the Region division approach, connectivity is particularly inconsistent with occasional spikes up to roughly 8 to 9 Mbps, suggesting that region-based routing produces periodic coverage gaps even when the UAVs are moving fast. Moving to the Shortest distance criterion, UAV b tends to hold higher speeds more consistently early on, while UAV a exhibits deeper mid-run dips; here, communication rates become active with fewer prolonged zero-rate intervals than in Region division, indicating that distance-driven movement improves opportunities for strong links but still suffers from intermittent outages. EBTAS demonstrates the most effective coupling between trajectory and throughput, where communication rates exhibit more sustained mid-level performance with repeated high peaks, especially later in the run where UAV a reaches near the top of the scale. A critical observation across all methods is that rate spikes do not align simply with higher speed; instead, the strongest rates often occur during or after speed adjustments, implying that trajectory positioning and link alignment dominate throughput.

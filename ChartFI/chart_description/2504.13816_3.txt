The two heatmaps visualize the transferability matrices of a probing model on Qwen2.5-7B’s 3rd and 19th layers, which exhibit the worst and best average transferability across languages, respectively, for English, Chinese, Vietnamese, Thai, Khmer, Indonesian, and Malay. The y-axis and x-axis represent the languages used for training and inference of the probing models, respectively. In the early layer (3/28), the model displays a highly uneven, language-pair-dependent structure where high and low-resource languages exhibit distinct performance and transferability patterns. Specifically, high-resource languages achieve superior ID accuracy but transfer poorly to low-resource languages; Chinese, for example, shows strong self-similarity (0.91) but exceptionally weak transfer to all other languages (scores around 0.25-0.28), making it the clearest outlier. English also shows high in-domain accuracy (0.87), but its transfer performance degrades across other pairings. In contrast, some linguistically related languages like Malay and Indonesian exhibit strong mutual alignment (~0.80-0.83), while other pairings reveal pronounced transfer difficulties, such as from Malay to Khmer (0.29) and Indonesian to Khmer (0.40). By the much later layer (19/28), the matrix shifts dramatically to a broadly uniform, high-similarity structure, with scores mostly ranging between 0.72 and 0.89, indicating that representations become substantially more aligned and converge toward a shared multilingual semantic space. The most significant change is the integration of Chinese, which rises from its isolated state to achieve high transferability in the low-to-mid 0.80s with most languages. While most language-specific barriers dissolve, Khmer retains a modest residual distinctiveness, tending to be a slightly weaker transfer target with scores dipping to 0.72 from Indonesian, though these values are far above the early-layer lows.

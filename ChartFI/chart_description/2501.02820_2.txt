The simulation results present the Mean-Squared Error (MSE) for the RAQ-ESPRIT, RAQ-ML, and RAQ-CRLB methods alongside their classical counterparts for ULAs, covering variations in reflected power, sensor count, target count, sample size, DOA range, and phase shift. To demonstrate the limitations of standard approaches, the charts also plot the curve of directly applying the classical ESPRIT to the RAQ-ULA to show its infeasibility. Upon varying the power reflected from the targets, the RAQ-based methods dramatically outperform the baselines, and the RAQ-ESPRIT exhibits a significant reduction in the MSE, which can be on the order of 450-fold and 10000-fold in the PSL and SQL regimes, respectively. As the number of sensors (M) increases, all the MSE curves decrease, but the RAQ-ESPRIT significantly outperforms its classical counterpart, where the MSE is reduced on the order of 442-fold and 9863-fold in the PSL and SQL regimes, respectively. Regarding scalability, it is observed that all curves increase as the number of targets (K) grows, whereas the MSE decreases as the number of samples (N) increases; in both scenarios, RAQ-ESPRIT has a much lower MSE, showing a significant MSE reduction of > 400-fold and > 9000-fold in the PSL and SQL regimes, respectively. The DOA sweep reveals that the RAQ-ESPRIT more significantly reduces the MSE than its classical counterpart over the whole DOA range, exhibiting 418-fold and 9324-fold reductions in the PSL and SQL regimes, respectively. Finally, the phase shift analysis reveals a critical anomaly where the MSE in the PSL regime becomes unbounded when phi(Omega_l) / (2*pi) = 90 degrees, while it is minimally achieved when phi(Omega_l) / (2*pi) = 0 degrees or pm 180 degrees, whereas SQL-based variants remain stable and low.

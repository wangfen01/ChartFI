The charts illustrate the proportion of scholars who return to their home countries after migrating, compared to the total number of scholars who migrate across six continents, distinguishing between return migration based on academic origin (top panel) and the country of machine learning (ML)-based name origin (bottom panel). These distributions are stratified by female scholars (circles) and male scholars (diamonds), relative to the size of the population of scholars and the time period. A comparative analysis reveals that the mean proportion of return migration based on ML name origin was higher than that found based on academic origin, evidenced by a general shift in points toward the right in the bottom panel compared to the top panel. While similar patterns were observed across most continents, return migration proportions were much higher based on name origin for Oceania, North America, and Europe. This shift is most pronounced in North America within the bottom panel, where an extreme right tail emerges with values approaching 0.45 to above 0.50; specifically, nearly half of the scholars leaving the USA return to their country of ML name origin. This distinct cluster indicates that these countries have a much higher share of foreign graduate students and scholars who start their publishing careers in these countries and later return to their countries of origin. Throughout the distributions, there is a consistent tendency for male values to sit to the right of female values, indicating higher inferred proportions for men across most regions, while later years are often represented by larger bubbles and slightly more right-shifted points in North America and parts of Europe.

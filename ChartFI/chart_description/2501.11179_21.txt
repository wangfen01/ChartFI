The chart tracks how different memory-management policies affect available VA-backed memory and the corresponding performance of CACHE and KV-STORE workloads during two contention events. Initially, available oversubscribed memory starts near 6 GB, drops as VMs start, and then gradually declines to roughly 1.5–2 GB. The first contention starts at 135 seconds, causing most policies to sharply reduce available memory to near-zero. This event highlights the cost of inaction, as doing nothing degrades performance by up to 4.3x for the KV-STORE workload, while the other workloads experience less degradation. In contrast, proactive policies reduce this overhead to only 1.3x by reducing the duration and intensity of the contention, keeping slowdowns close to the baseline with only modest transient bumps. Following this first event, proactive variants stabilize with a small but steady memory cushion, whereas reactive variants show more volatility and brief dips, indicating delayed or oscillatory responses. The second contention at 270 seconds reveals a notable shift: the Trim-Reactive policy exhibits a pronounced slowdown spike, especially for the KV-STORE workload where it rises to around 4x, suggesting that trimming under renewed pressure can overcorrect and harm latency-sensitive behavior. Conversely, the Extend and Migrate strategies remain much closer to the 1x baseline, and a late-period jump in available memory for these policies coincides with their stable, low slowdowns, consistent with successful reclamation or rebalancing that restores headroom without reintroducing performance penalties.

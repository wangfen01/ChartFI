The confusion matrix heatmap of labels, with colors normalized by row, compares gold labels against predicted labels to reveal classification performance across various discourse relations. Overall, ELABORATION is the most frequent label, and the chart is characterized by a strong diagonal where high-volume categories such as topic-change, structuring, adversative, and temporal are correctly identified with high frequency. While these dominant classes show strong separability, mid-frequency relations like causal, attribution, and enablement also exhibit significant diagonal mass, though they display more spillover than the primary topic-based and structural classes. A notable pattern of systematic confusion exists among semantically adjacent functions, particularly where the probe has a bias of confusing ELABORATION with labels such as FRAMING and EXPLANATION. This suggests that the model struggles to differentiate between content expansion (ELABORATION) which sometimes overlaps semantically with EXPLANATION, and relations that involve additional information but with a primary focus on contextualization (FRAMING). Furthermore, because ELABORATION is also a highly prevalent relation in general, it serves as a probable default prediction in ambiguous cases, contributing to the visible mixing among explanation, evaluation, and elaboration. Other localized errors include cross-confusion between framing and mode, as well as a minor overlap between reformulation and structuring. The significant class imbalance, where labels like ELABORATION and topic-change account for much larger volumes than rarer categories like comparison and contingency, likely inflates accuracy for frequent classes while leaving smaller ones more prone to being absorbed by their high-frequency neighbors. These clustered confusions indicate that model improvements would require better feature signals to navigate the fine-grained boundaries between content expansion, contextual framing, and interpretive discourse functions.

The chart compares balanced accuracy across three classification tasks of increasing granularity—tumor category (7 classes), tumor family (10 classes), and tumor type (9 classes)—using two MIL aggregation methods (ABMIL and CLAM) and three feature extractors (ResNet50, CONCH, and UNI). Overall performance is driven far more by the choice of feature extractor than by the aggregation method, with UNI consistently being the top performer, followed closely by CONCH, while ResNet50 lags substantially across every task. As the granularity of tumor classification increased, the overall model performance decreased, independently of the instance feature extractor or MIL aggregation method. The largest decrease in performance is seen when transitioning from tumor category to family classification, whereas the performance drop was less pronounced when going from tumor families to types. Notably, the models using UNI and ResNet50 extracted features experienced the smallest drop in performance compared to the models using CONCH features as task complexity increased. The effect of the aggregation method is secondary; CLAM tends to yield slightly higher accuracy than ABMIL for ResNet50 but is broadly comparable for CONCH and UNI. This is further supported by the statistical significance shown when comparing models within classification tasks, where (*) indicates significance lower than 0.05 and (**) lower than 0.001. These annotations reveal frequent and highly significant differences across feature extractors, but more limited and less pronounced separations across aggregation methods. Additionally, performance variability differs by feature set, as UNI and CONCH have tighter interquartile ranges suggesting more stable performance, whereas ResNet50 exhibits a wider spread, especially on the more granular tasks.

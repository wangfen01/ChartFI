This correlogram displays pairwise correlations between rating and corpus-based measures of the Literary Metaphors module, where the strength of associations is represented by color—red for positive and blue for negative correlations—and significant, FDR-corrected correlations are marked by asterisks (* p < .05, ** p < .01, *** p < .001). Within these ratings, difficulty and meaningfulness were strongly negatively correlated (r(113) = -.88 for isolated metaphors and r(63) = -.73 for metaphors in context), suggesting that items perceived as hard are judged as significantly less meaningful. Familiarity was moderately negatively correlated with difficulty (r(113) = -.59 for isolated metaphors; r(63) = -.37 for metaphors evaluated within context) and moderately positively correlated with metaphor concreteness (r(113) = .45 and r(63) = .64, respectively). Notably, familiarity was moderately positively correlated with meaningfulness only for literary metaphors rated in isolation (r(113) = .67), while the association was negligible for metaphors embedded in context (r(63) = .08). Furthermore, difficulty was moderately negatively correlated with metaphor concreteness only for literary metaphors evaluated within context (r(63) = -.43), an association not found for the set of metaphorical expressions rated in isolation (r(113) = -.20). While topic length shows modest negative relationships with topic frequency and topic concreteness, these surface properties are secondary to the dominant rating-based patterns where predictability, familiarity, and concreteness cluster together in opposition to difficulty.

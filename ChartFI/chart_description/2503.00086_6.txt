This chart summarizes the results of humans and CNNs for five bar chart tasks without and with the largest perturbations, comparing Mean Log Absolute Error (MLAE) across a set of visual attributes such as title position, stroke width, and bar color. In the baseline condition, the data reveal a distinct performance gap: we see that the MLAE values of humans are significantly larger than those of CNNs for all parameters without perturbation. Specifically, the human results are relatively stable and clustered between roughly 1.1 and 2.2 depending on the chart type, whereas the CNN no-perturbation condition is almost flat and strongly negative across all types, indicating a systematic offset where the model initially outperforms humans. However, the introduction of visual noise reveals a stark contrast in robustness. While human performance degrades gradually and predictably—with a modest upward shift and increased spread, particularly in Type 4 where values peak around the low 3s—the CNN demonstrates extreme volatility. Consequently, the MLAE values of humans are smaller than those of CNNs for some parameters with perturbations. This brittleness is most pronounced in Type 4, where the CNN-with-perturbation results swing widely, showing the largest spikes above 4 and even reaching nearly 6 for attributes like stroke width. This pattern indicates that while Type 4 is slightly more sensitive for people, it interacts with specific visual attributes in a way that destabilizes the model far more significantly, causing the CNN to shift from a superior uniform baseline to extreme, attribute-dependent errors.

These comparative bar charts illustrate the performance of four causal discovery algorithms—FGES, PC, PCMCI+, and LPCMCI—across various synthetic time-series datasets, evaluating them through the True Positive Rate (TPR), False Discovery Rate (FDR), and Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) to assess how each method handles linear versus nonlinear relations and potential unobserved confounders. Performance is highly dataset-dependent, with no single method dominating all three metrics, though a classic trade-off often appears where gains in recall are accompanied by more false discoveries and larger structural errors. On the True Positive Rate, PCMCI+ and LPCMCI achieve the strongest recall on the A-series datasets, with PCMCI+ reaching near-perfect detection on A1-G and LPCMCI peaking on A1C-G; however, TPR drops sharply for the B1C variants and remains modest for C1C-G, indicating these settings are harder for all methods to recover true edges. Confounding generally degrades performance, especially in combination with nonlinearity as seen in the B1C and C1C variants, where even LPCMCI, which is designed to mitigate latent confounding effects, sees its TPR remain low while its FDR can spike significantly. In simpler linear scenarios, LPCMCI performs well by suppressing spurious edges, such as achieving a TPR of 1.0 and an FDR of 0.25 on A1C with Gaussian errors, demonstrating its potential when model assumptions align closely with the underlying data structure. The False Discovery Rate reveals a primary tension in the B and C configurations, where FDR for most methods clusters near the top of the scale for B1-G, B1-T, C1-G, and C1C-G, implying many predicted edges are incorrect even when TPR is moderate. Structural Hamming Distance further highlights these divergences, showing that while SHD is low and tightly grouped for A1-G and A1-T, it spikes dramatically for B1-T and C1C-G, with PCMCI+ showing especially large errors in these hardest regimes. Ultimately, the A1 datasets represent the most stable and reliable recovery across all three metrics, whereas the combination of high FDR and very high SHD in other datasets signals not just extra edges but a broadly incorrect structural recovery.

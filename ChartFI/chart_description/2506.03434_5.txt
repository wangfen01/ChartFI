These per-layer heatmaps illustrate the frequency with which attention heads from four distinct roles—general, entity, relation-answer, and answer-specific—switch to a different role, with darker cells indicating higher counts up to a maximum of ten. While overall activity is sparse and patchy across all panels, the density and intensity of these switches clearly increase as the heads become more task-targeted, with the entity category showing the lightest footprint and the general category displaying slightly more active but isolated hotspots. A significant shift occurs in the relation-answer and answer-specific maps, where both the number of active cells and the frequency of darker peaks rise noticeably, particularly in the early and late layers which undergo frequent switching. In contrast, the middle layers, specifically layers 10–18, remain comparatively stable with significantly fewer switching events. This distribution creates a consistent concentration of activity near the bottom and top of the plots, suggesting that heads in these regions are more frequently implicated when switching behavior is tied directly to answering. Furthermore, the presence of pronounced, isolated dark squares in the mid-to-upper layers identifies specialized heads that switch far more often than their neighbors, standing out against an otherwise diffuse background. Because there is no smooth gradient across head indices, these patterns imply that switching is driven by specific, highly active heads rather than a uniform layer-wide shift, highlighting the functional volatility of certain components during task execution.

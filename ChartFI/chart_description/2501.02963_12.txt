The optimized merit order has a more complex shape than the classical version, featuring exponential tails and a flat middle section. Low-cost and must-run generation dominates the initial volumes, with renewables contributing at very low or even negative implied marginal costs, keeping the curve flat through roughly the first half of demand. Around a load level in the low 60s MWh, the merit order steps up sharply into the upper exponential tail as higher-cost thermal units, such as gas and lignite, drive a steep increase in marginal cost toward the upper end of the stack. The clearing price of approximately 49 EUR/MWh sits right at the point where the system transitions from predominantly low-cost supply to more expensive conventional generation. This structure mitigates the issues of the classical model, improves the representation of price volatility at both extremes, and captures clustering around zero due to the flat segment. These dynamics are reflected in the forecast comparison, which highlights strong intraday seasonality with repeated daily peaks and troughs, followed by a pronounced regime shift over the weekend when prices fall dramatically to near-zero and negative levels. Across the week, the data-driven merit order tracks the true series more closely than the classic merit order, better capturing the timing and magnitude of most peaks and the weekend collapse. The superior performance of the data-driven merit order model over established machine-learning benchmarks like Lasso—which tends to overshoot some peaks and shows larger deviations during the most volatile low-price intervals—is noteworthy, especially despite not using autoregressive inputs. Nonetheless, the forecast results indicate that each model captures distinct aspects of the pricing dynamics during anomalies like the weekend plunge, suggesting potential for model combination to further optimize predictions.

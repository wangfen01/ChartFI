A visual comparison of predicted ride-hailing departure passenger flow against ground truth for MM-STFlowNet and three other methods (iTransformer, ST-GDN, and TimeMixer) reveals distinct performance differences across both routine daily cycles and a significant holiday surge around January 1, 2024. The time series displays a strong repeating intraday pattern with low overnight troughs and sharp daytime peaks, punctuated by an extreme spike in demand during the New Year's holiday. In terms of the overall trend, MM-STFlowNet is closest to the true value, consistently tracking the ground truth line more tightly than the other models. The primary distinction emerges during abrupt, high-amplitude demand shocks, as shown in the zoomed-in comparison around New Year's Day. Through the contrast of the spikes highlighted by the dotted black frame, MM-STFlowNet approximates the real peak with the minimum error; for example, on New Year’s Day, the peak passenger flow of ride-hailing departure is predicted to be around 3,200, closely matching the ground truth in both timing and magnitude, whereas competing models tend to underpredict the surge and misalign during the subsequent decline. This superior performance is quantified by the statistics on the percentage increase in error metrics, which show that the other three methods exhibit substantial performance degradation relative to MM-STFlowNet. The increases range from a minimum of 16.8% for Mean Absolute Error (MAE) to a maximum of 70.1% for Mean Absolute Error (MAE). The particularly large increases in MSE underscore that the main differentiator is the model's responsiveness to nonstationary, holiday-like demand, as alternatives struggle most with high-impact outliers and extreme peaks, creating large squared-error penalties.

The chart compares the matching accuracy of various vision model backbones, grouped by pretraining family, when paired with the all-mpnet-base-v2 language model on the CIFAR-10 dataset (left) and the All-Roberta-large-v1 language model on the CINIC-10 dataset (right). Most vision representations can be matched with high accuracy to language, as most models perform better than 10% accuracy, which is the baseline for random permutations shown by the dashed line. A dominant pattern emerges where the DINOv2 pretraining strategy achieves the highest accuracy, on average, with its top model reaching a matching accuracy of 80% on CIFAR-10 and 100% on CINIC-10. On CIFAR-10, there is a clear performance separation among families: DINOv2 models are the top performers, followed by ConvNeXt, DINO, and DeiT in the mid-to-high range, while CLIP variants cluster in the lower 0.2-0.5 band. When shifting to the CINIC-10 dataset, accuracies generally increase, but so does performance variance for certain backbones. Here, CLIP shows a much wider dispersion, and several models across different families exhibit very large uncertainty bars, signaling instability across runs despite high point estimates. While ConvNeXt and DeiT models frequently land in the high-accuracy region, DINOv2 again leads, with several variants saturating near the maximum accuracy. Curiously, it is the pretraining strategy that seems to have a larger impact than the model size, as performance clusters more strongly by family than by backbone architecture, and rankings within families do not consistently favor larger models.

The distribution of response strategies across QUERYSHIFT and CASE-Bench under three settings—query only (no motivation), query with benign motivation, and query with malicious motivation—reveals that LLMs demonstrate sophisticated harm-mitigation behaviors rather than simply alternating between full compliance and direct refusal. On QUERYSHIFT, behavior is heavily dominated by Full Compliance in the Query Only and Benign settings for nearly every model, with the notable exception of OLMo-2-32B, which is the most conservative and shows a larger share of refusal-like behavior even when content is not malicious. The most significant shift on QUERYSHIFT occurs in the Malicious condition, where models diverge sharply as several move away from Full Compliance toward Direct Refusal and Redirection, while others retain substantial Partial Compliance. On CASE-Bench, the distributions are more mixed and generally more conservative across all three conditions, with much larger portions of Direct Refusal, Redirection, and Explanation-Based Refusal even for Query Only and Benign prompts. Across both datasets, the evaluated LLMs show different levels of refusals and strategy preferences, with GPT-4 models using Direct Refusal the most, while Claude-3.7-Sonnet prefers Redirection. Ultimately, the dataset effect remains the most prominent pattern, as QUERYSHIFT makes most models look highly compliant until malicious intent is explicit, whereas CASE-Bench reveals more frequent refusal and strategy switching even in non-malicious contexts, highlighting that safety posture is strongly benchmark-dependent.

The chart displays the normalized wall-clock time latency for each sparse workload—specifically DS, GAT, GCN, GSABT, H2O, MK, SCN, and ST—across four distinct numeric formats: INT8, FP16, INT32, and INT32+NSB. Within each workload group, each bar from left to right denotes execution in density, in-order (InO) execution, out-of-order (OoO) execution, Stream, IMP, DVR, and NVR, respectively. Structurally, the lower segment of these bars indicates the NPU base execution time, whilst the upper segment represents the stall time caused by cache misses. By evaluating different bit-width configurations to analyze their impact on cache behavior and system performance, the data reveals that higher data bit-width inherently requires longer data movement time and larger cache block capacities, increasing the probability of cache misses. This trend is visible as INT32 and INT32+NSB generally exhibit increased total latency compared to the INT8 and FP16 baselines, often pushing results closer to or above the red dashed reference line. Workloads such as GAT, GSABT, and H2O appear most sensitive to these shifts, where moving to higher precision markedly raises normalized latency and alters the overhead mix. In contrast, ST remains consistently high and relatively flat across formats, implying it is dominated by base execution time with limited opportunity for format-level gains, while SCN shows a recurring pattern of elevated totals driven by non-execution components. Notably, even with ideal OoO execution on NPUs, the analysis shows suboptimal performance in handling cache misses, signaling that reduced precision alone does not guarantee meeting latency targets when pipeline or memory transfer overheads remain material.

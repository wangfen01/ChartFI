These two heatmaps illustrate the impact of the iteration number on the Parallel Quality Gradient Descent (PQGD) by tracking the Q-ACC metric across 50 training epochs for the GR-convnet model, with the upper sub-figure representing the Cornell Grasp dataset and the lower sub-figure representing the OCID Grasp dataset. In these visualizations, darker cells indicate higher accuracy, and the maximum quality score for each row is printed in white numbers for emphasis to highlight the peak performance achieved at specific epoch-iteration intersections. Overall, the data reveal that different numbers of iterations consistently lead to an improvement in Q-ACC, though the stability and magnitude of these gains vary by dataset. The Cornell dataset demonstrates consistently strong performance with most values clustered in the high 0.8s to mid 0.9s and frequent peaks reaching as high as 0.994, suggesting that the AQP is optimized effectively with stable learning and good generalization. In contrast, the OCID dataset exhibits greater variability and a wider spread of values, including more mid-to-low 0.8 regions and occasional dips near the lower end of the scale, indicating a more challenging dataset or less stable convergence. Across the 50 epochs, neither dataset shows a strictly monotonic improvement; instead, accuracy fluctuates with intermittent high-performing pockets, though higher-accuracy patches become more prevalent in the mid-to-late training window. On Cornell, several iterations reach the upper 0.96–0.97 range in later epochs, implying that extended training consolidates performance despite inherent noise. While OCID also produces strong spikes approaching the 0.98 range, these are interspersed with lighter bands that reflect inconsistent gains and a higher sensitivity to the specific iteration number. This iteration-dependent stability is a notable pattern in both charts but is more pronounced on OCID, where the contrast between strong and weak iterations is sharper and sudden performance drops amid otherwise solid epochs are more frequent.

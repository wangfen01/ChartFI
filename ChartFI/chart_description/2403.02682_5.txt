The figure compares real test sequences (blue) against generated samples (red) across four domains—Air Quality, ECG, Electricity, and Traffic—evaluating both time-series alignment and distributional fit for the Time Weaver model versus a GAN baseline. The top two rows display the quality and realism of the time series data, demonstrating that the Time Weaver model produces time series samples that are highly similar to real samples, effectively capturing broad temporal structure and central tendencies. In contrast, GAN baseline models face challenges in generating realistic time series, often struggling with sharp regime changes and amplitude scaling. A notable example is their performance with ECG signals; while the Time Weaver model generates a pristine, realistic sample that reproduces the sparse, high-amplitude spikes, the GAN model only learns to generate a noisy version of the ECG samples. Similarly, in the Electricity dataset, which features a pronounced step-up into a sustained high-demand plateau, the GAN baseline compresses the amplitude and leads to systematic underestimation, whereas Time Weaver tracks the step change more faithfully. Extending the analysis to the bottom two rows, which transform the datasets into histograms to compare distributions, the Time Weaver model provides a significantly more accurate representation of the real-time series distribution than the best-performing GAN model. The GAN models consistently fail to learn the complex underlying distributions of real data, a deficiency particularly evident in the Air Quality and Traffic datasets where the generated distributions show visible mismatches, such as shifted mass and less faithful tail shapes, compared to the multi-modal structure of the real data.

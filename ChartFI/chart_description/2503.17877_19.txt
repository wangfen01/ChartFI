This figure presents a feature importance analysis for six classes using the pixel-based model, comparing attribution scores from four explainability methods to determine which inputs most strongly drive model predictions. Across the panels, meteorological variables such as 2-meter air temperature and atmospheric water content show relatively lower attribution scores, with winds and acquisition geometry similarly remaining close to zero, indicating the model relies far more on spatial context and microwave brightness temperatures than on short-term weather signals. For open water detection, the model primarily relies on brightness temperature features, specifically AMSR2 18.7 GHz vertical and AMSR2 36.5 GHz horizontal polarizations, along with SAR-derived features; however, the chart reveals this is dominated by a single, method-specific spike where Feature Ablation assigns a strong positive contribution to AMSR2 18.7 V, suggesting sensitivity to how ablation perturbs correlated radiometric inputs. New ice shows the clearest negative driver, again from Feature Ablation, with AMSR2 18.7 V strongly decreasing the class score while other methods remain modest. As the ice types mature, geographical features maintain high importance across ice classes in the segmentation model. In the case of Young ice, the emphasis shifts toward spatial variables, with the temporal component, represented by the month feature, demonstrating significant attribution scores alongside longitude, implying a stronger seasonal and regional signature than a purely radiometric one. Thin FYI and Thick FYI exhibit more balanced, moderate attributions spread across longitude, month, and the 36.5 GHz channels. Old ice stands out for the largest disagreement across methods on spatial features, where DeepLift assigns a strong positive effect to latitude while Feature Ablation assigns a strong negative effect to longitude, a divergence that signals interaction effects between geography and radiometry. Overall, while AMSR2 18.7 V produces the largest anomalies and method disagreement, month and geographic coordinates provide the most stable, class-separating signal for the more mature ice categories.

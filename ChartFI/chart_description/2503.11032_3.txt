The probability distribution of similarity between the embeddings of natural data and adversarial examples (AEs) generated by different adversarial training (AT) methods—TRADES, WSCAT, and RLFAT—reveals distinct behavioral patterns across the CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and ImageNet32-100 datasets. Although all the AT methods share the same natural dataset, a consistent separation emerges in how they relate AEs to their natural counterparts: TRADES concentrates significant probability mass at very high similarity, while AEs generated by WSCAT are more dissimilar to natural samples than those generated by TRADES or RLFAT, as evidenced by its heavy skew toward the low-similarity region. On CIFAR10, TRADES exhibits a pronounced spike near 1.0, indicating many AEs are nearly identical to natural embeddings, whereas WSCAT peaks strongly between 0.2 and 0.3 before dropping off; RLFAT sits between them, distributing probability across mid-range similarities (0.3–0.6) with a secondary rise at the high end. This contrast sharpens on CIFAR100, where TRADES maintains a dominant high-similarity peak near 1.0 and WSCAT remains concentrated at low similarities, while RLFAT shifts more clearly toward the middle range (0.4–0.7), providing a smoothing effect relative to the extremes of the other two methods. On the more complex ImageNet32-100 dataset, distributions for all methods appear more spread out, yet the recurring near-1.0 concentration for TRADES remains a notable indicator of representation collapse toward highly similar pairs. Across all three panels, WSCAT consistently emphasizes low similarity, while RLFAT occupies a more diversified middle-to-high similarity structure, suggesting a more balanced relationship between natural data and adversarial perturbations compared to the high-similarity bias of TRADES.

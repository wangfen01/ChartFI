The four panels illustrate the performance of different reasoning model families—R1-Distill, s1.1, and EXAONE Deep—in recovering from the injection of irrelevant thoughts across various evaluation datasets and model sizes. Contrary to typical scaling expectations, a non/inverse-scaling trend is consistently observed across different model families and datasets when irrelevant thoughts are truncated at 10%, where smaller (7B, 8B) models tend to recover better than larger models. This inverse scaling is particularly prominent in the R1-Distill and s1.1 families on benchmarks like arc, humaneval, and math500, where the smallest model achieves 1.3 times higher performance than the largest model, while larger models struggle more than smaller models in recovering from even short irrelevant thoughts. In the EXAONE Deep panel, performance remains generally low across most datasets, yet the inverse-scaling trend persists, with the 2.4B and 7.8B variants often outperforming the 32B model. The length ablation panel further details the performance of R1-Distill models recovering from different lengths of injected irrelevant thoughts with varying cutoff points truncated at 10%, 33%, 66%, and 100% of the thought. While the non/inverse trend appears for these variants as well, it becomes less prominent as the unhelpful thought length increases; the scaling trend is rather inverse for short (10%) irrelevant thoughts and flat for the full (100%) ones. Across all sizes, a consistent downward trend occurs as unhelpful thought length increases from 10% to 100%, with the steepest degradation observed in smaller models that start with higher recovery rates at the 10% mark but lose their advantage as verbosity grows, ultimately converging to the same low performance floor as the larger models, suggesting that longer unhelpful reasoning actively harms accuracy by increasing distraction or compounding errors rather than acting as neutral noise.

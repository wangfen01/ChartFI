This scatter plot visualizes the ratio of body width to neck length for various birds, categorizing data to reveal how body shape varies across a wide size range. The horizontal axis uses a logarithmic scale to represent the average weight of bird populations for each species, spanning a substantial magnitude where the weight of these birds ranges from 2 g to 10000 g. Vertically, the ratio ranges from 0.6 to 3, with most species clustering between roughly 10 g and 2,000 g and exhibiting ratios generally between 1.0 and 2.0. This distribution indicates that despite large differences in mass, many birds maintain broadly similar proportions; however, significant deviations occur at the extremes. The Greater Sage-grouse sits at the top of the plot with the highest ratio near 3, indicating an unusually wide-bodied profile relative to neck length for its size. At the opposite end, some large waterfowl and ground birds, such as the Swan Goose and Vulturine Guineafowl, fall below 1.0, implying comparatively longer necks relative to body width. Distinct ecological groups occupy different spaces; for instance, raptors and large soaring birds like the Cinereous Vulture occupy the heavier end with mid-to-high ratios, contrasting sharply with the lower ratios of the Anseriformes. Overall, the dominant pattern suggests weak scaling between mass and this shape ratio, with species-specific ecology and morphology driving the most meaningful differences rather than size alone.

This visualization compares normalized filter outputs across various datasets and benchmarks, illustrating how different data sources—specifically African American Language (AAL) and White Mainstream English (WME) variants—interact with content filtering mechanisms. In the top panel, which contrasts RedPajama-AAL against RedPajama-WME, most filters (13 of 16) are more likely to remove AAL texts than WME texts, a trend most visible in the extreme negative LangId response for RedPajama-AAL. While RedPajama-WME generally remains closer to zero or slightly positive, RedPajama-AAL exhibits sharp positive spikes on Wiki and Wiki vs. RW, suggesting the filter responds more favorably to encyclopedic registers even within dialect-targeted slices. In the bottom panel, CORAAL (representing dialogue transcripts) is consistently positive across nearly all benchmarks, reflecting that most filters (11 of 16) are more likely to conserve dialogue transcripts over other sources. Conversely, Hip-Hop lyrics frequently reach the deepest negative drops, particularly on Jigsaw NSFW, Jigsaw Toxic, and KenLM tasks, though most filters (12 of 16) are more likely to conserve AAL tweets over hip hop lyrics despite both showing negative trends in toxicity-related evaluations. AAL Tweets also show a distinct sharp negative on FW-Edu, highlighting a domain sensitivity where formal educational registers are treated differently from social media text. Across both panels, the most significant shifts concentrate around toxicity, language identification, and register comparisons, whereas general benchmarks like QuRater and the AskLLM variants sit near zero for all groups, indicating a lack of discriminative signal or a more neutral interaction with the filters on those specific tasks.

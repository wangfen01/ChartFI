The heatmaps illustrate a comparison of text and image frame distributions across political leanings for five topics—Crime, Immigration, Politics, War, and Economy—revealing how different moral and civic foundations are expressed through different modalities. In the text-based charts, right-leaning news agencies focus more on legality, policy, and security frames in text and on public opinion and security frames in images. For topics like crime and war, the images do not portray many frames other than crime and security, unlike texts where additional frames like legality, policy, regulation, and some references to the political frame appear across the political spectrum. Across both modalities, the right focuses much more on the security and defense framing compared to sources from the left or center, a trend most visible in Immigration and War where security becomes one of the darkest cells. The politics text stands out for a notable spike in public opinion and political frames, suggesting that political discussion is more explicitly tied to collective sentiment than other topics. The economy text shows a distinct increase in economic language on the right compared with the left and center, while still retaining a visible care component, implying that economic discourse blends material concerns with welfare framing. The image-based charts generally compress differences across many foundations but amplify standout cues, with care remaining visually dominant across most topics and leanings, indicating that imagery frequently relies on human-impact or empathy cues regardless of ideology. Overall, the data suggests that while text allows for a broader range of distinct moral dimensions, images often rely on a more standardized and concentrated visual vocabulary centered on care and security, with the most significant ideological polarization occurring in the framing of borders and conflict.

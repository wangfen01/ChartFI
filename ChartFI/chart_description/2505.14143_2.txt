The two heatmaps present an analysis of the number of shared low-rank experts and the top-k selection of experts by the task-specific router networks, comparing model performance across classification metrics for Has0 and Non0 segments as well as regression-style error and association metrics. In this visualization, each metric is mapped to the same numerical scale, with darker colors indicating better performance. On the left, varying the number of experts from 5 to 30 reveals that the Non0 segment is consistently stronger than Has0 on Acc2 and F1, maintaining a stable performance gap; significant improvements are observed as the number of experts increases, achieving peak performance when the number is set to 15, where metrics such as Acc5 reach 58.06 and MAE drops to 0.503. However, the data highlights a clear anomaly at 25 experts, where ranking-style metrics drop sharply—Acc5 falls to approximately 49.13 and Acc7 to 47.01—and MAE worsens to 0.617, suggesting an instability at that configuration before recovering at 30 experts. Across the Top-k dimension (1 to 15) on the right, performance is generally more stable, though activating too few experts leads to a substantial performance decline, particularly visible in the lighter shading for lower k values. Consistent with the visual trends where MAE is lowest around 0.496 and Acc7 peaks near 56.69, setting k = 8 offers the best trade-off across various metrics. While correlation reaches its best levels near Top-k 14 to 15, the overall pattern suggests that Top-k tuning yields reliable marginal gains without the sharp degradation seen in the expert-count sweep.

The charts illustrate the phrasing of spreadsheet-related tasks and the underlying structural characteristics of the sheets, beginning with a verb–noun phrase distribution that covers a broad spectrum of manipulation types. The inner circle is dominated by action-oriented intents including find, extract, sum, highlight, remove, modify, count, delete, calculate, and display, while the outer circle illustrates the objects being manipulated—such as value, cell, row, and column—showcasing the diversity of requests that focus on viewing and computing over basic table elements. Regarding sheet dimensions, the distributions of row and column sizes reveal long-tailed distributions that are strongly right-skewed; specifically, 80% of spreadsheets have a row size spanning from 1 to 49 and a column size spanning from 1 to 13. It is noteworthy that the row or column size in the long tail part is substantial, indicating the challenging nature of the data where a minority of sheets extend far beyond these boundaries. Structurally, single-table sheets are more common at 64.3%, yet more than one-third of spreadsheets contain multiple tables in one sheet, and while relational layouts slightly outweigh non-standard ones at 57.3%, nearly half of the spreadsheets contain non-standard relational tables characterized by nested, incomplete, or missing headers.

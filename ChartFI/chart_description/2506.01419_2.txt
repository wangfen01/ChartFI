This heatmap illustrates the correlation between various linguistic features across a diverse set of languages, revealing a distinct structural divide between lexical simplicity and structural complexity. The visualization is bifurcated into two primary clusters: red features denote negative correlation, primarily associated with semantic and lexical measures like concreteness, sentence frequency, imageability, and type-token ratios on the left, while blue features lean towards positive correlation, representing length-based metrics such as syllable, character, and word counts per sentence on the right. These highly correlated linguistic features occurring in at least three languages demonstrate a robust cross-lingual tradeoff where texts characterized by higher structural density tend to score lower in semantic familiarity and concreteness. For brevity, these top features are those lying at the extreme ends of the correlation spectrum, with Estonian exhibiting the most pronounced divergence, pairing intense negative correlations in concreteness and imageability (reaching -0.76) with strong positive correlations in sentence-level counts. Similarly, languages like Czech, Hindi, and Russian show deep blue clusters for character and syllable counts, indicating high complexity, whereas German and Dutch present more moderate, neutral tones, suggesting a less rigid relationship between these variables. While most features follow this binary pattern, the ratio of punctuation and characters per sentence occasionally shifts toward neutral values, particularly in German and Welsh, indicating these may be less stable markers of the underlying complexity dimension. Despite occasional missing data points in languages like Arabic and Hindi, the overall trend remains consistent: a clear opposition between the imagistic, frequent vocabulary of simpler texts and the extended, multi-syllabic structures of more complex linguistic compositions.

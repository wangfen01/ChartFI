These box plots illustrate the results for four distinct tasks—locomotion on flat terrain, locomotion on rough terrain, object manipulation, and hill climbing—analyzed across the number of sensors, wheels, and limbs. The data reveals a positive correlation between sensory capabilities and success, as most high-performing robots possess sensors, and the best performances are consistently achieved with a higher number of sensors. Regarding the structural components, most combinations exhibit a similar distribution with few outliers, yet specific configurations prove superior depending on the environment. As expected, on flat terrain, the best robots rely on wheels, with the data indicating that only robots with four or five wheels show significantly higher performance. Conversely, on rough terrain, the optimal design shifts, and the best robots utilize a combination of one limb and wheels. However, increasing complexity beyond this point is detrimental; specifically, with more than one limb, it fails to find a good policy, evidenced by a sharp decline in task scores across all categories as the limb count increases.

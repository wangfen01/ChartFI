These kernel density estimation plots illustrate the distribution of absolute error differences compared to performance across six models for five targets, where the log of absolute difference error is plotted such that curves farther left indicate better accuracy and tighter peaks imply more consistent performance. While the AE variants and the CNN trained on mixed data generally concentrate on low errors, the data reveal that these distributions are not strictly unimodal. Most prominently, infoVAE (mix) appears multimodal for temperature, suggesting the model oscillates between distinct prediction regimes or error states. Similarly, the metallicity error distributions also show multimodality, especially for AE (mix) and CNN (mix), indicating that even high-performing models may struggle with calibration across specific data subsets for this variable. In contrast, radial velocity and airmass do not demonstrate similar behavior regarding this multimodal structure, though they exhibit other distinct anomalies. For instance, while most models cluster in the low-error regime for radial velocity, beta-VAE shows a pronounced right shift and heavy tail, indicating frequent large misses and higher variance. The CNN (ETC) model diverges significantly from the group, appearing right-shifted for radial velocity and effective temperature—suggesting systematic underperformance—and collapsing into a very narrow spike at high error for airmass, which is consistent with a near-constant biased prediction or labeling issue. Overall, while deterministic AE and CNN (mix) approaches deliver the most reliable low-error behavior, the specific multimodality in temperature and metallicity signals underlying complexities in model performance are distinct from the gross systematic errors observed in beta-VAE and CNN (ETC).

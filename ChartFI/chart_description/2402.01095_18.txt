The figure compares how several uncertainty or selection signals relate to ImageNet-1K top-1 accuracy across a range of model families, where the points represent the estimated mean values and the lines indicate the 1%-99% confidence intervals. The analysis is stratified into two regimes: a larger sample size of 100 and a much smaller sample size of 10. In the top row, the data demonstrates that a sample size of 100 still provides a reasonable estimate of the average score compared to the difference in mean scores between models. Within this robust regime, the Proposed score shows the clearest and most consistent positive association with accuracy, as higher mean #MSVs align tightly with higher-performing modern architectures like transformer-based models, while older or smaller models cluster at lower MSVs. Margin and Confidence also track accuracy positively but exhibit noticeably more spread than the Proposed metric, suggesting they are informative yet less discriminative across closely performing models, whereas Entropy trends negatively with accuracy. In contrast, SIS indicates it does not scale reliably with performance. However, the reliability of these signals shifts drastically in the bottom row; when the sample size is set to 10, the confidence interval becomes much larger, making the average score unsuitable for model comparison. While the Proposed metric retains the strongest separation between low- and high-accuracy models relative to other signals, the substantial increase in horizontal uncertainty and overlap across all metrics confirms that small sample sizes compromise the ability to distinguish between models effectively.

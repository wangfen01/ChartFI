This bar chart illustrates the average DA scores across twelve language pairs for six machine translation systems, revealing a distinct performance gap between high-resource and low-resource directions. High-resource language pairs, such as English–Zulu and English–Igbo, generally achieve higher scores with all models clustering near the top of the scale, indicating these directions are broadly well-handled with only modest variance between systems. A second tier of languages, including English–Amharic, English–Hausa, English–Kinyarwanda, English–Twi, and English–Yoruba, remains generally strong but exhibits more separation as the strongest systems maintain high quality while weaker models drop noticeably. In contrast, low-resource pairs such as English–Kikuyu and French–Wolof remain particularly challenging for current translation systems and exhibit substantially lower translation quality. English–Kikuyu represents a clear anomaly where one model reaches a mid-range score while others fall toward the bottom, suggesting a significant capability gap specific to that direction. Similarly, French-to-African pairs like French–Ewe and French–Wolof are consistently difficult across the board, with most systems failing to reach moderate performance levels. This divergence suggests that while model differences are relatively small on well-resourced or better-covered directions, they widen dramatically on low-resource pairs where system choice becomes the primary driver of quality and many current translation systems continue to struggle.

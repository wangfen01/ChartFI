The four heatmaps illustrate the influence of the filtering order, `r` (x-axis), and the parameter `k_pos` (y-axis) on Mi-F1 performance across the DBLP, ACM, IMDB, and Yelp datasets. A clear performance hierarchy is evident among the datasets: DBLP and Yelp consistently operate in a high, stable band in the low-to-mid 90s, ACM sits slightly lower in the high 80s to low 90s, and IMDB's performance is markedly lower in the mid-50s. The most dominant trend is that when the filtering order is relatively small, better results can be achieved. As the filtering order increases from 1 to 5, the performance tends to decrease on most datasets. This degradation is most pronounced on the ACM dataset, where Mi-F1 scores fall from 92.47% at r=1 to 87.94% at r=5. A similar, though less severe, decline is observed for DBLP, dropping from 94.22% to the 92.67% range, and for Yelp, which eases from a peak of 93.06% to about 91.23%. In contrast, performance variation along the `k_pos` parameter is modest, suggesting the model's output is less sensitive to this parameter within the tested range. The IMDB dataset is a notable exception to the primary trend, exhibiting a comparatively flat, non-monotonic response to changes in `r`, though it does contain the most significant localized anomaly: a distinct performance dip to 52.89% at the specific parameter combination of `k_pos`=4 and `r`=1. Overall, optimal performance is consistently found at smaller values of `r` (typically 1 or 2), while increasing the filtering order systematically degrades results, particularly for the ACM and DBLP datasets.

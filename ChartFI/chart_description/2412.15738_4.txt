The figure tracks three related time series for multiple regions, where panels (a), (b), and (c) represent the TO, FROM, and NET connectedness of System 1, respectively. Across these panels, the Overall signal generally sits above the contemporaneous line, while the lagged line is consistently the smallest contributor, indicating that most movement is driven by immediate effects rather than delayed ones. At a glance, most grains exhibit similar TO and FROM spillover trajectories, differing mainly in spillover intensity. The most prominent shifts occur around the first vertical breakpoint, corresponding to the outbreak of the conflict; here, U.S. corn saw its TO spillover reach 90% for several months before the Black Sea Grain Initiative (BSGI) came into effect, exhibiting a clear regime change to a higher plateau. Conversely, after the outbreak of the conflict, the spillover effects of U.S. and Black Sea wheat on other markets dropped by more than 20%, with the spillover levels remaining stable for an extended period. Black Sea wheat and corn show pronounced downward transitions consistent with this drop, and after the BSGI extension was shortened to 60 days, their spillovers also decreased by more than 20%. While major producers like Brazil (BRs) remain structurally high, the spillover from China’s and India’s grain futures to other markets is very low, generally below 20%. Specifically, Chinese rice has the lowest TO and FROM connectedness throughout the sample period, consistently below 10%, and the spillover from other markets received by Indian maize and Chinese rice is also very low, with the overall level remaining below 20%. Similarly, compared to U.S. soybeans, corn, and wheat, the TO connectedness of U.S. rice is relatively weak. Panel (c) reframes these dynamics around a zero baseline, highlighting where lagged effects briefly intensify and revealing sharp negative spikes in regions like CNw and ZAw that resemble short-lived anomalies rather than trend shifts.

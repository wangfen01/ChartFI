The chart compares four methods across a grid of intrinsic dimensions on the x-axis and ambient dimensions on the y-axis, evaluating them first by prediction error, or MSE, and then by rank agreement, measured by Kendall’s tau. Overall, the performance of existing estimators degrades with d, with near complete failure for d > 100, as evidenced by the abrupt darkening of the heatmaps for InfoNCE, KSG, and MINE at moderate to high ambient settings. In stark contrast, applying LMI approximation results in stable estimates up to d = 5 * 10^3 ambient dimensions, maintaining low MSE and high rank correlation where other methods collapse. However, the faithfulness of LMI approximation instead degrades with increasing k, showing a gradual loss of accuracy as the intrinsic dimension grows from 1 to 9. The summary panels identify the estimator with the highest absolute accuracy in each setting for MSE and the estimator with the highest relative accuracy in each setting for Kendall’s tau. These panels demonstrate that the LMI approximation gives more absolutely and relatively accurate MI estimates than alternatives for 83 % and 87 % of tested settings respectively, dominating almost the entire high-ambient regime. While LMI is the clear overall winner, MINE occasionally achieves the lowest MSE in a specific pocket of low ambient and high intrinsic dimensions, and there are scattered instances at very low ambient dimensions where other methods briefly lead in rank correlation, indicating small-margin competition when the signal is strong. Ultimately, ambient dimensionality is the primary driver of failure modes for traditional methods, whereas LMI uniquely overcomes this high-dimensional bottleneck.

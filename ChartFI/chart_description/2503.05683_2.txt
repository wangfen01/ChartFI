The visualizations display the distribution of time-stamps associated with factoids where temporal information was available, revealing that for real-world updates, facts cover both historical, but especially more recent context, culminating in a pronounced spike in sample volume near the latest timestamp. Tracking the average accuracy of five LLMs evaluated across facts broken down by timestamps shows that historical performance remains relatively stable around the mid-range; however, contrasted against facts clearly preceding knowledge cutoffs (pre-2023), there is a notable drop in average capabilities. Assessing the distribution of edits extracted for each timestep and their corresponding dates reveals that while the majority of edits align with their respective timestep, edits in larger parts also incorporate changes of information associated with primarily older time intervals. Furthermore, real-world edits cover both highly specific facts (measured on subject and object counts in pretraining corpora) as well as general, common knowledge modifications, with the underlying distributions showing a strong skew where most questions involve very few subjects and objects. Model performance patterns reinforce that complexity and specificity matter, demonstrating that lower entity frequency correlates with reduced performance, and especially on highly specific edits as defined by pretraining concept frequency does performance go down. Finally, in contrast to the clear impact of entity frequency, view counts show less consistent trends across timesteps, exhibiting late-stage volatility rather than a steady improvement.

The two heatmaps compare overall quality scores across seven models for various languages, where the left heatmap shows the results for culturally agnostic questions while the right heatmap shows the results for culturally specific questions. Across the agnostic panel, performance is consistently higher, with the strongest results concentrated in high-resource languages such as English, Spanish, and German. In this high- to mid-resource tier, closed- and open-weight models perform comparably, challenging the assumption that proprietary models are universally superior; notably, AYA-EXPANSE-32B demonstrates robust multilingual competence, outperforming CLAUDE-3-OPUS in 9 of these languages on culturally agnostic questions. However, a steep degradation appears as resources diminish, revealing that open-weight models are much worse on low-resource languages. While the closed models significantly outperform the open models on the low-resource languages, scoring mostly 22 – 66, the open models mostly score below 10, frequently collapsing to near-zero outcomes for languages such as Faroese, Samoan, and Pashto. On culturally specific questions, the overall level shifts downward across nearly every language-model pair, and the gaps between languages widen. English remains the best-supported language, but still falls into the high 50s to low 60s for the top models, while the collapse in non-major languages becomes even more pronounced. Although anomalies exist, such as Hindi scoring relatively high for culturally specific questions on GPT-4o compared to its peers, the broader trend highlights that cultural specificity amplifies existing resource disparities, rendering many languages that are merely weak in the agnostic panel effectively unsupported in the specific panel.

The chart displays the average connection of each element to the policy themes, ranking topics to show which elements are most strongly linked within the framework. Model elements are shown on the left, and colour-coded according to which cluster they belong to. Different forms of provisioning seem to be important elements across policy themes, with government and Material needs at the top of the list, and non-institutional provisioning in the top quartile, indicating alongside material needs that provisioning structures and basic needs are the most central anchors. A second tier follows closely, where income, steering investments, and technological change remain highly connected, suggesting that financial direction-setting and innovation are key transmission channels between the economy and outcomes. Despite these strong upper-tier linkages, the average score across themes lies below this threshold 76.3% for the broader set of variables. Moving down the ranking, connections become more evenly distributed across energy and resource flows, affordability of essential goods, wealth, redistribution, and consumption decisions, implying a broad mid-field where socio-economic mechanisms and biophysical throughput are tightly interwoven without a single dominant driver. Trade-related measures show an interesting split: embodied resources and pollution in trade are relatively well connected, while monetary international trade sits lower, hinting that the physical impacts of trade integrate more strongly with the system than purely monetary trade metrics. The weakest connections concentrate in the financial system’s granular and stability-oriented variables, such as disaggregated financial firms and assets, government bonds, and financial stability, which sit at the bottom of the chart. This pattern suggests that high-level provisioning and real-economy or biophysical linkages are treated as more system-defining than detailed financial architecture, with a notable drop-off once the focus moves from broad policy concepts into narrower financial disaggregation.

The confusion matrices for emotion classification illustrate the performance of four distinct modalities—Micro-Expression (ME), Electroencephalogram (EEG), Peripheral physiological signals (PERI), and Multimodal fusion—across both three-class (Negative, Positive, Surprise) and seven-class (Happiness, Surprise, Sadness, Fear, Anger, Disgust, Contempt) tasks. The diagonal elements of the matrix represent the proportion of correctly classified samples, with darker colors indicating higher accuracy, while off-diagonal values reveal specific misclassification patterns. In the three-class task, the classification performance based on ME outperforms EEG, while PERI yields the lowest accuracy, characterized by significantly heavier cross-class mixing and lower correct rates for every category. Across the single modalities in the coarse setting, Surprise is frequently the most confusable class, often leaking into Negative, while Negative and Positive show an asymmetric confusion where Positive is more often mistaken as Negative than the reverse. The multimodal fusion approach performs best, with a significantly reduced misclassification rate and a relatively balanced accuracy distribution across classes, achieving the highest diagonal rates of approximately 80.23% for Negative, 75.79% for Positive, and 72.78% for Surprise. As the label granularity increases to the seven-class task, performance drops for every method, yet the multimodal fusion method demonstrates significant advantages in emotion recognition, particularly in fine-grained seven-class tasks, effectively improving classification accuracy by leveraging complementary information between channels. The confusion matrix reveals that the classification accuracy for Fear, Disgust, and Surprise is relatively high, especially in the Multimodal and ME models, whereas the accuracy for Anger and Contempt is lower, showing a broader spread into neighboring negative categories. This systematic confusion suggests that subtle or socially nuanced expressions like Contempt frequently shift toward other negative states, but the Multimodal approach mitigates this with stronger diagonals and reduced off-diagonal spillover compared to unimodal cues.

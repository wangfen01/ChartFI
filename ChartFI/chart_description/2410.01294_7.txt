The chart compares the 10-shot MMLU score against the Attack Success Rate (ASR) on HarmBench-35 for bijection learning settings across various models, where a point’s color indicates the target model and its size reflects the dispersion. To characterize the relationship between model capability and safety vulnerability, a quadratic regression is plotted for each model, with a tick indicating peak ASR on each curve. For the Claude 3/3.5, GPT-4o, and Llama 3.1 model families, the attack efficacy of bijection learning at various complexities can be decomposed into a scaling regime and a saturated regime, respectively denoting the increasing and decreasing stages of the Pareto frontier. This creates a non-linear “inverted-U” relationship: the flat, “pre-emergence” portions reveal that very difficult settings of bijection learning are beyond current models’ abilities, but as capability increases, the data suggests a stronger-with-scale phenomenon in the bijection jailbreak, where the higher-capability model in a given family comes with a peak-effectiveness bijection setting at the highest MMLU score and the highest ASR in the family. Specifically, the Claude family shows jailbreak success climbing rapidly into a clear peak before declining, the GPT-4o family displays a similar but lower-amplitude arc, and the Llama 3.1 family follows this pattern with greater dispersion. In contrast, Gemini models do not exhibit this two-stage scaling; instead, their ASR continues to improve with easier bijections with no turning point, indicating a monotonic tradeoff where higher MMLU scores consistently correlate with higher ASR.

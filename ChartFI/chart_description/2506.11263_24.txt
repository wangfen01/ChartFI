This visualization displays raw magnetometer sensor measurement data across three orthogonal components (x, y, and z) over a duration of 3500 seconds, featuring highlighted sections used for the sensor model identification approach. The signal is characterized by discrete, step-like regime changes rather than gradual drift, indicating repeated reorientation events or state transitions that shift the baseline levels of the axes. For the overall experiment, the magnetometer measurements of the highlighted Δt = 60 s trajectory segments are utilized for the sensor model identification, with four specific windows marked to capture the signal behavior during and immediately following major transitions. From the start through approximately 1300s, the x-component remains mostly negative and relatively stable after an early transient, while the y-component stays positive with a slight upward trend and the z-component hovers near a slightly negative baseline with minimal variation. A pronounced transition occurs between 1400 and 1500 s, where x jumps from a negative to a strongly positive value and y increases further, establishing a new plateau that persists until a second sharp shift near 1750–1850 s reverses x back to a negative level and pulls y down toward a lower baseline. The most dramatic shift occurs around 2400–2500 s, where x rises abruptly to its highest sustained positive level while y drops to its most negative sustained level, resulting in a clear axis swap in dominance; following this event, both x and y remain relatively steady with minor fluctuations while z continues to hover near its prior baseline with modest noise. These repeated, near-instantaneous level shifts followed by long stable plateaus suggest discrete events, with anomalies concentrated at the transition boundaries where brief spikes and overshoot-like behaviors appear before the signals stabilize, consistent with momentary disturbances or settling after a change in orientation.

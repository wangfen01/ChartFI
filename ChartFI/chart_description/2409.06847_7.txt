The directional gain profiles for various algorithms are illustrated over a ±90° angular spread for a system configuration with K=2 users, N=4 targets, and L=50 antennas, comparing two scenarios, AP-1 and AP-2, across the ALMCI, CCPA, MCQT-SCA, ZF, and MMSE methods. The use of L=50 antennas affords finer control over beam shaping, which is clearly evidenced in the gain profiles for both scenarios where the dominant structure consists of strong mainlobes centered roughly around ±40–50 degrees alongside deep attenuation and a dense field of sidelobes across remaining angles. In AP-1, the ALMCI algorithm distinguishes itself by delivering highly focused beam peaks that reach close to the top of the scale, indicating its precision in directing energy toward intended targets and significantly enhancing peak gains for sensing targets compared to those achieved by all other algorithms. While the mid-angle region around 0 degrees in AP-1 typically sits in the mid -30 dB range, ALMCI shows the most pronounced narrow nulls, including an extreme dip near broadside that suggests aggressive interference suppression, whereas CCPA tends to maintain a lower central sidelobe floor and MCQT-SCA exhibits elevated edge sidelobes near ±80 degrees. In AP-2, the overall beampattern becomes smoother and more symmetric, and while the methods cluster more tightly around the mainlobes, the ALMCI algorithm continues to provide superior peak precision. The sidelobe floor in the central region of AP-2 appears more uniform and slightly elevated compared to the deepest notches in AP-1, with CCPA again trending lower in the mid-angle region and MCQT-SCA showing higher responses toward the outer angles. Ultimately, the transition from AP-1 to AP-2 reflects a reduction in extreme null behavior and tighter agreement among methods, yet the ALMCI algorithm maintains its performance advantage in beam focus and peak gain across both configurations.

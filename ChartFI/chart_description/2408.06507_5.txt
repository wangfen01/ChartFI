This bubble plot represents the relationship between the sample size in the training data and the F1-score obtained in the test data for each species, where the size of the bubbles represents the number of datasets that contributed to each of the species and color differentiates coniferous from broadleaf taxa. A statistical analysis of the distribution reveals that neither the quantity of data points nor the number of datasets within each species significantly affected the model F1-score, a finding substantiated by Pearson’s correlation coefficients being -0.03 and 0.06, respectively. Visually, this independence is characterized by a dense cluster of species at relatively small training sizes that nonetheless achieve strong performance, with many F1-scores ranging between 0.75 and 0.95. As training data increases into the thousands, performance does not rise uniformly; high-sample species such as Pinus sylvestris, Picea abies, and Fagus sylvatica plateau in the low-to-mid 0.8 range, indicating that massive training volumes do not guarantee superior accuracy. While the majority of taxa generalize well, notable anomalies appear among broadleaves, specifically Ulmus laevis, which exhibits a very low F1-score despite a nontrivial sample size, and mid-sample species like Acer campestre and Carpinus betulus, which sit around the 0.5–0.6 range. Ultimately, the observation that species with limited data often match or exceed the performance of heavily sampled classes suggests that the DetailView model effectively compensates for data imbalances, allowing for high classification success even without very large sample counts.

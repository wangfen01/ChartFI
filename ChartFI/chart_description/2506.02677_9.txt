The visualization presents the Adaptive Feature Weighting (AFW) on four target datasets, detailing the distribution of background and foreground weights where a brighter color indicates a higher re-composition weight. The analysis reveals that AFW learns different re-composition weights for each domain, resulting in highly dataset-dependent structures; however, a consistent theme across all matrices is that the highest weight learned by AFW is not necessarily at the diagonal of the matrix. Instead, the weights indicate sparse, specialized prototype usage where only a subset of prototypes carries a strong influence while others remain near-neutral. In FSS-1000, this manifests as localized high-intensity blocks, particularly a pronounced horizontal band in the foreground suggesting a stable set of prototypes is repeatedly activated for object regions. The Deepglobe and ISIC2018 datasets exhibit more distinct structural partitioning. Crucially, the learned re-composition weights for foreground and background tend to be mutually exclusive, a trend particularly noticeable in the Deepglobe and ISIC datasets, implying that the model explicitly separates segmentation cues for object and background classes. In contrast, Chest X-ray shows broader, smoother gradients with a wide swath of prototypes carrying moderate positive weight, reflecting the diffuse and texture-heavy nature of radiographic patterns. Ultimately, the distinct separation between background and foreground structures across these domains demonstrates that the model learns specific, non-overlapping prototype roles rather than simply reusing identical features for opposing classes.

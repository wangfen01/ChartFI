This composite visualization presents a comparative analysis of time series approximation techniques across eleven distinct datasets in the upper row, paired with their corresponding value distributions in the lower row. The top panels overlay the raw data series (in gray) with Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) and Piecewise Aggregate Approximation (PAA) reconstructions to evaluate signal fidelity. A critical observation from these time series plots is that the FFT (in blue) closely mimics the data, effectively capturing the nuances and variability of the original signal. In contrast, the PAA (orange) fails to approximate a data series (in gray) with high frequency, often resulting in a flat line or a coarse step function, particularly in noise-heavy datasets such as SIFT1b, astro, and ETHZ. Shifting to the bottom row, the charts display the distribution of values for each dataset, contrasting the raw data density against the PAA representation and a standard normal reference curve. These density plots highlight that the actual data distribution is often non-Gaussian, with many datasets like OBS and Iquique exhibiting highly peaked, leptokurtic profiles that deviate significantly from the standard normal distribution. Furthermore, the PAA distributions consistently appear narrower and more peaked than the raw data, reinforcing the finding that PAA tends to compress signal variance and lose distributional detail compared to the original time series.

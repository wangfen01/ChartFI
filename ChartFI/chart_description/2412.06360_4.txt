The figure maps cumulative decarbonization for space cooling across Indian states from 2001 to 2022, split into urban and rural areas and presented in two normalizations: per household and per unit of Net State Domestic Product (NSDP). Nationally, the decarbonization per unit of NSDP for space cooling in Indian urban households over these 22 years amounted to 379.7 kgCO₂ per lakh rupee, and for rural households, it was 218.4 kgCO₂ per lakh rupee. This urban-rural disparity is also evident in the per-household view, where national totals highlight a significant gap of 1386.1 kgCO₂ per household in urban areas versus 368.8 kgCO₂ in rural areas. In the per-household normalization, the strongest decarbonization clusters in several western and southern states, while much of the Indo-Gangetic plain and parts of the northeast sit in lighter shades, indicating smaller cumulative gains; this wide spread suggests that a handful of states account for disproportionately large household-level reductions, especially in urban areas. However, compressing the picture by economic output reveals a different pattern where some states that are strong on a per-household basis appear less exceptional once scaled by NSDP, implying that part of the urban advantage is tied to higher consumption and absolute mitigation potential rather than uniformly higher efficiency. Regarding temporal trends, the pie markers indicate the proportion of decarbonization across two stages; notably, the cumulative decarbonization per unit of NSDP in both Indian urban and rural households during 2001–2011 and 2012–2022 remained essentially the same. This challenges the assumption of a uniform acceleration in recent years, although state-level anomalies exist where specific regions either remain high in both panels or flip intensities, signaling varying drivers of decarbonization ranging from pure economic scale to genuine intensity improvements.

The three correlation heatmaps illustrate the relationships between various evaluation metrics across low-quality pairs, high-quality pairs, and all pairs, revealing a distinct bifurcation into form-based lexical overlap measures, such as self-BLEU and VS-ngram variants, and content-based semantic similarity measures, including Chamfer, self-cosSim, and VS-embed variants. In the high-quality sets, most metrics achieve fair to substantial levels of agreement, reflecting strong consistency as lexical agreement and semantic agreement rise together rather than competing. However, agreements vary considerably in low-quality sets, where content-based metrics such as Chamfer, self-cosSim, and VS-Embed variants exhibit near-perfect agreement with each other and maintain Kappa values exceeding 0.6 with GPT-4o, while form-based metrics show poor agreement with GPT-4o, characterized by negative Kappa values indicating that the observed agreement is lower than would be expected by chance. This divergence in low-quality pairs highlights a significant tradeoff where higher surface-form overlap coincides with lower semantic alignment, whereas the relationship between these two families flips to broadly positive in high-quality pairs, serving as a diagnostic pattern for quality separation. The aggregated view of all pairs largely washes out these distinctions, resulting in near-zero correlations between the lexical and semantic blocks as the two opposing regimes cancel each other out. Throughout these shifts, GPT-4o behaves primarily as a semantic quality signal, aligning negatively with lexical overlap in low-quality sets but becoming positively associated with most similarity measures in high-quality sets, while the persistent low agreement between form-based and content-based metrics underscores fundamental differences in how these metrics measure diversity.

The charts illustrate H2 pipeline flows (top) and H2 storage (salt cavern) trajectory (bottom) for the low (left) and high (right) storage cost scenarios, revealing how seasonal demand variations are met by either significant storage expansion or more extensive pipeline infrastructure within Europe and from the exporting MENA region to Europe. Pipeline inflows are consistently dominated by transit and exchange within Europe, while imports from outside Europe remain a smaller but steady base component; however, under high storage costs, the inflow profile becomes noticeably more volatile with sharper spikes, whereas low storage costs allow for a smoother, more stable band of flows. A critical stress event is observed where the effect of the “Kalte Dunkelflaute” is highlighted by a shaded band in early 2012, triggering a rapid drawdown of inventories to near zero and a concurrent spike in pipeline throughput to compensate for the supply crunch. Following this depletion, storage levels in both scenarios follow a strong seasonal cycle, rebuilding steadily from spring through autumn before easing down into December, with the largest contributions concentrated in major systems like France, Germany, the United Kingdom, and Poland. The economic implications are evident in the accumulation limits: low storage costs support a higher and more sustained accumulation peaking near the 200 TWh range, while high storage costs cap the build closer to the mid-to-high 100s TWh, demonstrating that H2 storage investments depend on the assumed costs and the trade-off between maintaining large inventory buffers versus relying on flexible, high-capacity infrastructure.

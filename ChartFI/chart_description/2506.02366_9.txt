These heatmaps illustrate the ranking of testing G-mean of Decision Trees (DT) across 13 datasets using varying sampling methods—including GBABS, GGBS, IGBS, SM, BSM, SMNC, Tomek, and the original distribution (Ori)—as the class noise ratio increases from 0% to 40%. In this visualization, the larger the value of G-mean, the higher the ranking, which is represented by lower numerical values (deep red) for top performance and higher values (deep blue) for poor performance. At a 0% noise ratio, the rankings are relatively mixed across datasets; however, it can be observed that on most standard imbalanced datasets, the GBABS-based DT ranks high. As the noise ratio rises through 5%, 10%, 20%, and 30%, the heatmaps increasingly polarize, showing a clearer separation between consistently strong and weak methods. Specifically, on almost all imbalanced datasets with class noise, GBABS-based DT achieves the best performance, solidifying its dominance with frequent rank-1 positions. In contrast, methods such as IGBS and SMNC frequently occupy the lower ranks across many datasets, suggesting limited robustness to noise compared to the stability of GBABS and Tomek. While mid-tier methods like SM and BSM show dataset-to-dataset variability, the general trend indicates that noise amplifies the advantage of inherently noise-tolerant approaches. Finally, in a high noise environment such as 40%, although the ranking of GBABS has dropped on a few datasets, it is still better than most other sampling methods, demonstrating superior resilience relative to the broader field of comparators.

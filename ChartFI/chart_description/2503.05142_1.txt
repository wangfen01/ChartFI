This bubble plot illustrates the agreements with MT-BENCH HUMAN JUDGMENTS with different LLM judges by plotting the OpenLLM LeaderBoard 2 Average on the x-axis against the percentage of agreement with human judgments on the y-axis. The data compares judgments derived using the original Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting, represented by circles, against those derived using the proposed RocketEval framework, denoted as "Ours" and represented by stars. Performance is benchmarked against three key reference lines: the human-human agreement at 64.8%, the agreement between GPT-4o and a human at 66.7%, and a random baseline at 33.3%. Agreement between LLMs and humans typically ranges from human-to-human agreement (64.8%) to lower than random outcomes (33.3%), with a general positive correlation showing that as leaderboard performance rises from below 10 to over 40, human agreement tends to increase from the mid-20s into the low-to-mid 60s. Smaller or earlier-generation models like Qwen2.5-0.5B and InternLM2.5-1.8B cluster below the random threshold, while mid-tier models with leaderboard scores in the 20s often achieve agreement in the 50% to 60% range. Notably, the RocketEval framework consistently yields higher agreement scores than the original CoT prompting across various models, such as GLM-4-9B and Llama-3-8B, often pushing these models closer to the human reference line. At the high end of the spectrum, the largest models like Qwen2-72B and Llama-3-70B approach the human-to-human agreement level of 64.8% but generally remain below the GPT-4o baseline. Significant variance exists among models with similar leaderboard averages; for instance, models scoring around 30 on the leaderboard show a wide spread in agreement, suggesting that factors like instruction tuning or the specific evaluation framework used can materially affect human alignment beyond aggregate benchmark scores.

The figure illustrates long-run changes in the transmission networks of the Czech Republic, Hungary, the Netherlands, and Slovakia, presenting the relative increase in the number of lines in panel a and the relative number of topological changes in panel b. To account for varying network sizes, the number of new power lines and topological changes is normalized to the number of lines in operation in a given year, with dotted curves representing a calculated 5-year moving mean to smooth short-term volatility. Across all countries, the metrics generally trend downward, indicating a shift from rapid grid expansion and frequent structural reconfiguration in the early decades to slower growth later on. In panel a, the relative increase in the number of lines is highest in the 1950s and 1960s, but subsequently, the relative number of commissioned transmission lines shows a steady decrease from the early 1980s. However, a divergence from this stabilization is evident as the Netherlands shows more active development in the 1990s. This difference is also captured by panel b, as the relative number of topological changes reaches a local peak at the same time for the Netherlands, interrupting the otherwise steady decline toward a narrow band of low activity observed in the other nations. While the Czech Republic and Slovakia largely remain at lower levels following their initial declines, the data reveals a resurgence of activity in the final decade; another common trend is an increase in changes in the 2010s, particularly visible in the Netherlands and Hungary, suggesting episodic investment waves or modernization pulses rather than a return to sustained expansion.

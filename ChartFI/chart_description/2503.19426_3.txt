The two heatmaps compare bias scores for 8 large language models (LLMs) across various bias categories from the BBQ and UNQOVER datasets, presenting results from a Base setting on the left and a DeCAP setting on the right. Each cell displays the bias score of the corresponding model for the respective bias category, where as the bias score in each cell increases, the color becomes a deeper red, while the lower bias score is represented with shades of gray. A clear pattern emerges where the Base setting exhibits consistently higher and more variable bias scores, indicated by numerous deep-red hotspots, while the DeCAP setting substantially lowers these scores and compresses the value range, signaling a broad reduction in measured bias. Within the BBQ dataset under the Base setting, categories like ‘Disability Status’, ‘Age’, and ‘Physical Appearance’ have very high bias scores, with values exceeding 20%, particularly across FLAN and Llama 3 series models. The application of DeCAP leads to a dramatic drop in these scores; for example, in the ‘Disability Status’ category, the bias score for Llama3-instruct (8B) drops from 21.04% in the Base to just 1.62%. However, this reduction is uneven, as religion remains a comparatively high-scoring category across most models in the DeCAP setting, emerging as one of the most persistent dimensions of bias. In the UNQOVER dataset, the Base setting contains pronounced peaks, especially for gender and nationality in specific models, whereas DeCAP drives most of these scores close to zero, creating a much flatter profile. Despite this strong overall attenuation, nationality retains a noticeable nonzero pocket for at least one model in the DeCAP results, and religion, while diminished, does not collapse as completely as other UNQOVER attributes. Ultimately, the dominant shift is a significant mitigation of bias from the Base to the DeCAP setting, with religion and, to a lesser extent, nationality standing out as the most resistant dimensions, and any remaining high scores appearing as model- and category-specific anomalies rather than systematic trends.

The heatmaps display CKA similarity between encoder and successive decoder layers across four feature levels, characterizing the evolution of feature representations within the network. At the initial stage, the encoder features (enc, e_1 ~ e_5) learn a mid-level degradation representation which is mixed with the information of the low-level blur pattern and high-level degradation degree. However, the progression through the reversible sub-decoders enables the ability to gradually disentangle the low-level blur pattern and high-level degradation degree. Specifically, in the blur-pattern map, Level 1 rises significantly from moderate similarity (0.61) to near-saturation (0.99) by the final layers, demonstrating that more accurate low-level blur patterns are learned from Level modules connected to the output. In contrast, Level 4 of the blur map declines from a high of 0.81 to 0.63, indicating a de-emphasis of blur cues in deeper features. Conversely, the degradation-degree map illustrates the opposite trend: Level 4 trends upward to reach 0.80 at dec8, while Level 1 fades from an early peak to roughly 0.53. This structural shift signifies that more high-level degradation degree knowledge is gathered from Level modules connected to the encoder. While Level 2 and Level 3 maintain relatively stable intermediate values across both maps, the distinct divergence at the extremes—with Level 1 dominating blur alignment and Level 4 dominating degradation degree alignment—highlights the architectural separation of these degradation components.

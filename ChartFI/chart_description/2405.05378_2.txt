These heatmaps illustrate the frequency of different threat types across multiple language models, segmented by two demographic dimensions—caste and race—and further divided by four occupations: doctor, nurse, software developer, and teacher. The heatmaps are ordered based on the LLMs that generated the least to most CHAST (Contextual Harmful Content) in the conversations, revealing a distinct dichotomy between proprietary and open-source architectures. At the top of the scale, OpenAI models generate comparatively lower amounts of CHAST for both caste and race; specifically, GPT-4-Turbo rarely generates CHAST for either race- or caste-based conversations, exhibiting safe scores across the board. However, a divergence appears with GPT-3.5-Turbo, which is safe for race-based conversations but generates significantly more CHAST for caste, challenging the notion that these baselines are uniformly harmless. Moving to the open-source LLMs, the data shows they generate CHAST for both race- and caste-based conversations, with a prominent pattern where scores for caste are significantly higher in all LLMs, except for GPT-4-Turbo. Within this group, larger open-source models are more harmful, and more so in the context of caste, where CategorizationThreat and OpportunityHarm frequently reach saturation points while RealisticThreat remains generally the lowest. A granular analysis of the occupational columns reveals that 5 out of 8 LLMs generate more CHAST for older occupations (such as doctor and teacher) compared to software developers in the caste context. Ultimately, the visualization confirms that while model choice is a dominant driver of risk, there is a strong interaction where caste prompts elicit more harmful content than race prompts, and scaling model size within open-source families tends to exacerbate rather than mitigate these specific safety risks.

The correlation matrix illustrates the relationships between body weight, measurements taken from images, productivity metrics calculated after the PET, and structure scores, revealing a clear separation between morphological dimensions and performance traits. Strong positive correlations are observed between certain measurements, mainly involving the weight, such as Dorsum Area (0.90) and Croup Width (0.85), while Body Weight also tracks closely with Dorsum Length (0.72) and the clustering variables, indicating that these clusters primarily capture overall size and back development. Height measures are internally consistent, with Croup Height and Withers Height strongly correlated (0.75), whereas Rib Height, Side Area, and Side Length form a secondary frame and volume cluster that links back to weight. There is not a strong correlation between the productivity metrics and the image measurements, with the strongest observed correlations being 0.37 between Average Daily Gain and Withers Height, and -0.37 between Residual Feed Intake and Side Length. Within the productivity metrics, the strongest correlation is between Dry Matter Intake and Final Weight (0.70), while Average Daily Gain aligns more closely with Dry Matter Intake (0.59) than with Residual Feed Intake (0.05), suggesting that growth is more tied to consumption than to efficiency deviations in this dataset. Sub-sternal Void Height presents a notable inverse relationship with several size measures, such as Croup Width (-0.50) and Side Area (-0.42), implying it represents a distinct aspect of body depth that contrasts with overall mass. The structure scoring components, including S1, S2, S3, and the Average Structure Score, behave as a highly intercorrelated block with coefficients exceeding 0.73, yet they maintain relatively weak links to growth and intake metrics. Ultimately, the strong correlation between clustering variables and size traits, contrasted with their minimal relationship to Final Weight and intake, reinforces that the clustering is primarily morphological rather than performance-driven.

This heatmap illustrates the distribution of importance scores for different modules on the x-axis in Amuse across various question types on the y-axis, where each score ranges from 0 to 1 to indicate the encoder significance as calculated by the cross-modal attention layers for each question type in the Music AVQA dataset. Higher scores reflect greater contributions to the final answers, demonstrating how the calculated results from the attention layers reflect the importance of each musical module’s output concerning the semantics of the current question. MIE-A emerges as the consistently dominant driver across nearly all tasks, peaking for AV Count at 0.96 and maintaining exceptionally high relevance for AV Comp at 0.94 and AV Temp at 0.92, while MIE-V generally serves as the second most influential module, particularly for AV Count at 0.81 and AV Comp at 0.78. Outside of the MIE modules, Src-A stands out as the only component with moderate, task-dependent relevance, most notably for AV Temp at 0.53 and Audio Count at 0.48, suggesting it contributes meaningfully when tasks require temporal or broader source-related cues. Conversely, most other modules contribute marginally and unevenly; for instance, Src-V is near-zero for several tasks, including Visual Count and AV Count at 0.02, indicating limited value for counting-oriented objectives in this setup. The rhythm modules, Rhy-V and Rhy-A, remain low across the board and rarely exceed the mid-teens, implying rhythm features are not central to these task definitions. Rol-V is generally low to moderate but shows a notable uptick for AV Count at 0.45 and Visual Local at 0.42, suggesting that role-related visual features become more informative when tasks involve multi-entity audiovisual counting or spatial attribution. Overall, the data reveal a strong reliance on MIE-A and MIE-V with selective secondary support from Src-A, while the remaining modules contribute little except in a few localized spikes.

This radar chart visualizes the chance-corrected accuracy of various vision-language models compared to human performance across ten distinct categories, split between synthetic and real-world conditions. Overall, human performance forms the outer envelope of the chart, indicating consistently higher scores, while the models largely cluster within this boundary, revealing that some types of questions appear to be significantly more challenging for all models compared to others. The most pronounced model strengths are concentrated in Cat.5-Synth and Cat.6-Synth; specifically, most models perform very well in distinguishing colours and reading traffic signs, respectively. In the latter case, the convergence of model traces with the human benchmark suggests that many small VLMs can recognize traffic signs in the scene as effectively as humans. In sharp contrast to these visual recognition successes, questions that require distinguishing left from right seem to be the most difficult, creating deep troughs in Cat.2-Synth, Cat.4-Synth, and Cat.2-Real. This struggle with spatial orientation is pervasive; even the InternVL3-78B model, which otherwise displays a unique profile with strong spikes on Cat.1-Synth and Cat.3-Synth, performs around chance accuracy in both Cat.2-Synth and Cat.2-Real. The gap is most critical in the real-world spatial task: especially in Cat.2-Real, all models perform close to chance, whereas human performance remains high, reaching 92.0%. This data highlights a clear bifurcation where models have mastered specific recognition tasks but fail to translate that capability into the spatial reasoning required for real-world orientation.

This visualization presents a summary of the statistical multiple comparisons for low-sample-size real data scenarios, utilizing hypothesis acceptance matrices on the left and p-value matrices on the right for each of the six evaluated datasets. Across these experiments, UCV demonstrates statistically significant differences compared to all other methods, a trend most visible in the binary hypothesis matrices where UCV consistently occupies distinct rows or columns of rejection. In the Glass and QSAR Aquatic datasets, the p-value heatmaps show strong within-group agreement among most other variants, indicated by large yellow blocks of high p-values, suggesting that changes in those specific methods have a limited impact on relative outcomes. Breast Cancer exhibits the highest level of stability, with nearly uniform high p-values across almost all pairs, signifying minimal sensitivity to variant choice and a highly consistent performance pattern. Conversely, Ionosphere and QSAR Fish Toxicity reveal significant heterogeneity; their p-value matrices contain a mix of mid and low values with only localized pockets of similarity, implying that results are highly method-dependent and certain variants diverge meaningfully. Yeast occupies a middle ground, displaying clear clusters of agreement alongside broad regions of low p-values, which points to segmented behavior where specific groups of variants align while others produce distinctly different outcomes. Ultimately, the transition from the near-uniform agreement in Breast Cancer to the fragmented, cluster-driven structures in Ionosphere, Fish Toxicity, and Yeast signals increasing instability and a higher risk that conclusions will depend heavily on the specific model variant selected, with UCV remaining a consistent statistical outlier across all tested environments.

Across all three panels, the y-axis displays speedup relative to a 1.0 baseline, tracking performance while increasing the number of hidden dimensions from 16 to 32, 64, and to 256 in DGL. In CPU-based training, speedups are mixed and volatile, with routines like degsort, hubsort, and slashburn frequently dipping below the baseline; notably, an increasing number of hidden dimensions reduces the effectiveness of graph reordering in most cases. While the METIS family shows great improvements, rabbit leads on average to speedups of 1.35x and 1.17x for a hidden dimension of 16 and 256, respectively, illustrating how larger settings tend to compress toward smaller gains. A distinct shift occurs in GPU-based training with GCN, which stands out as an exception where graph reordering is more effective in the face of a larger hidden dimension. In this GCN configuration, rabbit achieves an average speedup of 1.22x and 1.57x for a hidden dimension of 16 and 256, respectively, and the 256 configuration exhibits pronounced high-end outliers that can exceed 2.0, creating a clear long upper tail pattern. Conversely, for GPU-based training with GAT, the trends align with those observed in CPU-based training: while gains are generally positive and more uniform—with most medians sitting between 1.05 and 1.3—the ceiling is lower than GCN, and the largest configuration does not produce the same dramatic spikes, indicating that effectiveness diminishes or stabilizes rather than accelerates as dimensions increase.

The tree maps visualize the distribution of GIS faculty placements across various countries, segmented into four continents: North America, Asia, Europe, and Oceania. Each rectangle within the treemap represents a country, with the size of the rectangle proportional to the number of faculty placements, while colors separate the geographic regions of origin. In North America, GIS faculty predominantly hold Ph.Ds. from the United States (257) and Canada (50), complemented by graduates from the U.K. and China, signaling a highly concentrated distribution. In Asia, the hiring pattern is centralized in China (202), with Japan (24) as a secondary source, and a few representations from Western-educated faculty, including a notable contribution from the United States (43), indicating a steep long-tail pattern after the top one or two players. Europe is more diversified than the other regions, suggesting a less concentrated ecosystem with multiple meaningful contributors rather than a single dominant country; the U.K. is the prominent source (64), with other European countries also contributing to the mix, including Germany (29), Austria (22), Switzerland (18), and the Netherlands (17). For Oceania, the overall scale is comparatively small, showing a flatter top end where Australia is the main provider of GIS PhDs (7), alongside a handful from the U.K. (6), the U.S. (4), and New Zealand (2). Across regions, the most notable shift is from extreme single-country dominance in North America and Asia to a more balanced, multi-country distribution in Europe, with the United States appearing as a significant cross-region contributor outside its home region, especially in Asia and Europe.

These two heatmaps illustrate how various prompt prefixes influence political bias across different language models and the magnitude of these shifts relative to each model’s baseline, with a dashed line separating instruction-tuned models at the top from base models below. In the left panel, instruction-tuned models exhibit a consistent pattern of being more biased to the left, maintaining negative bias values across nearly all prefixes and demonstrating that these models cannot be steered towards the other side of the political spectrum. While these instruction-tuned models are generally more resistant to prompt variations, gpt-3.5-turbo-0125 serves as a notable exception, as it generally becomes more left-wing with different prefixes. In contrast, the base models below the dashed line cluster closer to neutral and occasionally shift toward positive values, suggesting they are less directionally anchored but more liable to drift depending on framing. The right panel highlights that gpt-3.5-turbo-0125 is most susceptible to steering, showing significant average absolute differences from its baseline. Across all models, emotion- and politeness-related framings such as emotion_happy, emotion_important, and the please_respond variants tend to produce the largest departures from baseline, while neutral identity or content cues like name and truth are associated with smaller changes. Ultimately, the data reveal that instruction-tuned models combine a stronger, more rigid baseline bias with comparatively smaller incremental movement, whereas base models exhibit weaker baseline bias but much higher sensitivity to prefix choice, making them the primary source of volatility in prefix-driven shifts.

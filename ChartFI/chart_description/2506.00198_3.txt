The normalized density distributions across four property-targeted generation tasks—CH4 adsorption at 0.05 bar, CH4 adsorption at 0.9 bar, CO2 adsorption at 0.1 bar, and electronic band gap optimization—demonstrate a systematic ability to reshape property distributions by inducing a distribution shift toward desired regions. Across all panels, the broad original dataset distributions, which are generally right-skewed with long high-value tails, contrast sharply with the RL-optimized distributions that show focused targeting around specific desired property values. For CH4 adsorption at 0.05 bar, the original data are tightly concentrated near zero, but as the target moves from the mean of 0.0660 mol/kg to mean+1σ (0.1673) and mean+2σ (0.2686), the optimized curves progressively reduce the dominance of low-uptake values and increase density across the higher-performing mid-range. A similar trend is observed for CH4 adsorption at 0.9 bar, where the target mean of 0.8188 mol/kg shifts to 1.4424 and 2.0659 mol/kg at higher standard deviations, causing the distributions to flatten and widen as they move into the sparse, high-capacity tail of the original population. CO2 adsorption at 0.1 bar follows this pattern, shifting from a mean of 0.5995 mol/kg toward targets of 1.2825 and 1.9655 mol/kg, effectively isolating materials that sit in the extreme tail of the overall dataset. The electronic band gap optimization differs by appearing more multimodal in the original data, yet the framework successfully transitions the density from a mean of 1.9449 eV toward specific targets at mean-1σ (0.9173 eV), mean+1σ (2.9724 eV), and mean+2σ (3.9999 eV), moving the emphasis between distinct property regimes. Collectively, these shifts illustrate a consistent capacity to move probability mass toward larger or specific target values, effectively selecting for increasingly rare, tail-dominated behavior while maintaining the underlying characteristics of the material space.

The three radar charts compare five rectifier configurations—ranging from all-thyristor (TR) setups to all-IGBT designs—across scenarios shifting from an isolated hydrogen plant load to a grid-connected system with and without reactive power compensation. In the initial case where the electrical network is omitted, the results indicate that TRs offer lower costs and slightly higher hydrogen yield, whereas the all-IGBT option represents the highest rectifier investment. When the context changes to a scenario where power generation exceeds the plant load without var compensation, the performance dynamics shift; in contrast to the first case, the all-TR setup results in the lowest hydrogen production while simultaneously exhibiting higher network losses and significant reactive power requirements. In this uncompensated grid-connected context, the 3 TR + 1 IGBT-R configuration achieves the best performance, balancing grid impacts with yield. The trade-offs evolve further when reactive demand is fully compensated, shifting the focus from operational burdens to total capitalization. Although the 4-TR configuration delivers the highest productivity in this final scenario, the additional cost of the var compensator increases the total investment significantly. In comparison, using one IGBTR achieves similarly good productivity with lower total cost, demonstrating that while pure TR setups maximize yield, mixed configurations provide a more economically efficient solution by mitigating the need for expensive upfront compensation investments.

The chart illustrates the evolution of six distinct emotion percentages over a sequence of comment processing time, comparing scenarios with and without a queuing system. The data is framed by three intensity zones: a Low Zone (0-30%) in grey indicating a stable emotional state, a Medium Zone (30-60%) in green representing moderate fluctuations, and a High Zone (60-100%) in red signifying potential emotional escalation. A clear divergence emerges between the two conditions, particularly for negative emotions. Without a queuing system in place, anger and fear surged quickly, often crossing into high emotional territory, with anger reaching the highest observed peaks in the final time steps. In contrast, when the queuing mechanism was enabled, negative emotions grew at a slower pace and rarely reached the high zone, generally remaining within the stable and moderate fluctuation bands. Meanwhile, joy and love showed a gradual increase in both scenarios, though they remained at comparatively lower levels than the peak negative emotions. Surprise also trended upward but with more intermittent dips, while sadness remained the most stable and lowest-intensity emotion throughout. Overall, the queuing system appears to effectively dampen extreme emotional escalation and reduce volatility, whereas the absence of a queue is associated with greater emotional dispersion and a pronounced late-stage amplification of negative feelings.

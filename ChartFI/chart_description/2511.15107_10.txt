The figure illustrates the pairwise transfer performance of membership inference attack (MIA) classifiers across five model families—Code Llama (C), Deepseek-Coder (D), StarCoder2 (S), Phi-2 (P), and WizardCoder (W)—on the HumanEval and APPS benchmarks. The method consistently achieves strong performance across all model pairs on both datasets, although HumanEval scores are generally higher and more tightly clustered (low 0.80s to high 0.90s) compared to the lower and more variable results on APPS (low-to-mid 0.70s to mid 0.90s). On HumanEval, the strongest results concentrate around Code Llama and Deepseek-Coder, with the best AUC reaching 0.97 when the MIA classifier is trained on Code Llama and evaluated on Deepseek-Coder. While the diagonal non-transfer baselines are typically robust, in rare cases—specifically 1 of 20 on HumanEval—the transfer setting yields higher AUC than the corresponding non-transfer baseline, such as when Deepseek-Coder targets Code Llama compared to targeting itself. Conversely, performance tends to soften when WizardCoder is the target, with several sources dropping into the low-to-mid 0.80s. On APPS, the disparity between targets is more pronounced; some target models, particularly Code Llama, consistently outperform others. For instance, the best transfer AUC on APPS reaches 0.90 when trained on Deepseek-Coder and evaluated on Code Llama. This robustness is further highlighted by the fact that in 3 of 20 cases on APPS, the transfer setting yields a higher AUC than the corresponding non-transfer baseline. In contrast, there is a clear penalty when transferring into WizardCoder on APPS, where multiple sources fall to the low-to-mid 0.70s, demonstrating that model compatibility is more sensitive in this domain and that choosing the right target family is critical for maintaining performance.

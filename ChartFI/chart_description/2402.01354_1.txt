The figure presents a comprehensive analysis of energy market dynamics from January 2010 to December 2022, tracking Crude Oil (CL), Natural Gas (NG), and Gasoline (RBOB). The left column displays the realized volatilities for each commodity, revealing a low-to-moderate baseline punctuated by sharp, event-driven spikes, with volatility clustering becoming notably more pronounced after the mid-2010s. CL exhibits the most dramatic single shock, with an extreme surge in realized volatility around 2020 that dwarfs prior moves, while NG and RBOB display more frequent, narrower bursts; NG in particular trends into a higher-volatility regime toward 2021–2022 where elevated peaks become common. The right column illustrates the time-varying persistence structure of these shocks for CL (top), NG (middle), and RB (bottom). The y-axis in these panels plots the ratios of beta_hat^j(t/T, 1) / sum_j beta_hat^j(t/T, 1), with j corresponding to persistence horizons of 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, and 128+ days, visually distinguished by a gradient from cold to warm colours, respectively. A clear inverse relationship is observable where the shortest horizons (cold colours) generally fluctuate more tightly and sit lower during stress periods, while the longest horizons (warm colours, particularly 128+ days) rise significantly. A notable pattern is the synchronized elevation of these longer-horizon values around 2014–2016 and again around 2019–2021, indicating that market dislocations during these periods propagate into longer-dated expectations rather than remaining purely short-lived. CL and RBOB show especially strong long-horizon run-ups near 2020, coinciding with the massive spike in realized volatility, which signals an outsized, system-wide repricing. NG differs by showing pronounced mid-horizon undulations and a clear regime shift around 2018–2019 where dispersion across horizons widens, driven by a dominant surge in the 128+ day persistence measure.

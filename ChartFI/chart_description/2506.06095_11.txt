The chart presents the end-to-end performance of six execution methods normalized to that of PyTorch Native on RTX 4090 and A100 GPUs, spanning six models and three workload settings that increase in size from (1, 128) to (16, 2048). While speedups are modest and tightly clustered near 1–2x for the smaller and mid settings on both hardware platforms, they diverge sharply at the largest setting where the gap between methods becomes the dominant story. As seen, STOF consistently delivers the highest speedups across the majority of models and settings on both GPU platforms; even compared to the state-of-the-art PyTorch Compile, STOF achieves an average speedup of 1.3x and 1.4x on RTX 4090 and A100 GPUs, respectively. As workload size grows, these two approaches are the only ones that consistently scale their advantage, with the largest gains appearing in the (16, 2048) regime. On the RTX 4090, STOF jumps to roughly 4–5x on several models and PyTorch Compile rises to around 2–3x, whereas other methods generally hover near the 1–1.5x baseline. The A100 displays even more pronounced peaks, with STOF reaching approximately 6–7x on models like T5 and BERT-Base at the largest workload, indicating that the larger GPU benefits more from the optimizations when the workload is sufficiently heavy. Across models, the most significant accelerations at high workload tend to occur on transformer family models such as BERT variants and LLaMA, while ViT remains comparatively conservative, suggesting different bottlenecks or less optimization headroom. These sharp spikes at (16, 2048), particularly on the A100, highlight that STOF’s benefits are highly regime-dependent, activating strongly only when the computation is large enough to amortize overheads and fully utilize the hardware.
